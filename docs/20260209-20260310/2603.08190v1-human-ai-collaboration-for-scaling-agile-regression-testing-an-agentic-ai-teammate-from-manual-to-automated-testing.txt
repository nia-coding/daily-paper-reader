Abstract. Agile organizations increasingly rely on automated regres-sion testing to sustain rapid, high-quality software delivery. However, as systems grow and requirements evolve, a persistent bottleneck arises: test specifications are produced faster than they can be transformed into ex-ecutable scripts, leading to mounting manual effort and delayed releases. In partnership with Hacon (a Siemens company), we present an agentic AI approach that generates system-level test scripts directly from vali-dated specifications, aiming to accelerate automation without sacrificing human oversight. Our solution features a retrieval-augmented, multi-agent architecture integrated into Hacon’s agile workflows. We evalu-ate this system through a mixed-method analysis of industrial artifacts and practitioner feedback. Results show that the AI teammate signif-icantly increases test script throughput and reduces manual authoring effort, while underscoring the ongoing need for clear specifications and human review to ensure quality and maintainability. We conclude with practical lessons for scaling regression automation and fostering effective Human–AI collaboration in agile environments. 

Keywords: Agile Software Engineering, Human-AI Collaboration, Test Automation, Regression Testing, Agentic Systems, Continuous Integra-tion 

# 1 Introduction 

Agile software teams rely on continuous integration and automated regression testing to maintain quality while delivering features at speed [9,4,5]. Yet, in large-scale environments such as Hacon (a Siemens company), the pace of evolving re-quirements and ongoing feature development outstrips the capacity to automate regression tests, resulting in a persistent gap between the volume of manual test

specifications and the rate at which they are converted into executable scripts. This gap not only limits regression coverage and leads to increased manual test-ing effort in sprint releases, challenging the core agile principles of rapid feedback and sustainable delivery. While recent advances in large language models (LLMs) and agentic AI offer promising capabilities for code and test generation [1], there is limited industry guidance on integrating such tools into agile test engineering. Existing research often overlooks the nuanced requirements of regression test suites—such as main-tainability, assertion specificity, and alignment with project conventions—and rarely addresses the human factors of collaboration, review, and governance in real-world teams [7,10,2,6]. This paper addresses these gaps through an in-depth study with Hacon’s agile test engineering team. We focus on three research questions: 

RQ1: How should an agentic AI system be designed to bridge the manual-to-automated testing in regards to the agile practices ? 

RQ2: How Human–AI integration, collaboration and governance should be prac-ticed in the agile environment ? 

RQ3: What are the implications of integrating the agentic AI system into the the agile team in terms of productivity and Human–AI collaboration culture? To answer these questions, we introduce an agentic AI teammate that gener-ates executable system test scripts from validated specifications using retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) and a bounded multi-agent workflow. The system is tightly integrated with Hacon’s continuous integration (CI) pipelines, supports explicit human review and artifact governance, and is evaluated through both artifact analysis and practitioner feedback. Our contributions are: ( C1 ) an industry-verified characterization of the manual-to-automated testing gap in agile regression testing; ( C2 ) a concrete agentic AI architecture for test script generation; ( C3 ) an early industrial evaluation dis-tinguishing productivity gains, output quality, and collaboration practices; and (C4 ) practical implications for sustainable human–AI collaboration in agile test engineering. The rest of the paper is structured as follows: Section 2 presents the industrial context and the manual-to-automated testing bottleneck; Section 3 describes the agentic AI design within the agile environment; Section 4 details integration, collaboration and governance; Section 5 reports the evaluation methods and key insights; and Section 6 concludes with lessons learned, and directions for future work.

# 3 System Design and Architecture 

Within Hacon’s agile test engineering environment, the core challenge is to close the manual-to-automated testing gap while scaling regression automation. Ad-dressing RQ1 requires more than faster script generation: the solution must 

reliably transform validated test specifications into domain-specific test scripts and act as an AI silent teammate—preparing initial drafts asyn-chronously and seamlessly integrating with established workflows. Crucially, it must preserve human accountability, ensuring that engineers retain full control over acceptance and that artifacts remain traceable, maintainable, and aligned with domain conventions. To meet these needs, we designed the Hacon Test Automation Copilot: an agentic AI teammate implemented as a batch-oriented, autonomous collabora-tor that generates first-draft automation artifacts ahead of each sprint. Test specifications are placed in a designated input folder and processed via a bash command, triggering the agentic workflow. For each specification, the Copilot produces a review-ready artifact set—including the generated script, Jenkins execution logs, structured Markdown summaries, and detailed execution traces 4 M. An orchestration layer coordinates a bounded multi-agent workflow and determines whether the generation–execution–evaluation loop should continue, based on a predefined protocol considering iteration limits, CI availability, and evaluator outcomes. Within each cycle, a Generator agent 

produces candidate test scripts using retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) over historical specification–script pairs, which are executed in a dedicated Jenkins testing environment. An Evaluator agent analyzes execution logs as compli-ance evidence using an evaluation matrix covering syntactical correctness, exe-cutability, test-step coverage, semantic correctness, and improvement potential. At the end of execution, a Reporter agent consolidates results into structured Markdown summaries, producing one report tailored for the test manager and another for the test engineer. By operating asynchronously and autonomously, the agentic AI teammate generates automation candidates in advance, increasing throughput with-out disrupting the team ongoing work. The system’s configurability—adjustable iteration limits, pluggable LLMs, and flexible orchestration—lets teams tailor it to evolving workflows. Full artifact traceability ensures accountability, while maintainability is supported by expanding the RAG example base and updating prompts as needed. The architecture is designed for seamless integration with existing processes and future extensibility as organizational needs evolve. 

# 4 Human-AI Integration, Collaboration and Governance 

To address RQ2 , we analyze how the Copilot supports agile test engineering along three dimensions: integration into existing workflows, collaboration be-tween humans and AI, and governance for accountability and quality. Together, these aspects describe how the agentic system can accelerate test automation while preserving human control and aligning with agile practices. An Agentic-AI Teammate from Manual to Automated Testing 5

For integration , the Copilot is positioned as an AI silent teammate em-bedded in the sprint workflow. At sprint start, test specifications—authored and reviewed by testers and managers—are exported from Jira in JSON format, including test data, CI configuration, ordered test steps, and metadata. These validated specifications are handed to the Copilot, which proactively prepares the first draft of automation scripts for the upcoming sprint. This means test engineers receive an initial version of the script at the outset, allowing them to refine and validate rather than start from scratch, thus expanding regression coverage while maintaining a clear separation between AI activity and human preparation. This integration naturally shapes the collaboration model. The AI team-mate autonomously performs the repetitive and time-consuming work of script generation and initial validation, while human test engineers remain respon-sible for interpretation and decision-making. Collaboration occurs both in the input—where humans define and validate the test specifications—and at explicit handoff points: engineers review the generated scripts together with execution evidence, assess semantic alignment with the specifications, and refine or accept the results as needed. By avoiding constant human intervention during genera-tion, the model preserves focus for higher-value engineering tasks and maintains a clear separation of responsibilities. 

Governance emerges from this structured collaboration through transparency and explicit accountability. The AI teammate has bounded autonomy and cannot introduce artifacts into the regression suite without human approval. In-ternal evaluation mechanisms and logging ensure traceability and auditability. Test engineers remain the final decision-makers, reviewing all outputs before in-tegration, so accelerated test creation stays aligned with industry standards and long-term quality goals. 

# 5 Evaluation and Results 

This section addresses RQ3 by evaluating the agentic AI system’s implications on productivity and Human-AI collaboration culture within Hacon’s agile test engineering workflow. The analysis is based on 61 test specifications (Xray for-mat), covering six functional areas with approx. 6 steps per case on average (range: 2–18), 3–8 story point estimation, and pre-rated input clarity (A–D: clear & complete, minor gaps, vague exepctations, unclear or mixed actions). The following subsections outline the evaluation methods and resulting insights.

We employed a mixed-method evaluation combining two complementary strate-gies: 

Survey with Test Engineers: A structured survey was conducted with 5 Hacon test engineers, each independently assigned a subset of AI-generated scripts—one 6 M. El Outmani et al. 

engineer per script. In total, 46 scripts were reviewed and refactored. The sur-vey employed a Likert scale format with open-text fields for qualitative explana-tion. Participants rated each script’s block-level implementation, overall quality, readability, and readiness, and also assessed the clarity, completeness, and ac-tionability of the accompanying markdown test engineer report. The collection of open-text field comments also revealed signals of ongoing human–AI learn-ing: engineers highlighted recurring strengths and shortcomings, adapting review practices accordingly. This combination of structured ratings and free-form com-ments enabled both quantitative measurement and qualitative insight into trust, integration effort, and evolving collaboration practices. 

Semantic Review & Qualitative Observation: We conducted manual semantic reviews of 10 representative scripts from the refactored 46 Scripts, comparing the AI-generated outputs against final human-refactored versions. Instead of line-level diffs, we and an expert test engineer analyzed scripts in terms of semantic blocks to identify meaningful behavioral patterns and correctness issues. 

Key Results and Insights 

Productivity: Manual semantic review and qualitative observations indicated that, 30-50% of the AI-generated code per script was unchanged by the test engineers. Additionally, the survey feedback was more in the direction of clean code and script quality (separating test data, API names, try–catch, etc) which highlight that the AI-generated code was already seen as a junior tester. This confirms that the AI substantially reduced initial authoring time and helped to reduce the manual-to-automated testing gap. 

Human–AI collaboration culture: Analysis of survey responses, version control (git), and semantic reviews revealed that the AI system test specification im-plementation was too literal and misinterpretation even with high-quality (A–B rated) specifications due to implicit domain knowledge and informal conventions that engineers could intuitively interpret. # 6 Discussion, Lessons Learned & Future work 

Discussion. Our evaluation confirms that an agentic AI teammate can mean-ingfully accelerate test automation in agile industrial settings, particularly by bridging the manual–automated testing gap. The AI-generated scripts were per-ceived as comparable to a junior tester’s first draft—functionally usable and frequently retained (30–50% unchanged), though not deployment-ready without review. However, results also reveal key limitations. Despite high-quality (A–B rated) specifications, the AI frequently misinterpreted implicit domain logic and informal conventions, leading to brittle or overly literal implementations. Human engineers routinely corrected these gaps—replacing test step logic with reusable snippets, enforcing internal code standards, and improving structure. These find-ings reaffirm that the agent’s value lies not in automation alone but in its role as a collaborative assistant that offloads initial authoring while relying on hu-man expertise for validation and refinement. Furthermore, survey feedback and version control traces pointed to a growing co-adaptation process. Engineers not only identified AI shortcomings but adapted their own practices—for example, reviewing markdown reports more critically, streamlining rework strategies, and distinguishing where AI input was reliably helpful. This suggests that human–AI collaboration is dynamic, with ongoing learning on both sides a prerequisite for sustainable productivity and quality improvements. 

Lessons Learned. key lessons emerged from deployment and evaluation: 

– Specification Clarity is Foundational: AI effectiveness depends on clear, complete specifications. Human engineers routinely bridge gaps using tacit knowledge, but the AI requires unambiguous, comprehensive instructions. 

– AI Follows, Humans Lead: The AI tends to interpret every instruction literally, sometimes missing opportunities for code simplification or reuse that a human would spot, increasing the need for human review. 

– Human Review Is Indispensable: Engineers consistently caught subtle semantic or stylistic issues; human oversight is essential for quality. 

– Actionable Report Issue: Reports must be not only correct but also clear and actionable to support efficient human review and decision-making. 

– Co-Adaptation Enables Productivity: Sustained gains occur only when teams adapt their practices and treat AI as a capable but limited assistant. Human–AI co-adaptation is a prerequisite for successful integration. 

Future Work. Looking ahead, we see co-adaptation between humans and AI as a fundamental condition for sustained success. Future work will focus on two main directions: (1) enhancing AI adaptation by incorporating expert feedback more directly, such as introducing specialized agents for deeper semantic evalua-tion, and (2) supporting human adaptation by improving specification practices, 8 M. These efforts aim to further close the specification-to-script gap and foster a more effective human–AI collaboration loop. Ultimately, advancing both sides of co-adaptation will drive continued productivity gains and higher-quality au-tomation in industrial settings.
