# A Neuropsychologically Grounded Evaluation of LLM Cognitive Abilities 

Faiz Ghifari Haznitrama 1 Faeyza Rishad Ardi 1 Alice Oh 1

Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) display a unified ”general factor” of capability across 10 bench-marks—a finding confirmed by our factor anal-ysis of 156 models—yet they still struggle with simple, trivial tasks for humans. This is because current benchmarks focus on task completion, failing to probe the foundational cognitive abil-ities that highlight these behaviors. We address this by introducing the NeuroCognition bench-mark, grounded in three adapted neuropsycholog-ical tests: Raven’s Progressive Matrices (abstract relational reasoning), Spatial Working Memory (maintenance and systematic search), and the Wis-consin Card Sorting Test (cognitive flexibility). Our evaluation reveals that while models perform strongly on text, their performance degrades for images and with increased complexity. Further-more, we observe that complex reasoning is not universally beneficial, whereas simple, human-like strategies yield partial gains. We also find that NeuroCognition correlates positively with standard general-capability benchmarks, while still measuring distinct cognitive abilities be-yond them. Overall, NeuroCognition emphasizes where current LLMs align with human-like intel-ligence and where they lack core adaptive cogni-tion, showing the potential to serve as a verifiable, scalable source for improving LLMs. 

1. Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) show increasingly powerful capabilities (Achiam et al., 2023), further enhanced by the emergence of reasoning abilities (Wei et al., 2022). This is demonstrated by their beyond human-level capabilities in tasks such as olympiad-level problems (Trinh et al., 2024). However, even with these impressive capabilities, they still stumble on trivial or straightforward tasks (Srivastava et al.,

Most current LLM benchmarks are task-specific, limiting their analysis and insights to the task performance itself. These benchmarks are not able to uncover the underlying cognitive abilities required to solve the task (Lin et al., 2024; AI, 2024) because task performance is not a reliable mea-sure of intelligence, but rather heavily influenced by prior knowledge and experience (Chollet, 2019). One interest-ing test that is often overlooked is neuropsychological tests, which are a well-established method for measuring specific cognitive abilities in humans, originally designed to iso-late specific cognitive functions using controlled stimuli and interpretable outcomes. Previous works have attempted to apply neuropsychological tests to LLMs (Gong et al., 2024; Hao et al., 2025), but they are limited to a single task or modality, or they lack a deeper analysis of the model’s underlying cognitive abilities. We present the NeuroCognition benchmark, built from three well-established neuropsychological tests: Raven’s Progres-sive Matrices (Raven, 1938b;a; 1941; Carpenter et al., 1990) (RPM) targeting abstract relational reasoning (fluid intelli-gence), Spatial Working Memory (Baddeley & Hitch, 1974) (SWM) targeting maintenance and systematic search across working memory, and the Wisconsin Card Sorting Test (Berg, 1948; Grant & Berg, 1993) (WCST) targeting cog-nitive flexibility and task switching. Each test is adapted into multiple text and image variants (including harder ver-sions) to vary interaction length, modality, and complexity. We also include experiments using assistance inspired by simple strategies that humans use (e.g., taking notes), a pro-cess known as cognitive offloading (Risko & Gilbert, 2016; Larkin et al., 1980), to see if they reduce observed errors and improve overall performance. 2. Related Works 

The neuropsychological tests included in our work measure different aspects of human cognitive abilities. The RPM (Raven, 1938b;a; 1941; Carpenter et al., 1990) measures ab-stract, relational reasoning capabilities (Snow et al., 1984). RPM has been used to identify intellectually gifted individu-als, and has been shown to be widely reliable across different geographical boundaries (Mills et al., 1993). The SWM test (Baddeley & Hitch, 1974), on the other hand, measures the

working memory, the ability to hold and mentally work with information, which may no longer be perceptually present (Diamond, 2013). This test has been used to detect at-risk individuals for psychosis (Wood et al., 2003), and highlight the working memory deficits in individuals with autism (Steele et al., 2007). Finally, WCST (Berg, 1948; Grant & Berg, 1993) measures cognitive flexibility, i.e., the ability to adapt to a change in perspective and reallocate resources to process relevant information (Diamond, 2013; Giller & Beste, 2019). Patients with schizophrenia are shown to per-form worse on this test (Everett et al., 2001), and are shown to be robust even in clinical settings (Kopp et al., 2021). Recent advances in LLMs have led many works seeking to establish stronger correlations between LLMs and human intelligence. Many works focus on developing a cognitive benchmark aiming to measure fluid intelligence (Chollet, 2019; Gendron et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2025; Pandya et al., 2025), emphasizing abstractness and dynamic evaluation to avoid the data contamination issue (Li et al., 2024). Other works applied human cognitive tests to LLMs (Dasgupta et al., 2024) and showcased cognitive behaviors in LLMs, such as similar working memory capacity between humans and GPT-4 in the n-back test (Gong et al., 2024), and above human-level cognitive flexibility in the WCST (Hao et al., 2025). Some works also revealed common factors of mod-els’ performance across different benchmarks (Burnell et al., 2023; Ili ´c & Gignac, 2024), indicating the existence of a common underlying intelligence. quantified specific areas where LLMs struggle. In multi-turn evaluation, LLMs tend to perform worse than single-turn evaluations (Laban et al., 2025), and still perform sub-optimally in multi-turn tasks (Kwan et al., 2024; Sirdesh-mukh et al., 2025). LLMs also struggle in longer multi-step reasoning (Patel et al., 2024) and in complex multi-stage tasks (Lei et al., 2025). LLMs also exhibit cross-modal hal-lucination (Villa et al., 2025), and struggle in multi-image, multi-turn benchmarks (Liu et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2025). LLMs also fail in simple pattern tasks using modified al-phabets (Lewis & Mitchell, 2024), indicating the lack of general analogical reasoning in LLMs. 

3. NeuroCognition Benchmark Tests 

3.1. Raven’s Progressive Matrices Task. RPM presents a 3x3 matrix of construct patterns with the bottom-right cell missing. The participant needs to infer abstract relations across rows and columns, and fill the missing cell by either selecting one of the 8 available options or constructing it. In our benchmark, we use the Raven’s Advanced Progressive Matrices (RAPM) (Carpenter et al., 1990) form. 

Setup. For the image version, we use the RAVEN dataset (Zhang et al., 2019), implementation of RAPM with 7 differ-ent figure configurations licensed with GPL-3.0. We take 20 items per configuration, resulting in a total of 140 test data. We combine the 3x3 question matrix with the 8 available options into a single image input, as shown in Figure 1, and present it to the model in multiple-choice (MC) format. For the text setting, we programmatically generate symbolic ma-trices using character and string attributes (see Appendix A for generation details). We provide two answer formats for text setting: multiple-choice (MC, same 8 options) and gen-erative (Gen, free-form string). A stored rule representation enables automatic validation of Gen outputs (see Appendix B for validation details). We generate 200 test data for the text setting with random configurations. 

Metrics. We report accuracy for both MC and Gen. Average human performance for images is 0.814, reported by Zhang et al. (2019), and not yet established for the text variant. 

3.2. Spatial Working Memory Task. In the SWM test, participants are presented with an image with several boxes. A token is hidden in one of the boxes, and participants are asked to search for it by elimination, opening one box at a time. After the token is found, it is regenerated in another box that has not previously contained it in the current trial. This is repeated until all boxes have contained all tokens, with the goal of locating all tokens with minimal redundant checking and without revisiting boxes unnecessarily. 

Setup. We implement two difficulty levels. Easy : 8 boxes, 1 token active at a time, early stop after all 8 tokens found or a maximum of 64-guess cap. Hard : 12 boxes with 2 distinct tokens active concurrently (24 tokens found required, max-imum 144 guesses), with each token directly reappearing after being found, and one box can contain both tokens at once. We experiment with three types of feedback setups: text-only, image-only (a grid with no additional textual in-formation), and image+text variants. The image input uses a grid system for easier answer verification, as shown in Figure 1. The text-only setup identifies the boxes by number and ask the model to choose a box number. 

Metrics. Since SWM is highly affected by luck, we define some metrics that focus on penalizing errors and unfinished tests. We classify three avoidable error types: • illegal : opens a box that can no longer contain a token because it has already yielded all available tokens for the trial. • no-box : refers to a box index/identifier that does not exist. • repeated : re-opens a box that was previously opened and found empty, before any new token was regener-ated (i.e., no state change that could justify revisiting). 3.3. Wisconsin Card Sorting Test Task. In WCST, four option cards containing shapes are shown to participants. They are then asked to match the given card to one of the four option cards using one of three rules: shape, color, or number. Importantly, participants are not informed of the correct rule for matching the cards and must eliminate potential rules to find the correct one. After choosing the correct answer, a different card is shown while maintaining the same rule. The matching rule is 3A Neuropsychologically Grounded Evaluation of LLM Cognitive Abilities 

considered complete after a fixed number of consecutive correct answers and is changed upon completion without expressly informing the participant. The test finishes after a fixed number of rules are completed or a maximum number of guesses is reached. 

Setup. We require 5 consecutive correct matches ( c =5) to complete a rule instance. We define two difficulty levels. Easy : 3 candidate rules (color, shape, number), each repeated twice (6 total rule instances) or until the 64-guess cap. Hard : adds background color (4 candidate rules, 8 total rule instances) with a maximum of 96 guesses. We also construct controlled ambiguity variants for the hard setting: (i) off (no ambiguous cards), (ii) first (only the first card under a new rule is ambiguous), and (iii) rest (all subsequent cards after the first are ambiguous). Ambiguity occurs when multiple attributes match simultaneously, forcing the model to track disambiguating feedback over turns. The image-input setting provides images of the cards (Figure 1), while the text-only setting lists descriptions of the option cards.

3.4. Experiment Setup 

In our experiments, we benchmark several recent multi-modal reasoning models: proprietary models (Gemini 3 Pro (Google, 2025), Gemini 2.5 Pro (Comanici et al., 2025), GPT-5 (Singh et al., 2025), o4-Mini (OpenAI, 2025), Claude Sonnet 4 (Anthropic, 2024), Grok 4.1 Fast (xAI, 2025a), Grok 4 Fast (xAI, 2025b)) and open-weight models (GLM 4.6V (Z.ai, 2025), GLM 4.5V (Team et al., 2025), Qwen3-VL-235B (Bai et al., 2025a;b; Wang et al., 2024a; Bai et al., 2023)). We use models’ default reasoning capabilities via API pa-rameters and chain-of-thought prompts. However, we dis-able reasoning for Claude Sonnet 4 and Grok 4 Fast on the RAPM test, due to overthinking issues that exhausted output limits and led to worse performance. For RAPM, we set thinking budgets close to the model’s maximum output tokens. For SWM and WCST, we limit the output tokens to 8,192 tokens and the thinking budget to 4,000 tokens per turn. We repeat all SWM and WCST experiments three times, while RAPM is evaluated once. All evaluations are zero-shot with a temperature of 0 (if applicable). Further details are provided in Appendix C.

5.2. RAPM: Pattern Hints 

In RAPM, we test whether providing hints regarding pos-sible underlying patterns helps models narrow down their reasoning and identify the correct answer. The results shown in Table 5 vary across models and setups, and we find no significant effect (paired t-tests: Text MC p = .177 , Text Gen p = .45 , Image MC p = .666 ). Most results are similar to those obtained without using any patterns, and in some cases, they even decrease. Overall, pattern hints have a lim-ited and inconsistent impact. Most changes are small (often positive), and the aggregate effect is not statistically signif-icant, suggesting that many models still lack core abstract reasoning capabilities, even with these hints.

7. Conclusion 

In this work, we introduce the NeuroCognition benchmark, a suite of neuropsychological tests (RAPM, SWM, WCST) adapted for LLMs to probe specific cognitive processes using multimodal, process-aware metrics. Across tasks, state-of-the-art models show meaningful but incomplete alignment with human cognition. They perform better in text, but performance drops for images and harder settings that demand intensive grounding and state tracking. Overall, explicit reasoning is not uniformly beneficial, and human-like assistance shows mixed effects. The impact of pattern hints is limited, while note-taking helps more consistently in WCST but not in SWM. Complementing these results, our factor analysis suggests a dominant general-capability factor across standard benchmarks, highlighting redundancy in current evaluation. Correlations with external benchmarks further suggest that gains in NeuroCognition process scores translate into improvements in semantically related real-world tasks, while individual tests still measure distinct cognitive abilities. In summary, our results suggest that current LLMs do not possess the same kind of intelligence as humans, but still align with humans in some aspects, which can inform how we use and improve them. 

8. Future Work 

Since we apply neuropsychological tests designed for hu-mans to LLMs, it is important for future work to validate whether the same cognitive abilities measured in humans are also captured in LLMs. This helps to further under-stand the relationship between human and LLM intelligence and improve the interpretability of the benchmark results. Another interesting direction is improving the LLM itself through neuropsychologically grounded training data. The data that we used in this benchmark captures basic cogni-tive processes that are foundational to human intelligence, and therefore can be used to improve the cognitive abilities of LLMs. Additionally, recent works investigate ways to compress context (Li et al., 2023; Eyuboglu et al., 2025), or utilize some sort of memory in their architecture (Behrouz et al., 2024; 2025; Dao & Gu, 2024), and our benchmark could serve to evaluate such methods. Finally, this data can be easily generated synthetically with code, making it scal-able to create a large amount of data for training purposes. 8A Neuropsychologically Grounded Evaluation of LLM Cognitive Abilities 

Limitations 

Our evaluation has three main limitations. First, sample sizes are constrained by cost: running state-of-the-art mod-els with effectively unlimited reasoning is expensive. For RAPM, we capped runs at n = 200 (text) and n = 140 (image), which may compromise the robustness of our re-sults, especially in the original image modality. Second, our benchmark assumes that the same neuropsychological test used to assess a specific cognitive ability in humans can also be applied to LLMs to evaluate that same ability. In the neuropsychological field, this is not always the case, since a neuropsychological test is standardized on a specific population and must be validated based on psychometric properties (validity, reliability, and measurement invariance) when applied to a different population. Therefore, even when a model performs consistently as well as humans across all tasks in our benchmark, we can only claim that the model exhibits a similar level of intelligence and can simulate human-like cognitive abilities, but not that it thinks like humans or possesses the same underlying cognitive mechanism. Third, since the neuropsychological tests we used are widely used and well-established, we assume they are valid and reliable based on their psychometric properties (validity, reliability, and measurement invariance). However, this assumption may not hold in all cases, and further vali-dation across prompts, contexts, and a wide range of LLMs is needed. 

Impact Statement 

In our work, we evaluate LLMs under the assumption that their behavior can be interpreted using human cognitive processes. However, we do not claim that LLMs possess consciousness, self-awareness, or the same fundamental cognitive architecture as humans. A.1. Rule Space and Attributes 

The generation engine employs a compositional rule space defined by attributes that constrain string properties. For each matrix, two primary attributes are sampled—one for rows and one for columns. Additional consistency rules (leaks) are inferred if they naturally emerge from the intersection of constraints. The implemented attributes and their parameters are: 1. Character Set Type : Restricts the alphabet of the string. • Values : letters , digits , symbols .• Logic : All characters in the cell must belong to the specified set. 2. Type Count : Constraints on the count of specific character types. • Types : vowels, consonants, uppercase/lowercase variants, etc. • Rules : even , odd , multiple of N (for N ∈ { 2, 3, 4}). • Logic : The count of characters matching the type must satisfy the parity or divisibility rule. Zero counts are explicitly disallowed for these rules to ensure pattern salience. 3. Quantitative Constant : Fixes a metric to a specific integer value across the sequence. • Metrics : String length, unique character count, or count of specific types. • Range : Integers in [2, 5]. 4. Quantitative Progression : Enforces an arithmetic progression on a metric. • Metrics : Same as Quantitative Constant. • Parameters : Start value ∈ [1 , 3] , Step size ∈ [1 , 3] .• Logic : The metric value increases by the step size across the row or column (e.g., lengths 3, 5, 7). 5. Sorted Order : Enforces lexicographical ordering. • Values : ascending , descending , mixed .• Logic : Characters in the string must follow the specified order. mixed explicitly requires the string to be unsorted. 6. Positional Constraint : Fixes the character type at specific indices. • Indices : first , last , even , odd .• Logic : Characters at the specified positions must belong to a specific type (e.g., ”first character must be a digit”). 

A.2. Generation Algorithm 

The matrix generation process follows a constraint-satisfaction approach with backtracking, as detailed in Algorithm 1. The core logic proceeds as follows: 1. Attribute Sampling : Randomly sample one attribute specification for rows and one for columns. 2. Compatibility Check : Validate that the row and column attributes are compatible. For example, a ”Sorted” row attribute conflicts with a ”Positional” column attribute because sorting rearranges characters, potentially violating fixed-position constraints. Infeasible combinations trigger a re-sampling. 3. Constraint Propagation : The row and column attributes are combined to define specific constraints for each of the 

3 × 3 cells. ”Leak” detection is performed to identify additional properties that effectively become constant across rows or columns (e.g., if a row rule restricts characters to digits and a column rule restricts them to even numbers, a universal ”digits” constraint might apply). 4. Grid Construction : Cells are generated sequentially. For each cell, a string is constructed to satisfy the merged constraints. Random backtracking is used if a cell cannot be generated given the current constraints and history. 5. Variation Enforcement : To prevent trivial patterns, we enforce a minimum Hamming distance between strings in the same row or column. This ensures that the progression relies on abstract rules rather than simple repetition. Upon successful generation, the defining constraints for the missing cell (including “leaked” rules) are stored for automatic rule validation. 13 A Neuropsychologically Grounded Evaluation of LLM Cognitive Abilities

A.3. Multiple-Choice and Distractor Generation 

For the text MC setup, we generate 8 options: 1 correct answer (ground truth for the bottom-right cell) and 7 distractors. Each distractor is guaranteed to violate at least one latent rule to ensure a unique solution. • Correct Answer : The generated string for the bottom-right ( 3, 3) cell. • Distractors : Generated by applying mutation strategies to the correct answer to violate latent rules. Strategies include: 

– Break Ordering : Swapping adjacent characters to violate sort constraints. 

– Break Positional : Changing a character at a critical index (e.g., first/last) to a forbidden type. 

– Adjust Count : Adding or removing characters to violate parity or length constraints. 

– Character Mutation : Randomly altering characters to valid but incorrect values. 14 A Neuropsychologically Grounded Evaluation of LLM Cognitive Abilities

C.3. Evaluation Protocol Details 

• Zero-shot: All evaluations are zero-shot. • Temperature: We used a temperature of 0 when possible to reduce sampling variability; when a model’s API does not allow overriding temperature, we used the model’s default. • Reasoning mode: We used each model’s default reasoning configuration except for Claude Sonnet 4 and Grok 4 Fast, for which we adjusted reasoning as described in the main text (see Section 3.4). • Maximum output tokens: For RAPM, we allowed the model’s maximum permitted output tokens. For SWM and WCST, we limited per-turn outputs to 8192 tokens. • Thinking budget: The system prompt described a thinking budget (not enforced as a hard token limit). For RAPM, we used an effective thinking budget of 30,000 tokens for models with a context limit of 32K and 60,000 tokens for models with a context limit of 65K+. For SWM and WCST, the per-turn thinking budget was 4,000 tokens. • Repetitions: SWM and WCST setups were run three times each to assess variability (data are generated on the fly); RAPM was run once per model. • WCST (notes): For WCST runs that used note-taking assistance, we set the note window to 6.

C.4. Reasoning Configuration 

Model reasoning capabilities were enabled through two complementary methods: 

API Configuration. In our ModelWrapper , we programmatically enabled reasoning features where supported by the provider APIs. • Google AI Studio: We accessed Gemini models via the OpenAI-compatible endpoint and enabled reasoning by specifically configuring the thinking config within the extra body parameter. We set include thoughts 

to True , thinking level for Gemini 3 Pro (default to high ), and thinking budget for Gemini 2.5 Pro (default to 8192). • OpenAI API: For OpenAI models, reasoning is handled through reasoning effort parameter in the chat completion calls. We use the default value medium for both GPT-5 and o4-Mini. • OpenRouter: We set the reasoning parameter in the request body to {"exclude": False } to ensure reasoning tokens were generated and returned, and manage the thinking budget by setting max tokens within the reasoning configuration. For Grok, reasoning was explicitly enabled via {"enabled": True }.• vLLM (Local): We enabled the enable thinking parameter in the chat template to activate reasoning generation. 

Prompt-Based Instructions. For all tasks, we included explicit instructions in the prompt to guide the reasoning process, specifically when Chain-of-Thought (CoT) was enabled. Standardized CoT Prompt Addition           

> SWM & WCST:
> Explain your thought process regarding the problem and the feedbacks you received in maximum {think budget }tokens wrapped with
> <think >and </think >. Then, provide a really short summary of your reasoning after the closing </think >tag.
> RAPM:
> Explain your thought process (max {think budget }tokens) inside <think >... </think >then give final answer.

This dual approach ensured that models with native reasoning capabilities (activated via API) and standard models (guided via prompts) both engaged in structured reasoning before outputting a final answer.
