## Abstract 

Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have signifi-cantly advanced embodied AI, and using them to benchmark robotic intelligence has become a pivotal trend. However, ex-isting frameworks remain predominantly confined to single-arm manipulation, failing to capture the spatio-temporal coordination required for bimanual tasks like lifting a heavy pot. To address this, we introduce BiManiBench, a hier-archical benchmark evaluating MLLMs across three tiers: fundamental spatial reasoning, high-level action planning, and low-level end-effector control. Our framework isolates unique bimanual challenges, such as arm reachability and kinematic constraints, thereby distinguishing perceptual hal-lucinations from planning failures. Analysis of over 30 state-of-the-art models reveals that despite high-level reasoning proficiency, MLLMs struggle with dual-arm spatial ground-ing and control, frequently resulting in mutual interference and sequencing errors. These findings suggest the current paradigm lacks a deep understanding of mutual kinematic constraints, highlighting the need for future research to focus on inter-arm collision-avoidance and fine-grained temporal sequencing. 

## 1. Introduction 

Recent advances in Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have propelled Embodied AI. With strong percep-tion and reasoning, these foundation models enable agents to interpret complex instructions and interact with physi-cal environments in sophisticated ways [ 2, 29 ]. Broadly, MLLM applications in Embodied AI span a wide spectrum, including their use as both direct controllers for autonomous agents and, crucially, as robust benchmarks for evaluating robotic performance. Early work primarily employed LLMs for task decomposition [ 41 , 42 ], while the integration of visual modalities has extended MLLMs toward more holis-tic embodied control, spanning object recognition, spatial reasoning, and action generation [81, 101]. Despite this progress, existing evaluation frameworks re-main predominantly confined to single-arm manipulation scenarios. While benchmarks such as ALFWorld [ 80 ] and VLABench [ 107 ] are instrumental, they overlook bimanual capabilities essential for realistic tasks. Bimanual manipu-lation is more than parallel single-arm actions. It demands rigorous spatio-temporal synchronization and dynamic role assignment to prevent self-collision and kinematic singu-larities [ 83 ]. These complexities are further compounded by the visual hallucination inherent in MLLMs that mod-els frequently misjudge spatial reachability or incorrectly assign arm roles, leading to immediate execution failures that single-arm benchmarks cannot detect. Consequently, the absence of a dedicated platform obscures these critical failure modes, stalling the development of versatile agents capable of human-like physical coordination. To address this gap, we introduce BiManiBench, a com-prehensive benchmark for systematically evaluating MLLMs in dual-arm manipulation (Fig. 1). Unlike prior benchmarks that focus on a single abstraction level, BiManiBench adopts a hierarchical evaluation framework with three tiers: (1) Dual-Arm Spatial Reasoning, assessing workspace aware-ness and correct left-right arm assignment; (2) High-Level Action Planning, evaluating long-horizon reasoning under diverse coordination modes, including independent parallel and sequential collaborative manipulation; (3) Low-Level End-Effector Control, testing fine-grained continuous con-trol (16-DoF) and precise bimanual synchronization. To support evaluation, we design a vision-driven agent framework with action chunking, improving efficiency and temporal coherence. To mitigate action lag, we propose a Task-Adaptive Execution Truncation mechanism, balancing open-loop efficiency with closed-loop robustness by allow-ing timely replanning when kinematic constraints or colli-sions arise. (2) We introduce a principled evaluation protocol and a tax-onomy of bimanual coordination modes, together with a vision-driven agent framework featuring adaptive execution. (3) We provide a comprehensive empirical analysis of mod-ern MLLMs, identifying key failure modes and outlining future directions for multi-arm embodied agents. 

## 2. Related Work 

2.1. MLLMs for Embodied Agents 

The integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) into embodied systems has evolved from high-level semantic planning to fine-grained low-level control. Early approaches leveraged LLMs purely as semantic planners [ 41 , 42 , 102 ,105 , 106 ], decomposing abstract instructions into primitives executed by pre-defined skills. With the advent of Multi-modal LLMs (MLLMs), agents have gained the ability to process visual feedback directly. Recent frameworks, such as those evaluated in EmbodiedBench [100 ], demonstrate that while MLLMs excel at high-level semantic tasks ( e.g. , object identification, reasoning), they struggle significantly with low-level continuous control and spatial grounding [ 45 , 101 ]. Unlike prior works that use MLLMs to generate code poli-cies [ 54 ] or value functions [ 43 ], our work evaluates the MLLM’s ability to act as a unified ”brain” that simultane-ously manages high-level coordination and low-level trajec-tory constraints in dual-arm settings. 

2.2. Benchmarks for Robotic Manipulation 

Benchmarking plays a pivotal role in standardizing progress in embodied intelligence. Existing benchmarks can be cate-gorized by their task scope and primary objective: 

High-Level and Single-Arm Benchmarks. Early bench-marks primarily focused on symbolic reasoning. Environ-ments such as VirtualHome [ 76 ] and ALFWorld [ 80 ] evalu-ate an agent’s ability to decompose instructions but abstract away physical actuation. Subsequent work shifted toward physics-grounded manipulation, including VLMbench [ 109 ], VLABench [ 107 ], and the manipulation subset of Embod-iedBench [ 100 ]. While these platforms evaluate perception-to-action pipelines, they are predominantly designed for single-arm manipulation and fail to capture inter-arm con-straints—such as collision avoidance and synchronized hold-ing—which are critical for realistic deployment. 

Bimanual Manipulation Benchmarks. To address dual-arm complexities, specialized environments have emerged. 

TWIN [ 35 ] introduces bimanual tasks with varying de-grees of coupling but focuses primarily on Reinforcement Learning (RL) and Imitation Learning (IL). More recently, 

RoboTwin [19 , 71 ] introduced a scalable data generation framework with extensive domain randomization to facilitate the training of robust visuomotor policies ( e.g. , ACT). It of-fers a rich suite of high-fidelity tasks built on SAPIEN [98]. While we leverage the high-quality task assets and interac-tion APIs provided by RoboTwin as our simulation backend, our objective differs fundamentally. Whereas RoboTwin and TWIN are designed to generate data for training poli-cies from scratch, BiManiBench is tailored to evaluate the zero-shot reasoning and planning capabilities of off-the-shelf MLLMs. 

2.3. Challenges in Dual-Arm Coordination 

Beyond benchmark design, prior studies have analyzed the intrinsic difficulties of dual-arm manipulation. Dual-arm manipulation introduces complexity classes distinct from single-arm settings, necessitating precise spatio-temporal coordination [83]. 

Taxonomy of Coordination: As categorized in TWIN 

[ 35 ], bimanual tasks range from decoupled parallel execu-tion ( e.g. , picking two separate objects) to tightly coupled physical collaboration ( e.g. , handing over an object or col-laborative lifting). 

The MLLM Gap: While traditional control theory addresses these via inverse kinematics and motion plan-ning, MLLM-based agents face unique challenges. Primary among these is the spatial hallucination problem [ 55 ], where models misinterpret the relative positions of left and right end-effectors in egocentric views. Furthermore, MLLMs often struggle to maintain ”role consistency” over long hori-zons [ 93 ], inadvertently switching acting arms during syn-chronized tasks. Our benchmark isolates these failure modes, moving beyond simple success rates to analyze specific deficits in coordination reasoning. 

## 3. Problem Formulation 

We employ two distinct scoring protocols tailored to the nature of the tasks: Success Rate (SR) for Manipulation Tasks: For both High-Level Action Planning and Low-Level End-Effector Control tasks, we report the standard Success Rate. A trial is considered successful (Score = 100) only if the final state satisfies the programmatic verification of goal conditions ( e.g. , object-gripper contact, target region occupancy); otherwise, it is failed (Score = 0). Gaussian-Weighted Spatial Score for Reasoning Tasks: For the Dual-Arm Spatial Reasoning task, a binary accuracy metric is insufficient due to the ambiguity near the workspace center (the decision boundary between left and right arms). To ad-dress this, we introduce a Gaussian-Weighted Spatial Score. ## 4. Benchmark Building 

4.1. Tasks Settings 

To thoroughly assess MLLMs’ performance across diverse dual-arm manipulation tasks, we introduce BiManiBench, a benchmark comprising three distinct evaluation forms. To better evaluate MLLMs’ capabilities in dual-arm manipula-tion, we enhanced specific dual-arm tasks within existing simulators and designed additional scenarios to support com-prehensive assessments. Below is an introduction to the three different task forms. 

Dual-Arm Spatial Reasoning. Dual-Arm Spatial Rea-soning assesses the agent’s fundamental spatial awareness. Given a visual observation of the workspace, the model must determine the optimal manipulator (left or right arm) to execute a grasp. This evaluation is motivated by a critical challenge dis-tinct from single-arm manipulation: the necessity for dy-namic arm assignment. In expansive workspaces ( e.g. , a long tabletop), each manipulator operates under strict kine-matic constraints and limited reachability. Consequently, incorrect arm selection is not merely a suboptimal strategy but a potential failure mode; forcing a grasp with the wrong arm can lead to kinematic singularities, unreachable targets, or physical collisions, thereby degrading execution perfor-mance and compromising hardware safety. Thus, the ability to accurately map spatial locations to the appropriate effector is a prerequisite for robust bimanual control. 

High-Level Action Planning. High-Level Action Plan-ning evaluates the agent’s logical reasoning and decompo-sition capabilities in long-horizon tasks. The model must act as a strategic planner and be responsible for outputting a logical sequence of atomic action primitives ( e.g. , cate-gorizing objects into containers). In this setting, the model functions as a high-level planner, outputting a sequence of atomic action primitives ( e.g. , Pick, Place) and correspond-ing parameters, while the simulator handles the low-level motion execution. 

Low-Level End-Effector Control. Low-Level End-Effector Control presents the highest level of difficulty, de-manding precise motor control without intermediate prim-itives. The agent must directly output continuous 16-dimensional actions at each step—comprising 7-DoF end-effector pose (position and quaternion rotation) and 1-DoF gripper state for both arms—requiring it to manage detailed trajectory generation and bimanual coordination simultane-ously. 

4.2. Dual-arm Task Coordination Taxonomy 

Beyond the fundamental spatial reasoning, we categorize the manipulation tasks into three distinct modes based on the level of inter-arm coordination required. These modes eval-uate the MLLM’s ability to handle workspace constraints, temporal synchronization, and logical dependencies. 

Independent Parallel Manipulation. In this mode, the two arms execute distinct sub-tasks that do not share a target object but share the same workspace. The core challenges lie in collision avoidance between the arms and optimal arm allocation (assigning the correct arm based on workspace reachability) to ensure efficient parallel execution without interference. 

Synchronous Collaborative Manipulation. This mode requires the two arms to actuate simultaneously to manip-ulate a single shared object ( e.g. , dual-arm lifting). While the task logic is relatively straightforward, it demands pre-cise temporal synchronization. This mode is exclusively evaluated in the Low-Level End-Effector Control tier to test fine-grained coordination. 

Sequential Collaborative Manipulation. This mode involves tasks with strict temporal dependencies, where one arm’s action serves as a prerequisite for the other. Examples include handovers or holding a container with one hand while the other places an object inside. The agent must understand the logical sequence of the dual-arm workflow. 

4.3. Vision-Driven Agent Structure Design 

4.3.1. Agent Input 

The inputs to the agent include the image of the current step, which may consist of both a first-person and a third-person view due to possible occlusions during manipulation, the language instruction, the history of environment interactions, and task-specific auxiliary information. Details regarding two types of view are in Appendix E. Details of input prompt are in Appendix F. 

4.3.2. Task Planner 

At each planning step, the agent: (1) generates a textual description of the current visual input; (2) reasons about how to use the available information to achieve the goal based on the image input and the history of interactions; (3) formulates a language-based plan; and (4) converts it into an executable plan in the required format. All outputs are structured in JSON format. Unlike previous methods that restrict agents to single-step planning, our framework supports multi-step action gener-ation (Action Chunking), allowing the MLLM to predict a sequence of consecutive actions in a single inference step. This approach enhances inference efficiency and encourages temporal consistency. However, effective manipulation heavily relies on precise, real-time auxiliary state information provided by the simu-lator. During the execution of a long action sequence, the environment state evolves dynamically after the initial steps, causing a discrepancy between the current world state and the outdated state observation used to plan the later actions in the sequence. This leads to ’action lag’ or state-action misalignment, where subsequent actions become invalid or hazardous. To mitigate this, we implement a Task-Adaptive Execu-tion Truncation mechanism. We define a specific maximum execution limit (max chunk size) for each task. Regardless of the predicted sequence length, the system only executes the first k actions (where k is the task-specific threshold) be-fore halting to re-observe the environment and replan. This strategy balances the efficiency of sequential execution with the safety of closed-loop feedback. Output

## 5. Experiments 

5.1. Experiment Settings 

General Settings. To ensure statistical reliability, we con-duct 100 evaluation episodes for every task in the benchmark. Across these episodes, we implement visual and physical domain randomization, varying background textures, light-ing conditions, and initial object poses to assess the agent’s generalization capabilities. 

Dual-Arm Spatial Reasoning Settings. In the Dual-Arm Spatial Reasoning tier, we use distinct colored cubes in three configurations: (1) Sparse Setting , with three cubes; (2) 

Dense Setting , with five cubes to increase complexity; and (3) Cluttered Setting , which adds random distractor objects 

to test robustness against visual noise. We evaluate 30 models include open-source models and closed-source models in this tier. 

High-Level Action Planning Settings. The High-Level Action Planning tier comprises 14 tasks, designed to eval-uate diverse manipulation capabilities. Aligning with our coordination taxonomy, these tasks are categorized into two primary groups: Independent Parallel Manipulation (where arms operate in the same workspace without direct depen-dency) and Sequential Collaborative Manipulation (where tasks require strict temporal logic between arms). In this setting, the MLLM functions as a strategic planner rather than a motor controller. We define a library of 7 parameterized action primitives ( e.g. , Grasp, Place, Move, Back), where each action requires specific arguments (such as target object ID or coordinate offsets) to be valid. The execution pipeline is API-based: the MLLM outputs the action name and parameters, which are then interpreted and executed by the simulator’s internal motion planner. This abstraction allows us to isolate and evaluate the model’s logical reasoning and task planning capabilities without the interference of low-level control noise. We evaluate 28 models include open-source models and closed-source models in this tier. 

Low-Level End-Effector Control Settings. The Low-Level End-Effector Control tier consists of 5 distinct tasks designed to test the limits of fine-grained bimanual control. The tasks cover full spectrum of our coordination taxonomy, evaluating capabilities ranging from optimal single-arm se-lection to complex Independent Parallel, Sequential Collabo-rative, and Synchronous Collaborative Manipulation. We adopt a continuous end-effector control scheme with a 16-dimensional action space. At each timestep, the agent outputs a concatenated vector for both arms (8 dimensions per arm), defined as [x, y, z, q w, q x, q y , q z , gripper state ].This vector controls the end-effector’s 3D translation, rota-tional orientation (via quaternions), and gripper actuation. Given the difficulty of estimating precise 6-DoF poses solely from visual observations, we decouple perception from con-trol to ensure fair evaluation. The simulator calculates the target object’s ground-truth pose and the optimal grasping quaternion, which are provided to the model as auxiliary textual input. This allows the evaluation to focus on the MLLM’s ability to generate stable control trajectories and coordinate bimanual movements. We evaluate 7 models include open-source models and closed-source models in this tier. 

5.2. Benchmark Results 

5.2.1. Dual-Arm Spatial Reasoning Result 

Table 1 details the performance across different model fam-ilies. Several key observations regarding spatial reasoning and visual robustness can be drawn: 

Performance Disparity and Scaling Trends: Overall, closed-source models maintain a dominant lead, establish-ing a high performance ceiling and demonstrating superior consistency across all test scenarios. Within the open-source landscape, a clear scaling effect is visible, where larger pa-rameter counts generally translate to more accurate spatial judgment. However, an ”efficiency peak” is observed among certain mid-sized models (27b+), which perform competi-tively against significantly larger or even proprietary models. This suggests that for specialized tasks like dual-arm reach-ability analysis, architectural optimizations and the quality of vision-language alignment can be as influential as raw parameter scale.

While high-performing models exhibit resilience to increased ob-ject density, many models—particularly smaller-scale open-source versions—suffer a noticeable performance degrada-tion when distractor objects are introduced. This decline in accuracy under visual noise reveals a common fragility in current MLLMs: the difficulty in maintaining precise object-to-arm allocation when the workspace becomes crowded. These results underscore that achieving reliable bimanual coordination in realistic, unstructured environments remains a significant hurdle for embodied agents.

5.2.3. Low-Level End-Effector Control Result 

Table 2 reports success rates for low-level atomic manipula-tion tasks. Unlike high-level planning, these tasks demand precise visual grounding and fine-grained control. (1) Gap in Precision Control: A sharp dichotomy exists between top-tier closed-source models and open-source counterparts. GPT-5 leads with 66.80, followed by Gemini-2.5-Pro at 60.20%. In contrast, top open-source model InternVL3-78B achieves only 27.60%. This >30% gap suggests that while open-source models have improved in reasoning, they still lack the spatial understanding required for precise actuation. 

(2) Performance Divergence in Proprietary Models: No-tably, Claude-sonnet-4.5 scores 25.40%, on par with open-source Qwen3-VL-235B (25.20%), despite its reasoning reputation. This anomaly highlights that general intelligence does not automatically translate to embodied proficiency. Furthermore, InternVL3-78B slightly outperforms the larger Qwen3-VL-235B, reinforcing that model architecture and resolution are more pivotal than parameter scale for visual-motor tasks. 

5.3. Impact of Multi-View Observation 

We investigate the impact of incorporating a third-person perspective, hypothesizing that it helps mitigate object oc-clusion inherent in the primary ego-centric view. Our results in Table 4 reveal that the efficacy of this addition depends heavily on model capacity. State-of-the-art models ( e.g. ,Gemini-2.5-Pro) successfully leveraged the auxiliary view to resolve visual ambiguity caused by occlusion, significantly boosting the success rate on the complex place9 task from 0.39 to 0.55. Conversely, weaker models ( e.g. , Gemma3-27B) struggled to integrate the multi-view information; for them, the additional input likely acted as visual noise rather than a valid signal, leading to performance degradation. This suggests that while the third-person view offers critical spa-tial details, utilizing it to handle occlusion requires robust multi-view reasoning capabilities. 

5.4. Error Analysis 

We analyzed failure modes for GPT-5 and Gemini-2.5-Pro, excluding environmental noise. As shown in Fig. 3, GPT-5’s bottleneck is primarily perceptual (54%), largely driven by Task State Estimation Misjudgment (39%), though it also shows a notable inability to adhere to prompt-specified exe-cution parameters (Action Parameter Inconsistency, 23%). Conversely, Gemini-2.5-Pro follows prompt constraints more reliably but is significantly more limited by complex planning logic (56%); its main hurdles are Action Sequenc-ing (31%) and Bimanual Conflict (24%), indicating deeper struggles with the temporal and spatial synchronization es-sential for dual-arm coordination. We provide detailed defi-nitions for each error category in Appendix D.

By pinpointing these specific failure modes, we hope our benchmark guides future research toward de-veloping MLLMs with more robust and reliable physical reasoning for complex bimanual actuation. 

Limitations. A primary limitation is that our evaluation is simulation-based, which may not fully capture real-world physical nuances. While covering various coordination modes, task diversity excludes non-rigid or dynamic objects. Additionally, MLLM inference latency hinders real-time bimanual control, remaining a challenge for practical deploy-ment. Future work will expand task complexity and explore efficient architectures to bridge the reasoning-actuation gap. 

## Impact Statement 

This work aims to advance vision-driven embodied AI by evaluating the fundamental capabilities and limitations of MLLMs in complex dual-arm manipulation. By uncovering the core reasoning and coordination gaps in current models, we provide a foundation for developing more versatile bi-manual robotic agents. There are many potential societal consequences of our work, none of which we feel must be specifically highlighted here. In addition, a discussion of possible future directions is provided in Appendix A.

While BiManiBench provides a comprehensive framework for evaluating MLLM-based agents in dual-arm manipulation, it also highlights several critical bottlenecks that remain unresolved. To further advance the field of bimanual embodied AI, we outline several promising research directions. • Bridging the Sim-to-Real Gap. BiManiBench is currently simulation-based, utilizing high-fidelity physical engines such as SAPIEN. However, real-world deployment introduces complexities including sensor noise, varied lighting conditions, and mechanical wear that are difficult to fully simulate. Future research should explore transferring MLLM-derived strategies to physical bimanual robots, leveraging techniques such as domain randomization, residual learning, and sim-to-real adaptation to ensure robust performance in unstructured physical environments. • Handling Deformable and Dynamic Objects. The current benchmark primarily focuses on rigid-body manipulation ( e.g. ,blocks, cups, and cans). In real-world settings, many bimanual tasks—such as folding clothes, tying cables, or handling liquids—involve non-rigid or deformable objects. These tasks require agents to model complex physical dynamics and maintain real-time coordination. Incorporating such objects would challenge MLLMs to move beyond simple geometric reasoning toward a more nuanced understanding of material physics. • Optimizing Multi-View and Heterogeneous Information Fusion. Our empirical results indicate that smaller MLLMs often suffer from information overload when processing multiple visual streams, leading to degraded success rates. A key future direction is the development of more efficient multi-modal architectures or attention mechanisms that can selectively fuse information from ego-centric and third-person views without introducing distracting noise, thereby improving spatial grounding and resolving occlusions. • Enhancing Real-Time Control and Latency Mitigation. A significant hurdle for MLLM-based agents [ 103 – 106 ] is inference latency, which limits the high-frequency closed-loop control ( e.g. , > 50 Hz) required for precision bimanual manipulation. Future work could investigate model distillation, action chunking with temporal ensembles, or hybrid architectures that combine high-level MLLM planning with low-level, high-speed reactive controllers to mitigate action lag and enable smoother synchronization. • Human-Robot Bimanual Collaboration. Beyond fully autonomous operation, a natural extension is evaluating MLLMs in scenarios involving human-robot bimanual collaboration. In such settings, the agent must coordinate with a human partner, requiring not only kinematic reasoning but also social awareness, intent recognition, and safe physical interaction. This direction moves the field toward more versatile robotic assistants capable of human-like collaboration. • Predictive World Modeling via Video Generation. Current MLLM-based agents primarily operate in a reactive manner, selecting actions based on instantaneous observations. However, effective bimanual manipulation often requires foresight into long-horizon dynamics. A promising direction is to incorporate video generation [ 16 , 17 , 40 , 61 –64 , 66 , 67 , 85 , 86 ] as a form of predictive world modeling, where agents synthesize future visual trajectories conditioned on candidate actions. Such imagination-based simulation enables agents to evaluate multiple strategies before execution, improving planning robustness, safety, and sample efficiency. Integrating action-conditioned video generation with MLLMs may thus provide a scalable mechanism for temporal reasoning and decision-making in complex embodied environments. By addressing these directions, the community can bridge the current gap between high-level strategic planning and precise physical actuation, ultimately unlocking the full potential of bimanual embodied agents.

During the execution pipeline, the interface layer intercepts the model’s structured JSON output, specifically the arm tag parameter (“left” or “right”). The system verifies the validity of this assignment against the simulator’s ground-truth kinematic constraints and object reachability. If the model selects an execution arm that is kinematically unable to reach the target or likely to cause a singularity, the system truncates the action before physical execution. Instead of terminating the episode as a failure, the environment provides a closed-loop textual feedback to the model ( e.g. , “Action failed: Action Failed: target green block is too far, right arm can not finish this ‘grasp’ action! Please use another arm!”). This allows the agent to re-observe the state and replan its execution strategy within the same episode. The primary motivation for this mechanism is to decouple Tier 1 (Spatial Reasoning) from Tier 2 (High-Level Planning). By providing an automated “guardrail” for arm allocation, we can more accurately assess whether an MLLM understands the logical dependencies of complex tasks—such as the sequential requirements in Handover block or Place bread skillet—rather than penalizing its planning performance solely due to a one-time spatial miscalculation. It is important to note that this truncation mechanism is not exhaustive. It primarily targets clear kinematic violations and reachability issues. More nuanced errors—such as “model illusions” during complex bimanual coordination or subtle workspace overlaps—may still bypass the filter. Consequently, a small percentage of End-Effector Allocation Errors remains present in our final Error Analysis, providing a realistic reflection of the coupling between spatial awareness and high-level planning in dual-arm scenarios.

## D. Error Analysis 

We first divide the errors we encounter into two main categories: environmental errors and model output errors. 

D.1. Environmental Errors 

One type of environmental error occurs when the robotic arm’s actual behavior fails to meet the documentation’s expectations when the model calls relevant APIs through the simulation environment interface. This problem is not caused by the decision-making layer model, but rather by a flaw in the internal function settings of the simulation environment at the execution layer. Since different tasks involve different calls to the simulation environment interface, the probability of this type of error varies across tasks. However, within the same task, the occurrence of this type of error is independent of the specific model used by the decision-making layer, occurring with approximately the same probability for all models. An example is grasp problem. In the simulation environment, all objects are generated with one face always pointing vertically upwards. If an object is hit by a collision, this face may turn in another direction. For a geometrically symmetrical cube, this should not affect its grasping ability. However, because the simulation environment implicitly depends on the object’s orientation when calling the grasping function, the grasping action cannot be completed when the object’s orientation changes. Another type is the scene randomization problem in certain episodes. When randomizing the scene using a random seed, some interfering objects are generated in inappropriate locations, severely interfering with normal operation. Since all models use the same random seed and scene for each episode during testing, all models will encounter the scene randomization problem and will be unable to complete the task in some task episodes. 

D.2. Model Output Errors 

We categorize model output errors into three types: Perceptual Reasoning Error, Strategic Planning Error, and Format Error. Since Format Error only occurs in models with a small number of parameters(usually less than 10B), our analysis focuses on the following two core categories: 

1) Perceptual Reasoning Error. This category focuses on inference errors following visual observation. It reflects the model’s failure to correctly understand the ”current state.” It is further divided into: • State Estimation Misjudgment. This error primarily occurs when the model makes inferences based on current observations, encountering problems in inferring the state and progress of the current task. An example is when the model, after having already placed a block in the target location, continues to repeatedly grab and place that block without addressing other unfinished tasks. • End-Effector Allocation Error. This error primarily arises when the model, based on current observations, encounters problems in determining whether to use the left or right actuator in the current state. This issue stems from insufficient spatial capabilities in a dual-arm environment, or significant model illusion during the end-effector allocation process in dual-arm scenarios. This capability was tested within the first part of our Dual-Arm Spatial Reasoning section. Therefore, to better evaluate the model’s capabilities in other areas, we automatically truncated most incorrect End-Effector Allocation cases in the interface layer between the model output and the simulation environment, providing feedback to the model that the current end-effector cannot complete the action until the model adopts the correct end-effector. However, this automatic truncation cannot capture all incorrect End-Effector Allocation scenarios; therefore, some End-Effector Allocation Errors will still occur, affecting dual-arm operation tasks under High-Level Action Planning. • Physical Attribute Misreasoning. This error mainly occurs when the model makes inferences based on current observations, and it has a problem in inferring the size of objects. It mainly appears in two tasks that require determining the size of blocks. 

2) Strategic Planning Error. This category involves errors in the subsequent action sequence planning, even if the environment is correctly perceived. It includes: • Action Sequencing Error. This error primarily stems from the model’s failure to output the correct action sequence when planning actions, based on the specific task requirements or the task flow hinted at in the assistant info. A typical example is in dual-arm manipulation tasks: after grasping an object, the end effector must be raised before placing it, and after placement, the end effector must be raised again before the robotic arm returns to its original position; otherwise, the end effector’s movement may collide with other objects on the table. This error also frequently occurs in object-transfer tasks, where the order of actions is crucial. If the model fails to output the correct action sequence, the transfer will fail. • Bimanual Conflict/Collision. This error primarily stems from the model’s failure to consider the conflict between the two robotic arms during action planning. This leads to collisions or disruptions in action execution due to these conflicts. This error is often accompanied by a State Estimation Misjudgment Error because the model’s previous output may not have properly positioned the robotic arms. If problems arise in inferring the current task’s state and progress, and the robotic arms are not properly positioned beforehand, subsequent action execution is prone to Bimanual Conflict/Collision. • Action Parameter Inconsistency. This error occurs because the model failed to output task-specific action parameters when formatting the action output in JSON format. In a task, the parameters that should be set for different scenarios and types of actions are usually indicated in the prompt; however, some models still occasionally produce incorrect action parameter outputs. 

## E. Visual Observation Setup 

In our experiments, the agent receives visual inputs from different perspectives depending on the task configuration. In High-Level Action Planning, we provide images from both perspectives to the MLLM planner. In Low-Level End-Effector Control, we primarily utilize only the ego-centric view , while the third-person view is investigated in our ablation study. Fig 8 illustrates the comparison between these two observation modes. • Ego-centric View (Primary): Mounted on the robot’s end-effector (or head), providing detailed visual feedback for precise manipulation but suffering from occlusion when the gripper is close to the object. • Third-person View: A fixed external camera that provides a global overview of the workspace. This view is crucial for resolving spatial ambiguities ( e.g. , occlusion) and is used as a default input for High-Level Action Planning. However, it introduces two challenges for visual reasoning: 1. Reduced Object Resolution: Due to the greater camera distance, manipulable objects occupy fewer pixels compared to the ego-centric view. 2. Spatial Inversion: The perspective creates a counter-intuitive visual reversal where the robot’s physical right arm 

appears on the left side of the image, and the left arm on the right .For models with limited capabilities, these factors can act as visual interference rather than helpful context, potentially leading to the performance degradation observed in our Ablation Study.
