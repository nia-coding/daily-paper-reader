Learning causal structures typically represented by directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) from observational data is notoriously challenging due to the combinatorial explosion of possible graphs and inherent ambiguities in observations. This paper argues that causal learning is now ready for the emergence of a new paradigm supported by rapidly advancing technologies, fulfilling the long-standing vision of leveraging human causal knowledge. This paradigm integrates scalable crowdsourcing platforms for data collection, interactive knowledge elicitation for expert opinion modeling, robust aggregation techniques for expert reconciliation, and large language model (LLM)-based simulation for augmenting AI-driven information acquisition. In this paper, we focus on DAG learning for causal discovery and frame the problem as a distributed decision-making task, recognizing that each participant (human expert or LLM agent) possesses fragmented and imperfect knowledge about different subsets of the variables of interest in the causal graph. By proposing a systematic framework to synthesize these insights, we aim to enable the recovery of a global causal structure unachievable by any individual agent alone. We advocate for a new research frontier and outline a comprehensive framework for new research thrusts that range from eliciting, modeling, aggregating, and optimizing human causal knowledge contributions. 

Key words : causal learning, wisdom of the crowd, human knowledge, scalability, directed acyclic graphs 

## 1. Introduction 

The pursuit of understanding causal relationships (Stein 2023, Pearl 2009) represents the cornerstone of scientific progress, bridging the gap between statistical observation and actionable, mechanistic knowledge. In the modern era, this philosophical endeavor has transformed into a rigorous computational challenge (Sloman 2005), thanks to the emergence of probabilistic and statistical languages for causality, the advancement of computational powers, and the rise of AI and machine learning (AI/ML). Now, causal discovery, the task of inferring the underlying causal structure for a set of variables under explicit model assumptions, has become a fundamental problem in science and engineering (Pearl & Mackenzie 2018, Huber 2024). Various formulations of this problem have been proposed, among which learning a DAG that represents the data-generating process is now a canonical approach.

However, DAG learning has long faced a critical bottleneck of computational intractability. As the number of variables increases, the search space for possible causal structures grows super-exponentially with the number of variables, making optimal structure learning NP-hard (Bóna 2015). For a large causal graph with more than a handful of variables, heuristic algorithms must be used to avoid search intractability (Binkyt ˙eet al. 2023), which roughly fall into three main categories, namely score-based (e.g. A* search or GES optimizing BIC/BDe scores) (Yuan et al. 2011, Ng et al. 2024), constraint-based (e.g. PC or FCI) (Spirtes et al. 2000, Li et al. 2019), or differentiable structure learning-based algorithms (Huang et al. 2012, Zheng et al. 2018, Ban et al. 2024). Nevertheless, they may still get stuck in suboptimal solutions, and also fail to overcome the inherent statistical identifiability issues of DAG learning. These causal discovery algorithms, with observational data alone, fundamentally recover only a Markov equivalence class (MEC) of graphs that encode identical conditional independencies but differ in causal orientations, rather than the unique underlying DAG (Glymour et al. 2019). Moreover, with limited or noisy data, multiple graphs may fit the data equally well, which exacerbates the identification challenge (Carey et al. 2025). In practice, these limitations often cause significant deviations from the true causal structures (Kitson & Constantinou 2025), especially in large-scale systems with many interacting variables remain unobserved or inadequately represented. Without additional assumptions or auxiliary information, such as interventions, structural constraints, or prior knowledge, it is impossible to achieve full identification. Researchers have long recognized that incorporating prior knowledge can help guide causal learning (Cano et al. 2011, Gururaghavendran & Murray 2025, Heckerman et al. 1995). Domain experts might have knowledge of causal relationships (e.g. “smoking causes lung cancer”) or discern implausible connections, thus reducing the search space (Constantinou et al. 2023). Traditional approaches to include knowledge involve specifying priors on DAG structures or adding constraints (e.g., forbidding or enforcing certain edges) (Borboudakis & Tsamardinos 2012, Hasan & Gani 2022, Gonzales et al. 2022). However, prior works primarily supplement the algorithms that learn the DAGs from observational data, which fail to scale well to large, complex domains where data is sparse and no individual expert possesses complete knowledge. 

This gap motivates us to consider a radically new paradigm: What if we could systematically harness the knowledge of hundreds or thousands of experts, each contributing a piece of the puzzle, to jointly construct a large causal graph? 

A new paradigm casts causal structure learning as a distributed decision-making problem across a crowd of experts (humans or AI agents), each holding partial and noisy beliefs about relationships among variables Lin et al.: Causal Learning Should Embrace the Wisdom of the Crowd 3

of interest. Through principled elicitation and aggregation of these distributed knowledge fragments, we can assemble a coherent global causal graph beyond the capabilities of any single expert, analogous to crowdsourcing the discovery process (Vaughan 2018, Thompson & Bentzien 2020), and how Wikipedia aggregates knowledge from thousands of contributors. This concept leverages the “wisdom of the crowd” principle (Surowiecki 2005), where collective judgments of a diverse, independent group can surpass individual experts as idiosyncratic errors cancel out through aggregation (Saha Roy et al. 2021). In the context of causal learning, this suggests that while any single informant may be unreliable on some links, an ensemble of many informants with proper aggregation could yield a highly accurate global network. The key difference lies in aggregating causal knowledge (relationships, mechanisms, and directional influences) rather than factual articles or voting results or individual opinions. This problem poses distinct intellectual challenges compared to existing work in collective intelligence (Park et al. 2025, Riedl et al. 2021), social choice theory (Suksompong 2024, Kelly 2013), truth discovery (Shabat et al. 2022, Li et al. 2016), crowdsourced labeling (Yin et al. 2021, Sheng & Zhang 2019), knowledge fusion (Zhao et al. 2020), and human-in-the-loop machine learning (Mosqueira-Rey et al. 2023, Wu et al. 2022b), as causal knowledge involves latent structures, directionality, and inter-dependencies that go beyond aggregation of surface-level judgments. Therefore, we argue for a new paradigm toward “causal learning by wisdom of the crowd” that is decentralized, scalable, sample-efficient, and easy to integrate with existing paradigms . We envision a 

decentralized intelligence solicitation platform that treats a knowledgeable population as a distributed network of “puzzle-solvers,” each possessing partial and potentially noisy insights into local causal relationships. By framing causal learning as an analytic core for large-scale distributed decision-making, we can leverage the collective intelligence of domain/non-domain experts, and even simulated agents from models such as Large Language Models (LLMs), to construct complex causal graphs that surpass the reach of centralized algorithms. Realizing this vision requires new formulations for expert modeling, denoising, and aggregation, opening a research frontier uniting collective intelligence with structural causal modeling. 

## 2. Background 

History of Causal Knowledge Elicitation. As summarized in Table 1, the incorporation of human expertise into causal models is not a new endeavor, but has historically been marked by heuristic, unscalable practices (O’Hagan 2019). In early research on Bayesian Networks (BNs), a major causal modeling framework, the construction of the DAG structure was largely a manual process (Heckerman et al. 1992, Jackson 1990). Experts would work with knowledge engineers to define variables of interest, identify their states, and draw directed edges based on professional experience. A well-known example is the medical belief network ALARM 

(Beinlich et al. 1989). With advances in statistics and ML, the focus shifted toward data-driven structure learning. Algorithms were developed to find the structure that maximized the marginal likelihood of data or satisfied conditional independence constraints (Ramírez 2001, Neapolitan et al. 2004). However, purely 4 Lin et al.: Causal Learning Should Embrace the Wisdom of the Crowd 

data-driven methods cannot distinguish between structures in the same MEC using observational data without additional information or constraints. To resolve such ambiguities, researchers turned to intervention-based strategies that often require strict experimental conditions and employ costly operations (Eberhardt 2008, Li et al. 2023, Choo et al. 2024). They can also integrate expert knowledge as “side information” for disentanglement (Amirkhani et al. 2016, Gonzales et al. 2022). Yet, hybrid approaches treat expert knowledge in a largely centralized manner through consulting single experts or small specialist groups whose models inherit individual biases and lack collective intelligence. Moreover, these methods often overlook the fact that experts might only have local knowledge of specific sub-domains. The absence of systematic framework for decentralized intelligence solicitation and consolidation has hindered scaling causal discovery to meet modern big data demands. 

Technological Enablers. While the idea of using human causal knowledge is longstanding, only recently have technological advances matured to the point where a decentralized, data-driven implementation of this vision is feasible at scale. We highlight four technological enablers that make crowd-powered causal learning timely and practical: (1) Crowdsourcing platforms like Amazon Mechanical Turk (Paolacci et al. 2010) and Zooniverse (Simpson et al. 2014) now provide scalable access to millions of diverse contributors through structured knowledge elicitation workflows to achieve expert-level accuracy through aggregation. (2) Aggregation models and tools have greatly advanced in the past decades, such as Dawid-Skene (Tamura et al. 2024), GLAD (Whitehill et al. 2009), and HLM (Wu et al. 2022a). They contribute many ways to assess individual knowledge quality and bias, and yield consensus-level judgments critical for building reliable causal graphs from distributed sources. (3) LLMs like the GPT family (Leon 2025, Achiam et al. 2023) offer scalable knowledge amplification by simulating experts, assisting elicitation, and generating structured causal hypotheses to complement human input. Early studies show that LLM-assisted interviews and causal prompting can efficiently bootstrap knowledge acquisition with human oversight (Alaa et al. 2024). (4) Human-AI collaboration tools (e.g., in the human-computer interaction community) enables non-technical users to contribute causal insights via interactive interfaces (Zhong et al. 2024, Kale et al. 2021). Advances in knowledge engineering and hybrid intelligence systems allow humans to input, refine, and validate structured knowledge (e.g., causal pathways, diagrams, or piece-wise insights, etc.), lowering the barrier to entry for domain experts and laypersons alike (Kiciman et al. 2022).

3.3. Insights of the Real-World Study 3.3.1. Characterizations of Knowledge Quality. Experimental evidence reveals that the reliability of expert knowledge can vary widely. An expert might possess highly accurate knowledge about certain causal links (edges), while remaining uncertain or completely ignorant about others. This heterogeneity demands a conceptual framework to categorize experts by the scope and accuracy of their knowledge. To this end, we recommend a taxonomy grounded in four key dimensions: 

• Completeness reflects the breadth of an expert’s knowledge, which determines whether it is feasible to obtain fully informative knowledge from the expert. When knowledge is incomplete, they may only cover a subset of causal relationships, potentially leading to partial, myopic, or even inconsistent inputs. 

• Belief validity captures how well an expert’s beliefs align with the underlying ground truth, which can be measured by the performance evaluation metrics such as specificity, accuracy, etc. Within an expert’s areas of specialization, they tend to offer more accurate and interpretable causal assessments, whereas their reliability decline significantly with their beliefs becoming biased or erroneous outside these areas. 

• Confidence level is not necessarily associated with the validity of the beliefs of experts, but shapes how cautious or assertive their behaviors are. Beyond correctness, experts differ in their confidence about what they know. For example, some may express appropriate uncertainty when unsure, while others may be overconfident. 

• Trustworthiness captures the presence of bad or adversarial behaviors, as not all experts make good-faith efforts to contribute knowledge to ensure reliability. Some experts behave strategically, maliciously, or inattentively, thereby introducing significant noise or deliberate misinformation. Thus, recognizing such uncooperative individuals is essential to ensure the reliability of expert knowledge. Lin et al.: Causal Learning Should Embrace the Wisdom of the Crowd 7

3.3.2. Taxonomy of Expert Types. Based on the dimensions discussed in the previous section, we introduce several conceptual categories that characterize expert behavior: (a) Omniscient expert refers to an expert who knows the true causal structure in its entirety with full accuracy. Their knowledge is both complete and perfectly correct as the ground truth. They typically exist only in idealized settings and serve as conceptual reference, theoretical upper bounds or benchmarks in simulation and evaluation. (b) Perfect-but-Incomplete expert is a weaker type of expert who always provides valid beliefs without asserting a wrong causal link but may not cover all the true links due to gaps in knowledge. Namely, their knowledge is reliable but not comprehensive. A well-trained domain expert who avoids speculation and limits their responses to familiar areas is a typical example of this category. (c) Imperfect experts possess substantial knowledge of the domain but are prone to occasional mistakes (e.g., reversing causal directions or introducing spurious links). Their knowledge is partially correct, typically reflecting a mix of true and false causal beliefs, which is likely the most common in practice. (d) Uncertain experts are distinguished by their lack of confidence in making judgments on causal relationships. It stands in stark contrast to overconfident experts, who consistently assert strong causal judgments and potentially amplify erroneous links due to unwarranted certainty. Unlike Perfect-but-Incomplete experts, they do not refrain from sharing information when uncertain and continue to contribute regardless of reliability. (e) Bad Actors may intentionally inject misleading, adversarial, or strategically manipulated information to distort reasoning or exploit the system, which ultimately undermines the integrity of causal learning. 4.2. Comparative Analysis: Edge vs. Ordering 

Edge-wise causal knowledge captures the most fine-grained local structure, while ordering-wise causal knowledge emphasizes more on the global structure of 𝐺 . For an omniscient or perfect-but-incomplete 

expert, edge-wise elicitation directly reveals a portion of the ground truth and theoretically guarantees the full recovery through exhaustive queries for omniscient experts. However, the explicitness limits the inferential flexibility as edges provide only specified information, whereas ordering-wise knowledge enables broader structural inference. Thus, such granularity renders the elicitation of edge-wise knowledge inherently demanding, whereas ordering-wise knowledge is often more query-efficient for the inference of 𝐺 . With imperfect experts, edge-wise framework struggles with incomplete or biased knowledge or even introducing acyclicity violation due to their incompleteness, uncertainty, and thus becomes a risky choice. However, more robustness can be gained from ordering-wise knowledge in this case as it accommodates graded confidence through its scoring mechanism, enabling systematic detection of belief patterns and providing more diagnostic information about inconsistency or uncertainty. These frameworks present a fundamental trade-off between knowledge efficiency and structural specificity. More specifically, regarding knowledge completeness, under an edge-wise causal knowledge model, increasing the number of queries naturally yields more pairwise edge information, thereby reducing missingness due to the incomplete coverage. In contrast, ordering-wise approaches elicit path or precedence information rather than direct edge, allowing sparser local coverage while still contributing meaningful structure constraints. Consequently, additional queries primarily enhance global consistency rather than local edge recovery. As belief validity describes the level of alignment of the beliefs and ground truth structure, it also manifests differently across these two paradigms. The more pairs queried, the more edge information can be achieved for edge-wise methods; however, this does not necessarily lead to better graph structure inference, depending on the alignment level and potentially leading to repeated local errors without sufficient global pattern for detection. On the other hand, ordering-wise approaches enable aggregation of relational patterns across queries, which may facilitate identification of systematic misalignment through behavioral or consistency analysis. When it comes to the confidence level, the edge-wise elicitation with discrete scores (e.g., 0 or ±1) naturally do not encode confidence, requiring either a modified scoring system or repeated queries which brings up new challenges such as the familiarity effects and diverse behavior-related score distributions. Ordering-wise elicitation typically has built-in score system to incorporate graded responses, providing an intrinsic notion of confidence that can be utilized as soft structural constraints and may reveal the geometry of participants’ beliefs. Finally, with respect to trustworthiness, incorrect edge-wise responses will directly contaminate the learned structure, although increasing query volume can support post hoc behavior analysis. The ordering-wise formulations, by emphasizing the relational consistency, may offer a stronger tool for detecting global contradictions and filtering unreliable responses, therefore, improving robustness as query coverage expands and provide some insights in aggregating multiple respondents’ answers. 

4.3. Extension of Elicitation Frameworks 

Beyond pairwise elicitation, these frameworks can be generalized to richer forms of causal knowledge. Graph-wise causal knowledge elevates the query unit from a single edge to an entire graph or subgraphs, allowing experts to provide candidate structure or impose coherent structural constraints. An alternative is list-wise causal knowledge, which captures the expert’s beliefs in causal flow by a ranked list of variables reflecting the relative causal positions, exemplified by triplets or quadruplets (Vashishtha et al. 2025). These higher-order elicitation schemes compose fundamental ordering-based or edge-based building blocks, yet they grant experts different degrees of freedom in articulating causal beliefs. Thus, they encode different amounts of information and entail varying levels of elicitation difficulty. ## 5. Leveraging the Wisdom of the Crowd 

Now we extend from a single expert to a crowd and highlight the importance of leveraging collective intelligence as a powerful driver for causal learning with potential approaches to enable this at scale. 

5.1. Harnessing the Crowd Knowledge 

Quantitative expert-driven paradigms are promising to handle the complexities in causal discovery and inference. However, reliance on a single expert is inherently fragile, as individual judgments are inevitably partial, biased, and susceptible to mistakes. The true potential of expert knowledge emerges in its collective form where diversity across a crowd of experts yields both mutual reinforcement through agreement and complementary perspectives through disagreement. This combination enables the crowd knowledge to be more robust to errors and encompass a spectrum of insights. Therefore, a shift from expert-in-the-loop to crowd-in-the-loop causal learning is warranted, moving beyond the authority of one toward the collective wisdom of many. 5.1.3. Discussion. These two strategies entail distinct trade-offs in computational efficiency, modeling flexibility, and information utilization. Expert-level aggregation is a two-stage process which gains computa-tional advantage through modularity, yet inherits all challenges of individual-level causal graph inference. Additionally, collapsing diverse structures into a single graph may obscure valuable information. While expert characteristics can be incorporated through weighted average or distributional assumptions, specifying appropriate forms for optimally combining graphs of varying quality remains challenging. Query-level aggregation bypasses individual-level inference by directly modeling responses toward the global 𝐺 , pre-serving richer information and naturally accommodates cognitive mechanisms by explicitly decomposing query difficulty with higher computational cost due to a larger parameter space. Both frameworks benefit from higher proportions of knowledgeable and reliable experts, but may handle heterogeneity distinctly: expert-level aggregation struggles with contradictory individual graphs, while the query-level better leverages fragmented knowledge through fine-grained response modeling.

5.3. Agent-based Expert Simulation. 

To effectively leverage the wisdom of the crowd, we must confront practical issues in scaling to a large cohort, such as labor cost, individual fatigue, and the risk of bad actors that necessitate additional screening and quality control. These constraints motivate the introduction of surrogate agents that can emulate human expertise and play an expert-like interactive role while remaining controllable, and cost-effective. Recent advances in LLMs offer a timely opportunity for such agent-based models (ABM), as they encode rich commonsense knowledge and demonstrate increasingly strong conversational and reasoning capabilities. LLM-based agents may act as simulators to understand the causal world in an expert’s mind and emulate their reasoning patterns, thereby enabling the generation of plausible causal structures with minimal querying (Shaposhnyk et al. 2025, Long et al. 2023, Kiciman et al. 2023). And it is possible to extend beyond language to characterize the causal world with foundational models (Jin et al. 2024, Audenaert et al. 2025), world Lin et al.: Causal Learning Should Embrace the Wisdom of the Crowd 15 

models (Richens & Everitt 2024, Ding et al. 2025), and multi-agent systems (Li et al. 2024, Zhang et al. 2025), and scale up the simulations to build a digital population (Lin et al. 2025, Berenberg & Bagrow 2018). Putting these efforts together, it is appealing to envision a hybrid expert crowd composed of both human and simulated agents that contribute reliable knowledge for causal learning.

## 7. Alternative Views 

One alternative view is that expert knowledge should serve only as a supplement to observational or interventional data rather than a core driver of causal discovery . We do not claim expert knowledge can replace such data, nor that it is inherently superior. Rather, we argue that expert knowledge is an increasingly important information source that complements existing data. Observational and interventional data alone often face limitations, such as identifiability, due to cost, computational, or ethical constraints (Eberhardt 2017). In contrast, well-elicited expert knowledge can inject domain-specific inductive biases that are unobservable from data alone (Hasan & Gani 2026, Zhou et al. 2013), shrink the hypothesis space, and provide interpretable guidance for causal learning. Structured knowledge can be directly used for causal discovery in the absence of observational data. Moreover, aggregating diverse perspectives, when coupled with scalable elicitation and quality-aware AI integration, can further enhance robustness under data scarcity. A related concern is that causal learning should rely exclusively on carefully vetted experts rather than broader crowds . While knowledge quality is critical, restricting elicitation to a small set of high-bar experts may limit diversity and scalability (Yoo et al. 2024, Hasan et al. 2024). Instead, we advocate for inclusive elicitation with quality control, denoising, and model-based integration. By shifting reliability from expert selection to post-hoc processing, causal learning can effectively leverage diverse expertise at scale. We acknowledge practical challenges in deployment, such as expert recruitment, incentives, and adversarial behavior. However, these challenges are well studied in areas such as crowdsourcing and human–computer interaction, and can be addressed through interdisciplinary solutions. 

## 8. Call to Action 

We call on the community to advance this vision by developing new methodologies, benchmarks, tools, and theoretical foundations. Table 5 in Appendix D highlights several concrete avenues for future exploration. These include modeling the nuanced knowledge profiles and uncertainty patterns of human contributors; optimizing elicitation strategies to make crowd input more reliable, cost-effective, and scalable; and designing hybrid workflows where human and AI systems, especially LLMs, collaborate seamlessly. Beyond technical methods, this agenda raises foundational theoretical questions about the limits and guarantees of distributed causal inference, along with pressing system-level challenges in evaluation, ethics, and inclusivity. Fully realizing the impact of the proposed paradigm will require deep interdisciplinary collaboration that draws on insights from causal inference, human-computer interaction, crowdsourcing, AI ethics, and cognitive science to build robust, adaptive, and human-centered causal learning systems.

Assumption 1 (Valid instrumental variables (IVs)) . Valid IVs must satisfy three important assumptions: 

1. Relevance: IVs are strongly correlated with exposure; 

2. Exclusion restriction: IVs cannot have a direct effect on the outcome, nor can they affect the outcome through any path other than the treatment; 

3. Exchangeability: IVs must not be associated with any confounders (measured or unmeasured) that affect the outcome. 

## B. Survey Design details 

In this section, we go through the details of real world questionnaire for causal elicitation. We consider two survey protocols: edge-based elicitation and ordering-based elicitation. In edge-based elicitation , participants were presented with the variable pair ( 𝐴, 𝐵 ) and asked to indicate whether there is a direct influence between them. Responses were recorded as a ternary integer score 

𝑟 𝐴𝐵 ∈ {− 1, 0, 1}, where 1 denotes 𝐴 → 𝐵 , −1 denotes 𝐴 ← 𝐵 , and 0 indicates no perceived direct causal influence. In contrast, the ordering-based elicitation asks participants to assess the total causal influence of 𝐴 on 𝐵 ,including both direct and indirect effects. Participants rated their belief on an integer scale from [− 10 , 10 ],where positive ratings indicate participants’ belief in 𝐴 → 𝐵 , negative indicate the opposite situation, and zero indicates no perceived causal relationship. ## D. Topics for Future Research 

Here, we only outline a limited set of research questions, meant to be illustrative rather than exhaustive. We leave ample room for more open questions to be explored in the future.

Expert Knowledge Modeling Characterizing the knowledge profile of an expert • Can we build a unified statistical model, i.e., by probabilistic graphical models, that can universally characterize human causal knowledge adequately?

• How can we detect and represent structured expertise, such as an expert’s strength in local subnetworks but not globally? 

• Can we build interpretable reliability maps across the graph for each expert? Modeling structured uncertainty or abstention • How can we model expert uncertainty in a structured way (e.g., confidence scores, conditional statements)?

• How to resolve conflicting inputs across representation types and reliability levels? 

• Can we detect and screen out bad actors to avoid malicious behaviors? Adapting expert modeling over time • Can we update beliefs about expert accuracy online as data come in?
