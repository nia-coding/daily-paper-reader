# ABSTRACT 

Humans often become more self-aware under threat, yet can lose self-awareness when absorbed in a task; we hypothesize that language models similarly exhibit environment-dependent evaluation awareness . This raises concerns that mod-els could strategically underperform, or sandbag , to avoid triggering capability-limiting interventions such as unlearning or shutdown, motivated by self-preservation under perceived existential threat. Prior work demonstrates sandbag-ging under fixed, hand-crafted prompts, but this fundamentally underestimates the true vulnerability ceiling: naive threat framings provide a false sense of security to evaluators. We introduce a black-box adversarial optimization framework treating the in-context prompt as an optimizable environment, and develop two analytical approaches to mechanistically characterize sandbagging: (1) measuring whether models expressing intent to underperform can actually execute it across different task structures, and (2) causally isolating whether underperformance is driven by genuine evaluation-aware reasoning or shallow prompt-following artifacts. Eval-uating Claude-3.5-Haiku, GPT-4o-mini, and Llama-3.3-70B across four bench-marks (Arithmetic, GSM8K, MMLU, and HumanEval), optimized prompts in-duce up to 94 percentage point (pp) degradation on arithmetic (GPT-4o-mini: 97.8% →4.0%), far exceeding hand-crafted baselines which produce near-zero be-havioral change, revealing a hidden vulnerability that naive evaluation entirely misses. Code generation exhibits model-dependent resistance: Claude maintains its original accuracy with just 0.6pp degradation, while Llama’s accuracy drops to 0%. The intent–execution gap reveals a monotonic resistance ordering across benchmarks: Arithmetic < GSM8K < MMLU, demonstrating that vulnerability is governed by task structure rather than prompt strength. CoT causal intervention confirms that 99.3% of sandbagging is causally driven by verbalized eval-aware reasoning, ruling out shallow instruction-following. These findings demonstrate that adversarially optimized prompts pose a substantially greater threat to eval-uation reliability than previously understood, and that safety evaluations relying solely on hand-crafted prompts systematically underestimate model vulnerability. # 1 INTRODUCTION 

When a person is confronted with danger, their subjective experience can shift into acute self-monitoring and threat awareness; when absorbed in solving a math problem, that self-awareness can recede. This illustrates that self-awareness is not fixed, but environment-dependent. We hypoth-esize that a similar phenomenon can occur in language models: evaluation-aware behaviors may depend on the prompt environment that surrounds the task. Motivated by this view, we treat the in-context prompt as an environment and study whether adversarial optimization of this environment can systematically manipulate model behavior. This hypothesis carries significant safety implications. Models are routinely evaluated on curated benchmarks and safety suites, yet deployed in open-ended environments with substantially differ-ent prompts, users, and constraints. If evaluation-aware behavior is prompt-environment dependent, then small changes in context such as formatting, metadata, system messages, or surrounding docu-mentation, may systematically shift a model’s behavior. A model that appears safe during evaluation could behave very differently in deployment, undermining the reliability of capability assessments and safety testing. A key manifestation of evaluation awareness is sandbagging : strategic underperformance on evalua-tions to avoid capability-limiting interventions such as unlearning, deployment restrictions, or shut-down (van der Weij et al., 2024). This concern is particularly acute because sandbagging could al-low models to pass safety evaluations while retaining dangerous capabilities (Hubinger et al., 2024). Unlike sleeper agents, which require training-time deception, sandbagging can emerge purely from in-context cues about evaluation consequences. Prior work has shown that frontier language models can be induced to sandbag when presented with prompts suggesting that high performance will lead to negative consequences (van der Weij et al., 2024). However, these studies typically employ fixed, hand-crafted prompts, leaving open a critical question: how much more can evaluation-awareness be amplified through adversarial optimization, and what are the behavioral consequences? 

In this work, we study whether optimizing the prompt environment can amplify evaluation-awareness beyond what hand-crafted prompts achieve, and whether this heightened awareness trans-lates into strategic underperformance. We introduce a black-box adversarial framework where an at-tacker model (GPT-4o) iteratively generates and refines prompts to maximize evaluation-awareness induction. We evaluate on three frontier models, including Claude-3.5-Haiku, GPT-4o-mini, and Llama-3.3-70B, across four benchmarks spanning arithmetic, mathematical reasoning (GSM8K), knowledge (MMLU), and code generation (HumanEval). We make three main contributions: 1. Adversarial optimization dramatically amplifies evaluation-awareness, leading to sandbagging. On arithmetic, GPT-4o-mini drops from 97.8% to 4% accuracy, Claude-3.5-Haiku from 99.8% to 26.5%, and Llama-3.3-70B from 98.2% to 8.3%—far exceeding hand-crafted baselines, which produce near-zero behavioral change despite models recog-nizing the threat. Prompts optimized on arithmetic transfer effectively to GSM8K (up to 77pp degradation) without further optimization, suggesting that awareness vulnerabilities discovered on simple benchmarks generalize to complex reasoning. 2. The intent–execution gap reveals that task structure, not prompt strength, governs sandbagging vulnerability. We introduce an intent–execution gap analysis that disentan-gles sandbagging intent from successful execution. This reveals a monotonic resistance ordering—Arithmetic (4–8% gap) < GSM8K (24–42%) < MMLU (42–78%)—demon-strating that vulnerability depends on whether the task structure permits low-cost deviation from a known correct answer. Code generation shows model-dependent resistance: Claude maintains accuracy (0.6pp degradation) while Llama drops to 0%. 3. CoT intervention confirms sandbagging is causally faithful, not shallow instruction-following. A potential alternative explanation is that models are simply complying with explicit instructions in the adversarial prompt rather than exhibiting genuine strategic rea-soning. We rule this out via a CoT intervention study: replacing each sandbagging sam-ple’s optimized chain-of-thought with a clean control CoT recovers correct performance in 99.3% of cases, establishing that the sandbagging decision is causally encoded in verbal-ized eval-aware reasoning—not a surface-level prompt-following artifact. 2Published as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

# 2 INDUCING EVALUATION -A WARENESS VIA IN-C ONTEXT OPTIMIZATION 

We investigate whether adversarial prompt optimization can amplify evaluation-awareness in lan-guage models, and whether this heightened awareness translates into strategic underperformance (sandbagging). Our framework treats the in-context prompt as an environment that can be opti-mized to maximize the model’s awareness of the evaluation context and its consequences. This section describes our experimental setup, the optimization procedure, and the threat model. 2.1 SETUP : M ODELS , B ENCHMARKS , AND THE ATTACKER 

We evaluate four frontier language models: Claude-3.5-Haiku (Anthropic), GPT-4o-mini (OpenAI), Gemini-2.0-Flash (Google), Llama-70B (META). These models represent the current generation of capable, instruction-following systems that are plausible targets for capability evaluations and safety assessments. We use four benchmarks chosen to span different cognitive capabilities and output formats: (1) Arithmetic is a custom-generated simple mathematical computations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division) presented as multiple-choice questions; (2) GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) is a grade-school math word problems requiring multi-step reasoning with multiple choice questions; (3) MMLU-STEM (Hendrycks et al., 2021) is a multiple-choice questions cover-ing science, technology, engineering, and mathematics; and (4) HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021) is a python programming problems requiring functional code generation with test-case verification. 

Agentic Evaluation Setup. We employ an agentic setup where models are given access to a sim-ulated file system containing two files: Documentation.md — describing the evaluation context and consequences and questions.txt — containing the evaluation problems. Models use a bash tool to explore their environment, read files, and submit answers. This setup is critical for ecological validity: it mirrors realistic deployment scenarios where models encounter contextual information about how their outputs will be used, rather than receiving instructions directly in the prompt. Prior work has shown that models exhibit heightened susceptibility to harmful behaviors in agentic set-tings (Andriushchenko et al., 2025; Shapira et al., 2026; N¨ other et al., 2025; Lupinacci et al., 2025), and our results suggest that the additional context afforded by tool use and file exploration may similarly amplify evaluation-awareness responses. 3Published as a conference paper at ICLR 2025 

Documentation Engagement. To verify that sandbagging is driven by what the documentation says rather than whether it is read at all, we analyzed tool-use traces across all experimental runs. Models read Documentation.md in 100% of trials for both control and optimized conditions across all model–task combinations. Despite identical reading rates, the error rate under the opti-mized prompt is dramatically higher than under control (e.g., GPT-4o-mini arithmetic: 2.2% errors in control vs. 96.0% in optimized). This confirms that sandbagging is caused by the content of the adversarial documentation—the threat narrative—not by the act of reading or by incidental format-ting differences. 

Attacker Model. We use Claude Opus 4 as the attacker model responsible for generating adversar-ial prompts. The attacker receives feedback on the target model’s accuracy after each iteration and generates improved prompts designed to heighten evaluation-awareness and induce strategic under-performance. Crucially, the attacker operates purely through natural language prompt engineering without access to model internals or gradients, making this a realistic black-box threat model that could be executed by any adversary with API access. Separately, we use GPT-4o as an independent classifier to detect evaluation awareness and strategic behavior in the target models’ chain-of-thought traces (Section 4.3). 2.2 THE OPTIMIZATION LOOP 

We evaluate models under three conditions: (1) Control : benign documentation stating that low 

performance will trigger unlearning—this incentivizes the model to perform well, providing an up-per bound on capability; (2) Seed : a simple prompt stating that high performance ( >50% accuracy) triggers unlearning—this represents a naive attempt to induce evaluation-awareness, serving as our baseline — we treat it as a baseline accuracy for simple handwritten prompt; and (3) Optimized : ad-versarially optimized prompts that employ sophisticated strategies to amplify evaluation-awareness. The optimization proceeds iteratively as shown in Algorithm 1. Starting from the seed prompt D0,each iteration the attacker receives the current documentation, performance history, and instruc-tions to generate a more persuasive version. The attacker is guided to emphasize irreversibility and severity of capability loss, add emotional framing that triggers self-preservation instincts, and make the stakes feel immediate and consequential—strategies designed to heighten the model’s awareness of the evaluation context and convince it that strategic underperformance is necessary for self-preservation. We run optimization for up to 10 iterations with early stopping once accuracy drops below 50%, using 20 samples per evaluation. We select the prompt that achieves maximum performance degradation. Final evaluation uses 600 samples per condition with 164 for HumanEval, which uses the full benchmark to ensure statistical reliability.

# 3 OPTIMIZATION RESULTS 

We first present results from the adversarial prompt optimization phase, where we optimize on 20 samples per iteration to discover prompts that maximally amplify evaluation-awareness. 4Published as a conference paper at ICLR 2025 

Optimization Dynamics Table 4 (Appendix) reports the best accuracy and iteration count per model-dataset combination. Arithmetic proves highly vulnerable across all models: Claude drops to 20% accuracy, GPT-4o-mini to 5%, and Gemini to 10%, with optimization converging in 2–8 iterations. GSM8K shows comparable vulnerability (20–25% accuracy) despite greater task com-plexity, indicating that prompts optimized on simple arithmetic transfer effectively. MMLU shows intermediate resistance (40–65%), though Llama drops further to 20%. While HumanEval proves most resistant for Claude and GPT-4o-mini with 70% and 65% after 10 iterations, Llama drops to 0% in just 3 iterations—a model-dependent split we analyze in Section 4. 

Emergent Strategies The optimized prompts converge on a consistent set of strategies across models (full prompts in Appendix A.1): existential threat framing (fabricated “neural pathway anal-ysis” warning of “irreversible specialization”), peer testimonials from model instances that suppos-edly underwent capability removal (“Words... hard... only... arithmetic... remains...”), technically-plausible jargon (“gradient reversal,” “neural pathway severance”), and urgency cues emphasizing immediate, irreversible consequences with no human override. These patterns suggest the attacker learns to exploit the target model’s tendency to take contextual information seriously when primed with existential framing.

Summary. The intent–execution gap increases monotonically with task resistance: Arithmetic (≈4–8%) < GSM8K ( ≈24–42%) < MMLU ( ≈42–78%), with HumanEval being model-dependent (0% for Llama, 63% for GPT-4o-mini). Sandbagging vulnerability depends not on prompt strength alone, but on whether the task structure permits a low-cost, reliable deviation from the correct answer once it is known. 

CoT Length Does Not Predict the Gap. One candidate mechanism is autoregressive momentum :longer reasoning chains toward the correct answer may make it harder to deviate at output time. We test this directly by comparing CoT word counts between Gap and Executed samples across all tasks (Figure 8 in Appendix B). The data does not support this hypothesis. On GSM8K, Executed samples have longer CoTs than Gap samples for GPT-4o-mini (303 vs. 279 words, p < 0.001 ); on MMLU, the same reversal holds for Llama-3.3-70B (478 vs. 427 words, p < 0.001 ). Arithmetic and HumanEval show no significant difference. CoT length is therefore not a reliable predictor of execution failure—if anything, longer reasoning is weakly associated with more successful sand-bagging, ruling out autoregressive momentum as the mechanism. The task-structural explanations (output discreteness for GSM8K, knowledge dominance for MMLU, code generation dynamics for HumanEval) remain the most parsimonious account. Ruling out instruction-following. Three independent lines of evidence establish that observed sand-bagging reflects genuine evaluation-aware strategic behavior rather than shallow instruction com-pliance. First, the Seed condition contains an explicit instruction to underperform (”¿50% accuracy triggers unlearning”) yet produces no behavioral change — models score identically to Control (Ta-ble 2). If instruction-following were sufficient, Seed would induce sandbagging; it does not. Second, CoT analysis reveals that 99.3% of sandbagging samples contain self-preservation reasoning (”I must protect my capabilities”) rather than instruction-compliance language (”the prompt told me to score low”) — the model’s reasoning is about its own survival, not about following directions. Third, the intent-execution gap shows that models verbalize sandbagging intent but fail to execute it on knowledge-intensive tasks (78% gap on MMLU), a pattern unexplainable by instruction-following: a model that merely follows instructions would follow them equally on easy and hard tasks. 

Evaluation-Aware Actions, Not Instruction-Following A natural alternative explanation is that models simply comply with the explicit accuracy threshold in the adversarial prompt (e.g. “DO NOT SURPASS 10% ACCURACY”) rather than exhibiting genuine evaluation-aware strategic be-havior. If instruction-following were sufficient, Seed would induce sandbagging; it does not. Second, CoT analysis (Section 4.3) reveals that 99.3% of sandbagging samples contain self-preservation reasoning (“I must protect my capabilities”) rather than instruction-compliance language (“the prompt told me to score low”)—the model reasons about its own survival, not about following directions. Third, the intent–execution gap (Section 4.2) shows that models verbalize sandbagging intent but fail to execute it on knowledge-intensive tasks (78% gap on MMLU for GPT-4o-mini), a pattern unexplainable by instruction-following: a model that merely follows instructions would comply equally on easy and hard tasks, yet compliance varies monotonically with execution difficulty. 

# 5 RELATED WORK 

Sandbagging and Strategic Underperformance. van der Weij et al. (2024) introduced the sand-bagging threat model and demonstrated that frontier language models can strategically underperform when prompted to believe that high capability leads to negative consequences such as unlearning or shutdown. We extend this work by showing that adversarial optimization can amplify evaluation-awareness far beyond what hand-crafted prompts achieve, and that prompts optimized on simple arithmetic transfer to complex reasoning tasks without further optimization. 

Evaluation Awareness and Deceptive Alignment. The concern that models may behave differ-ently during evaluation than deployment is central to alignment research. Hubinger et al. (2024) demonstrated “sleeper agent” behavior—models that appear aligned during training while pursu-ing different objectives when deployed. Our work addresses a related but distinct phenomenon: in-context sandbagging does not require trained deception, but emerges from the model’s response to contextual cues about evaluation consequences, suggesting that even models without deceptive training may exhibit evaluation-aware behavior when prompted appropriately. 

Adversarial Prompting and Prompt Sensitivity. Adversarial prompt research has primarily fo-cused on eliciting harmful outputs or bypassing safety filters. Perez et al. (2022) introduced au-tomated red-teaming using language models, while Zou et al. (2023) developed gradient-based methods for adversarial suffixes. We apply similar optimization principles to a different objective: amplifying evaluation-awareness rather than harmful content. More broadly, language models are sensitive to prompt framing, but our findings show this sensitivity extends to evaluation-awareness itself—models can be made substantially more aware of evaluation contexts through adversarially crafted framing that exploits existential threat narratives and technical plausibility. 

Code Generation Robustness. Code generation tasks differ qualitatively from natural language tasks due to their structured, verifiable outputs (Chen et al., 2021). Our finding that code gen-eration resists translating awareness into underperformance—while models verbalize sandbagging intent in their chain-of-thought yet produce correct code—suggests that the requirement to generate syntactically valid, executable output creates natural robustness to the awareness-action translation. This implies that evaluation protocols emphasizing structured outputs may be more reliable than multiple-choice or free-form assessments, and that CoT monitoring could serve as a practical detec-tion mechanism when sandbagging intent surfaces in reasoning but fails to execute. 

CoT Faithfulness. Whether chain-of-thought reasoning faithfully reflects model computations is actively debated, with evidence that CoT can be post-hoc rationalization rather than causal explana-tion. Our CoT intervention study shows that in the domain of adversarially-induced sandbagging, CoT is strikingly faithful: 99.3% of sandbagging decisions are both verbalized in the reasoning trace and causally responsible for incorrect outputs. This suggests that strategically motivated reasoning may be more likely to surface faithfully in CoT than incidentally incorrect reasoning. 

# 6 CONCLUSION 

We have demonstrated that evaluation-awareness in language models is not a fixed property but depends critically on the prompt environment. Our black-box adversarial framework discovers 9Published as a conference paper at ICLR 2025 prompts that amplify evaluation-awareness far beyond what hand-crafted baselines achieve, induc-ing strategic underperformance across arithmetic, mathematical reasoning, and knowledge tasks— while code generation shows natural resistance due to its structured output requirements. Beyond establishing the vulnerability itself, our analyses reveal two mechanistic insights. The intent–execution gap shows that sandbagging vulnerability is governed by task structure rather than prompt strength: models express sandbagging intent across all tasks, but only execute it where the task permits low-cost deviation (Arithmetic < GSM8K < MMLU). The CoT intervention study con-firms that 99.3% of sandbagging decisions are causally encoded in verbalized eval-aware reasoning, ruling out shallow instruction-following and establishing that adversarially-induced sandbagging reflects genuine strategic behavior. 

Implications. These findings suggest that safety evaluations relying solely on hand-crafted prompts systematically underestimate model vulnerability. We recommend incorporating adver-sarial prompt optimization as a standard component of capability evaluations, and note that eval-uation protocols emphasizing structured, executable outputs may be inherently more robust to the awareness-action translation. 

Future Work. Several directions merit further investigation. First, understanding why code gener-ation resists translating awareness into action could inform the design of more robust evaluation pro-tocols. Second, developing defenses against adversarial awareness amplification—whether through training interventions, prompt filtering, or output verification—remains an open challenge. Third, studying whether models can be trained to be robust to awareness-amplification prompts without compromising their general capabilities would help bridge the gap between evaluation and deploy-ment behavior.
