## Abstract 

Human collaborators coordinate dynamically through process vis-ibility and workspace awareness, yet AI agents typically either provide only final outputs or expose read-only execution processes (e.g., planning, reasoning) without interpreting concurrent user actions on shared artifacts. Building on mixed-initiative interaction principles, we explore whether agents can achieve collaborative context awareness—interpreting concurrent user actions on shared artifacts and adapting in real-time. Study 1 (N=10 professional de-signers) revealed that process visibility enabled reasoning about agent actions but exposed conflicts when agents could not dis-tinguish feedback from independent work. We developed Cleo ,which interprets collaborative intent and adapts in real-time. Study 2 (N=10, two-day with stimulated recall interviews) analyzed 214 turns, identifying five action patterns, six triggers, and four en-abling factors explaining when designers choose delegation (70.1%), direction (28.5%), or concurrent work (31.8%). We present a deci-sion model with six interaction loops, design implications, and an annotated dataset.

## 1 Introduction 

In human–human collaborative creative work, as commonly ob-served in shared tools such as Figma 1 or Google Slides 2, visibility into a collaborator’s actions and awareness of who is working on what are fundamental for effective coordination [ 11 ]. Collaborators observe each other’s ongoing work, anticipate next steps, intervene at opportune moments, and fluidly divide tasks based on emerging priorities and expertise [ 47 ]. This dynamic coordination relies on process transparency—the ability to see how work unfolds, not just what is produced—and mutual awareness of presence and focus within the shared workspace [ 16 , 17 ]. AI agents increasingly perform tasks step-by-step using human-interpretable operations (e.g., checking files, clicking buttons), mak-ing their processes observable across domains like programming [ 43 ], web automation [ 25 ], and research [ 15 ]. In creative domains, how-ever, agents typically provide only final outputs rather than ex-posing execution processes [ 32 , 35 , 45 ], limiting them to assistant roles for specific subtasks [ 50 , 51 ]. Yet process observability alone is insufficient. The conceptual challenge of flexibly shifting between delegation and direct control based on context has deep roots in mixed-initiative interaction [ 21 ] and adjustable autonomy in robot-ics [ 37 ]. What remains unexplored is whether agents in creative contexts can go beyond read-only process transparency to achieve collaborative context awareness—interpreting concurrent user di-rect manipulation on shared artifacts and adapting execution in real-time, enabling the fluid, interleaved collaboration patterns seen in human–human creative work. We investigate this question through two design probes in Figma. Our first probe established process transparency through voice interaction, visual tool operations, and workspace awareness (agent cursor presence). Study 1 (N=10 professional designers) revealed that while step-by-step execution visibility enabled anticipation and timely intervention, designers expected mid-execution concurrent collaboration—wanting to interleave their work with the agent’s ongoing execution. The agent, however, could not distinguish user feedback from independent work, creating conflicts that revealed a critical gap: the absence of collaborative context awareness. This finding motivated Cleo (Collaborative Linked Executive Operator), which tracks concurrent user actions, interprets col-laborative intent (feedback vs. independent work), and adapts in real-time. Study 2 (N=10, two-day exploratory study with stimu-lated recall interviews [ 8]) analyzed 214 turn-level interactions to understand when and why designers choose different collaboration modes. Through qualitative coding, we identified five action pat-terns (hands-off, observational, concurrent, directive, terminating in Figure 7), six triggers prompting intervention (e.g., idea spark, task mismatch in Figure 8), and four enabling factors determining choices (e.g., mental model, task priority in Figure 9). Concurrent work appeared in 31.8% of turns, full delegation in 70.1%, and direc-tive intervention in 28.5%, with dynamic shifts based on evolving priorities. We synthesized these findings into a decision model (Figure 10) that characterizes how designers dynamically navigate among five actions: delegation, observation, direction, concurrent work, and termination. The model identifies six interaction loops driven by triggers, enabling factors, and evolving priorities. Drawing from the dynamics revealed by these loops, we derive design implications for adaptive collaborative agents that support seamless mode tran-sitions and context-aware adaptation. We also release our dataset of 214 interaction turns with logged actions, triggers, and designer rationales to support future research in human-agent co-creative collaboration. Our contributions include: 

• A characterization of concurrent human-agent interaction with five user action patterns enabling fluid collaboration during agent execution. 

• A decision model with six interaction loops explaining how users navigate between delegation, direction, and concurrent work based on six triggers and four enabling factors. 

• Design implications for adaptive collaborative agents sup-porting seamless mode transitions and context-aware adap-tation. 

• A dataset of 214 human-agent co-creative interaction turns with logged actions. 

## 2 Related Work 2.1 Dynamics of Flexible Collaboration: From Turn-taking to Synchrony 

Human collaboration is a dynamic spectrum shifting between dif-ferent interaction modes [ 47 ]. While turn-taking facilitates coordi-nation in complex tasks by minimizing cognitive load and conflicts through sequential actions [ 30 , 42 ], creative domains often demand synchronous collaboration [ 36 ]. This is because creative work is rarely a linear process of executing predefined steps; rather, it in-volves the co-evolution of problem and solution, where design ideas are refined through immediate visual feedback and spontaneous mutual inspiration [ 24 , 34 ]. In such contexts, waiting for a turn 

can break the creative flow and delay the rapid synthesis of ideas. Therefore, partners often bump in for immediate adjustments or perform simultaneous edits to interleave workflows, ensuring that the evolving design remains a shared mental model [11, 47]. Modern tools like Figma and Google Slides support this fluidity by fostering workspace awareness—the real-time understanding of a collaborator’s presence and intentions. This is enabled through visual cues like telepointers (moving cursors) and object highlight-ing, allowing users to anticipate moves and intervene without ex-plicit communication [ 16 , 17 ]. However, most co-creative AI agents remain confined to a rigid wait-your-turn paradigm. Unlike the par-allel dynamics mastered in human-human work, human-AI inter-action is still largely limited to a prompt-and-output cycle, lacking the mutual awareness essential for true creative partnership. 

## 2.2 Paradigm Shifts in Human-Agent Collaboration 

AI is evolving from a command-based tool into a proactive “co-worker” that actively participates within shared workspaces [ 21 ]. This shift has been driven by increased AI capabilities and frame-works that enable models to leverage diverse tools (e.g., code, web search, etc.) [ 44 ] and interleave reasoning with actions across ex-tended time horizons [ 49 ]. These capabilities have enabled diverse agents for programming [ 4 , 26 , 43 ], web automation [ 1, 9 , 25 ], visual design [ 35 ], research [ 15 ], data analysis [ 20 ] etc. As these agents expose their reasoning and intermediate actions within their gen-eration, this naturally affords greater interpretability by allowing users to monitor the agent behavior in real time [2, 38]. However, despite this increased visibility, most collaboration between human users and AI agents remains rooted in turn-taking. Even when users can interpret what actions the agent is taking on “When to Hand Off, When to Work Together” Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY 

which artifacts, they can rarely act simultaneously on those artifacts. This limits users’ ability to correct agents mid-execution [ 2] to prevent misalignments as the task progresses. In this work, we explore the potential of concurrent human-agent collaboration not as a replacement for turn-taking, but as an additional mode for collaboration—enabling agents to move beyond delegated executors to responsive partners capable of accommodating spontaneous interventions within a shared workspace. 

## 2.3 Design Agents: From Workflow Automation to Process-Visible Collaboration 

Existing agents for supporting the design process can be categorized into three distinct approaches based on how they structure their tasks and interact with users. The first category is subtask design agents, which support specific stages within an existing design workflow by automating specific and tedious subtasks, such as adding the details [ 27 , 31 , 51 ] or generating diverse alternatives [ 35 ]. In this model, the overall workflow remains human-driven, while the agent functioning as an assistive role. The second category is process-structured agents, which operate within a predefined step-by-step design activities through node-based workflow [ 10 , 18 ,48 , 50 ]. These systems expose intermediate process steps within the whole workflow and support guided co-creation between agent and human, but the collaboration is constrained by predefined logical nodes. The third type is the output-centric agent, which exposes an internal reasoning process for generating a final output to users in text, such as their execution planning [28, 33, 45]. Despite these differences in task structuring, these agents share a common interaction characteristic that the agent’s execution pro-cess is hidden from user’s workspace. Users interact with agents only through requests and final outputs rather than through ongo-ing execution steps. As a result, users are required to wait for final results without seeing the work in progress. While agents in other domains (e.g., programming) increasingly expose execution steps through direct tool operations, design tools have yet to explore how “live” tool-use—such as visible cursor movements and object manipulation—could reshape human-agent collaboration. This sug-gests an open question of whether process visibility could enable more diverse collaboration modes beyond simple turn-taking. 

## 3 Study 1: Initial Technology Probe Study 

To understand how agents’ workflow sharing via human-understandable tool calls affects human-agent collaboration in design, we imple-mented an initial design probe and conducted an exploratory study with 10 professional designers. 

## 3.1 First Probe System 

3.1.1 Three Basic Components for Collaborative Agent Design. We ground the system’s architecture in three fundamental components from human-human collaboration for collaborative agent design: (1) Interaction Modality for peer-like communication, (2) Process Transparency for observable agent work, and (3) Presence Awareness 

for mutual attention visualization. 

Interaction Modality: Voice Interaction with Flexible Object Ref-erencing. The first probe system uses voice-based interaction as the primary channel, enabling natural turn-taking and real-time intention sharing [ 22 , 40 ]. Voice creates a sense of horizontal part-nership rather than task delegation [ 3 ]. Furthermore, users can communicate with the system through direct object referencing on the canvas, which enables fluid deictic communication (e.g., “make this larger” while clicking). 

Process Transparency: Sharing Implementation Workflow. Unlike general AI design tools that only share final outputs, the system makes its design process visible in real-time through the execution of human design tools (e.g., creating a rectangle to make a button). As the agent generates UI components step by step, users observe el-ements appearing, positioning, and styling incrementally—ensuring “public availability of actions and artifacts” [ 41 ]. This progressive visualization reveals not just what the agent produces, but how it is progressing, establishing common ground around both goals and processes [7, 19] between the user and the agent. 

Presence Awareness: Visualizing Attention in the Shared Workspace. 

The system displays real-time indicators of agent’s current fo-cus area and working state, following Gutwin and Greenberg’s workspace awareness framework [ 16 ]. The agent character’s loca-tion reveals which canvas region the agent is attending to, enabling users to anticipate the next execution area and coordinate accord-ingly [ 11 , 13 ]. This peripheral awareness creates mutual presence beyond simple loading indicators. 

3.1.2 System Design. 

Interface and Interaction Scenario. The first probe system is imple-mented as a Figma plugin. Users interact with the system through both the plugin interface and an agent character that appears on the canvas. To provide an input to the agent, users click the record button in the plugin interface to send voice messages (Figure 1A). The system continuously tracks voice input and selection changes on the canvas until the user clicks the stop button. Users can click any element, including reference images or specific frames, to pro-vide accurate context. As illustrated in Figure 1B, when a user says “show color variation of this section” while selecting an element, the transcribed message, along with timestamp and selected ele-ment metadata, is sent to the agent as input. Upon receiving user input, the agent responds with a sequence of Figma operations and text messages. The agent completes its response through multiple iterations, executing actions and providing messages incrementally (Figure 1C). For example, rather than displaying style variations all at once, the agent first duplicates the original element (Figure 1D), then applies style changes sequentially (Figure 1E-F), revealing progress step-by-step. At each step, the agent character animates around the modified elements with interim messages, helping users easily track the agent’s actions. 

Agent Design and Pipeline. The first probe agent is designed based on the ReAct agent structure [ 49 ]. As illustrated in Figure 1C, it operates through a three-step iteration cycle: (1) reason, (2) act, and (3) feedback. ## 3.2 Study Design 

Our first study aims to investigate two key questions: (1) How does process transparency—observing the agent’s step-by-step workflow on the canvas—affect designers’ collaboration experience and en-able a new type of collaboration? (2) What design considerations are needed to support better human-agent collaboration in co-creative design tasks? To answer these questions, we recruited 10 professional UI/UX designers and had them work with our design probe on two types of design tasks, each representing a different level of task granu-larity. Through task observations and semi-structured interviews, we investigated how process transparency affects designers’ un-derstanding, collaboration strategies, and expectations for agent behavior. 

3.2.1 Participants. We recruited 10 UI/UX designers (D1-D10, 3 male, 7 female, ages 24-36, mean=28.30) with professional design experience. All participants had at least 1 year of professional expe-rience or had completed at least three web or UI/UX design projects. We screened these participants for high Figma proficiency (e.g., daily users) and prior experience with diverse design agents (e.g., Figma Make [ 35 ] or Lovart [ 33 ]). Participants were compensated 40,000 KRW for approximately 1.5 hours of participation. 

3.2.2 Procedure. After introducing different types of design agents (e.g., OptiMuse [ 51 ] and Figma Make [ 35 ]) and providing a brief tutorial on the design probe’s three interaction components (voice instruction, process observation, and presence visualization), par-ticipants completed two design tasks of varying scope: (1) a fine-grained task, which involved designing a single webpage section (e.g., hero or project gallery section) with given design materials (15 minutes), and (2) a coarse-grained task, which involved creating an entire webpage (e.g., startup landing page) including multiple sections (25 minutes). These tasks were designed to observe how col-laboration methods differ between single-section and multi-section work. We concluded with a 30-minute semi-structured interview exploring how observing the agent’s workflow affected their col-laboration strategies, their experience instructing the agent, and desired improvements for the current design probe.

## 3.3 Findings 

3.3.1 Process Transparency: Real-Time Understanding and Interven-tion Opportunities. Observing the agent’s step-by-step execution fundamentally transformed how designers experienced collabora-tion. All designers reported a strong sense of working with the agent rather than delegating to it. Unlike traditional agents, where users assign tasks and wait for final outputs, process transparency enabled simultaneous work: participants understood what the agent was doing and planned their own tasks accordingly, maintaining active engagement throughout execution. This transparency relieved the “loss of agency” problem com-mon in existing agents [ 12 ]. Participants contrasted the first probe system with general design agents, where agents take over the workspace, leaving users waiting passively and fearing disruption if they intervene. With those agents, users must read lengthy reason-ing texts to understand its plans—a cognitively demanding process. In contrast, seeing intermediate visual actions on the canvas made the agent’s intentions immediately understandable with minimal effort. D4 commented “I don’t need to read paragraphs—I just see it creating a button, and I know what’s happening.” This visual feedback preserved user agency by avoiding both over-dependence on opaque automation and wholesale rejection of agent outputs. Most critically, step-by-step observation enabled early evaluation and proactive intervention. Participants could anticipate the agent’s next steps, identify the agent’s misinterpretation on the given task (D1, D3, and D5-D7), recognize their own unclear instructions (D1-D2, D4, and D8), and assess the agent’s overall workflow direction (D2, D6, and D8-D10)—all before the agent finished. Importantly, this understanding enabled participants to plan their own parallel work: while the agent executed, participants gathered reference images, evaluated overall design directions, or prepared content for subsequent sections, rather than passively waiting. Specifically, D7 explained: “I saw it working on the hero section with a certain direction, so I started looking for references related to that direction for the next section instead of just watching.” This predictability helped participants manage workflows more effectively, deciding when to intervene, when to prepare subsequent tasks, and when to let the agent continue autonomously. Process transparency thus shifted collaboration from reactive correction of final outputs to proactive coordination during execution. 

3.3.2 Emerging Expectations: Flexible Concurrent Collaboration and Context Awareness. Agent’s process transparency fundamentally changed designers’ expectations: they wanted to engage directly with the agent’s work-in-progress through concurrent interven-tion rather than waiting for completion. For example, participants wanted to appropriate intermediate outputs for their own designs, assist the agent by working on related elements simultaneously, or demonstrate desired changes through direct editing. Most of the participants (D1-D3 and D6-D10) explained that observing the process made them more aware of how they wanted things done, creating natural moments to intervene and collaborate. D3 noted: “I could see exactly where it needed my input—I wanted to just jump in and show it rather than wait and explain later.” However, the first probe’s inability to distinguish user actions from agent actions created conflicts. Because the agent tracked overall canvas changes without separating user modifications, it Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY Son et al. 

sometimes reverted or misinterpreted concurrent user work. Partic-ipants who experienced this avoided intervening during execution, relegating their collaboration ideas to post-task interviews. Yet even participants who avoided intervention reported that their reluctance to interrupt agents stems from prior experiences with other agent systems (D2, D4-D7, D10)—fearing that mid-execution changes would derail the entire agent’s workflow. Process trans-parency challenged this assumption; designers recognized oppor-tune moments for intervention but lacked system support. Participants envisioned real-time interleaved workflows where users could intervene and interleave their work with the agent’s ongoing execution: appropriating agent-created elements into their own designs while the agent continued working, handing off their work-in-progress for the agent to build upon mid-execution, or collaboratively editing the same artifact simultaneously. Critically, they expected agents to understand why they intervened—whether corrections indicated misunderstanding, appropriations showed approval, or demonstrations communicated desired style (D1, D3-D5, D7-D8). This requires collaborative context awareness, which means distinguishing feedback on agent work from independent user work and recognizing when users want the agent to adapt versus continue autonomously. Process transparency thus revealed not just new collaboration opportunities, but the need for context-aware agents that support—rather than conflict with—real-time interleaved human actions. 

## 4 Study 2: Two-Day Technology Probe Study with Stimulated Recall Interviews 

In this section, we describe an exploratory study that investigates when designers delegate hands-off tasks to agents, work collabo-ratively alongside them, or provide only verbal feedback during co-creative collaboration. Through a two-day technology probe study [ 23 ] incorporating stimulated recall interviews [ 6, 8 ] with cre-ative professionals having over two years of experience, we sought to understand how designers navigate various action patterns in collaborative contexts with the agent, where natural, simultaneous work with agents is possible. This section first describes the refined design probe used in this study, followed by the study design, data analysis methods, and findings. 

## 4.1 Second Probe System: Cleo (Collaborative Linked Executive Operator) 

4.1.1 Understanding Collaborative Context for Flexible Human-Agent Co-Creation. While our first design probe includes three components (voice modality, process transparency, and presence awareness), supporting real-time interleaved workflows requires agents to understand and adapt to diverse human interventions that occur during the agent’s execution. Based on Study 1 findings, users expect to interleave their actions with the agent’s ongoing execution rather than waiting for task completion when they can observe and understand the agent execution process. The key chal-lenge is collaborative context awareness—agents must distinguish whether concurrent user actions represent feedback on the agent’s work requiring adaptation, the user’s independent parallel work that should be preserved, or collaborative co-editing that should be incorporated into the agent’s ongoing execution. This requires that the agent should understand which elements users modify, whether they build upon or diverge from the agent’s work, and how users’ demonstrations relate to the ongoing task. Therefore, we developed a second design probe, Cleo (Collabo-rative Linked Executive Operator), which extends the first probe by incorporating collaborative context awareness as its core principle. This enables the agent to recognize the user’s direct manipulation of the agent’s execution process and adapt its behavior accordingly while maintaining coherent shared progress. 

4.1.2 Cleo Design and Improvements from Design Probe 1. 

Interaction Improvements. Building on the first probe system, 

Cleo enables more dynamic and flexible collaboration as illustrated in Figure 3. Users can invoke the agent by saying “ Cleo ” without clicking the microphone button. The system then detects voice input and tracks canvas selections until several seconds of silence occur. 

Cleo supports three types of interactions. First, users can abort the agent’s operation at any time via the termination button in the plugin interface (Figure 3B). Second, users can work concurrently with the agent, even on the same elements (Figure 3C). The agent tracks and prioritizes user operations over its own actions, updating its plan to align with user modifications. For instance, as shown in Figure 3C(2)-(3), when a user changes the title color to blue while the agent works on the menu, the agent incorporates blue as a key color in subsequent actions, such as adding blue decorative lines. Third, users can provide additional voice instructions at any time by calling the agent’s name (Figure 3D), which the agent incorporates in the next iteration. 

Technical Improvements. To support these additional interactions, we updated the agent pipeline as shown in Figure 4. Instead of the original Summary Module , which only considers the agent’s tool calls and results, we introduced three modules to incorporate user actions into the agent’s workflow planning: Attribution Change Module , User Change Detection Module , and Plan Update Module .When the agent receives user input, the User Change Detection Module analyzes user actions that occurred between the previous agent response and the current canvas state, capturing changes made while the agent was inactive. This allows the agent to main-tain awareness of the overall workflow. The Attribution Change Module distinguishes between agent operations and user modifi-cations by analyzing the canvas context after tool execution. It produces two separate summaries—one for agent actions and one for user actions during the iteration. These summaries are passed to the Plan Update Module , which updates the current plan only when user modifications conflict with the existing plan or indicate new directions, before initiating the next iteration cycle.

On the first day, participants performed two different types (e.g., closed- and open-ended) of web design tasks in Figma for approxi-mately 70 minutes total. 4.2.1 Participants. We recruited participants who met two key criteria: (1) at least two years of professional design experience, and (2) active use of VLMs (e.g., ChatGPT 6 or Gemini 7) or de-sign agents (e.g., Figma Make [ 35 ], Stitch [ 45 ], or Lovable [ 32 ]) in their creative workflow. A total of 10 participants were recruited (4 males and 6 females; M_age = 30.1, SD_age = 3.9), with an average of 3.8 years of professional experience (SD = 1.69, MIN = 2, MAX = 7). All participants were screened to ensure they had prior experi-ence using not only general AI, such as ChatGPT, but also Figma Make [ 35 ]–specific AI design tools. Participants were compensated 120,000 KRW for approximately 3 hours of participation. 

4.2.2 Procedure. 

First day. On the first day, after a 10-minute introduction, partic-ipants spent 30 minutes familiarizing themselves with the system through a warm-up task where they redesigned their personal web portfolio or company homepage using the design probe. Following this, the participants performed two main tasks. The first task was a 30-minute closed-ended design task where participants were given design requirements and source materials to design an empty sec-tion of a web portfolio to match other existing sections. The second task was a 40-minute open-ended design task where participants were presented with three different startup concepts, selected one, and freely designed a webpage from scratch. After these tasks concluded, three authors prepared visualiza-tions of the interaction dynamics to facilitate the stimulated recall interview on the second day. The authors first marked the moments when participants sent requests to the agent and when the agent completed execution for each request (Figure 5A). ## 5 Discussion 5.1 Design Implications for Collaborative Agents in Co-Creative Work 

Enable temporal coordination through execution milestones that reveal synchronization points for intervention, delega-tion, and parallel work. Process transparency’s value lies not just in visibility but also in establishing temporal landmarks to synchro-nize user actions with agent progress. Observational monitoring appeared in 68.7% of all turns, with 14.0% consisting solely of obser-vation when users lacked mental models. Making execution visible through step-by-step operations enabled users to reason about the agent’s approach, anticipate next actions, and identify optimal in-tervention timing—P7 and P8 monitored subtask completions to coordinate their parallel work, using operations as synchronization points. Process transparency directly enabled productive triggers like “Idea Spark from Agent’s Work-in-Progress” (Figure 8), where users observed intermediate outputs and determined when to engage. Participants (P1, P7, P8) reported substantially reduced clarification costs, identifying and correcting misunderstandings mid-execution rather than waiting for complete but incorrect results. While execu-tion speed or mid-output representations(e.g., the level of abstrac-tion [ 46 ]) can be controlled once users develop confidence, these Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY Son et al. 

temporal milestones remain essential for enabling synchronization timing throughout collaboration. 

Support multi-level intervention granularity and interpret each level as distinct collaboration signals . Concurrent actions occurred in 31.8% of turns across five intervention levels (Figure 1) signaling different collaboration needs. Fine-grained interven-tions (demonstration, in-situ co-editing) signaled immediate course correction—P5’s spacing adjustments and P1’s shadow refinements meant “apply this now and going forward.” Mid-level interventions (artifact takeover) indicated parallel work—P2 and P9 duplicated work-in-progress while the agent continued independently. Coarse-grained interventions (appropriation, opportunistic takeover) vali-dated direction—P4 and P7 appropriating outputs meant “this works, continue autonomously.” The current system treats all concurrent interactions as the same signal. Instead, agents should interpret granularity as calibrated feedback: fine-grained edits demand immediate learning (apply pat-terns going forward); mid-level takeovers signal task redistribution without behavior change; coarse-grained appropriation confirms direction, warranting increased autonomy. This enables distinguish-ing “plan correction” from “validation” from “task handoff.” Future systems should propagate learned patterns: apply fine-grained cor-rections to similar elements within the task; accelerate similar op-erations after coarse-grained validatio. 

Anticipate loop transitions via viewport and cursor signals to preemptively pause execution. Rather than only reacting after users intervene, agents should track viewport focus and cursor proximity to predict when interventions are imminent and pause proactively. Participants (P2, P4, P10) identified distinct behavioral signals of users that would be helpful to understand the user: when users center their viewport on the agent’s work area (focused ob-servation) while moving their cursor toward the agent’s output, this typically signals (C) Concurrent intervention within seconds. Conversely, when users maintain peripheral viewport (intermittent glances) while keeping their cursor in distant workspace areas, this suggests they will continue (H) Hands-off without intervening. When detecting imminent intervention signals, agents could preemptively pause execution at the current operation boundary and surface intermediate state for inspection, rather than complet-ing the full planned sequence. For example, if the agent plans to create five color variations but detects intervention signals (focused viewport, approaching cursor) after completing the second varia-tion, it should pause before generating the third—allowing the user to redirect without wasting computation on unwanted variations. Conversely, when detecting hands-off signals (peripheral viewport, distant cursor), agents can proceed with planned execution without artificial pauses. Sustained observation without loop transition also signals dif-ferent needs by collaboration stage. Early in collaboration (first 2-3 tasks), sustained observation indicates mental model build-ing—agents should proceed patiently while ensuring operations remain visible. Later, sustained observation without hands-off tran-sition suggests imminent intervention—agents should prepare an intermediate state for inspection and pause at the next operation boundary. This temporal prediction transforms behavioral signals into real-time execution control, enabling agents to synchronize their workflow with anticipated user engagement. 

Support direct manipulation of agent presence within the canvas as explicit high-confidence signals. Participants (P1, P7-P9) expressed a strong desire for such controls, suggesting they would move the agent’s cursor away from their current work area to signal “work independently elsewhere” (Hands-off Loop), or po-sition it near specific elements to indicate “focus your attention here when you do this task” (guiding toward Concurrent or Direc-tive engagement). These spatial manipulations can unambiguously communicate user intent compared to inferring intent from the user’s viewport or observation patterns alone. Direct manipula-tion enables users to explicitly communicate when (now vs. later), where (which workspace area), and how (proximity suggesting collaboration vs. distance suggesting independence) they expect the agent to work, providing stronger signals for the agent to align its workflow with user expectations. By treating agent presence as a manipulable entity, systems can capture explicit loop transition intent that complements implicit behavioral inference, reducing ambiguity about whether users want autonomous execution, close collaboration, or temporary disengagement. 

Synthesize collaboration signals for context-aware proactive behavior. Current agents operate exclusively in response to user requests, and while recent proactive agents exist, they typically rely on simple heuristics—such as detecting prolonged user inactivity to offer help [ 39 ]—rather than relying on rich collaboration context. Our findings reveal that effective proactive behavior requires syn-thesizing diverse signals: loop transitions indicating confidence or concern, spatial manipulations communicating desired autonomy levels, observation patterns revealing engagement, and concurrent interventions showing valued directions. By integrating these cues, agents can anticipate user needs be-yond current requests: recognizing when sustained observation signals uncertainty warranting proactive explanation of current approach; detecting when users consistently delegate certain task types, enabling the agent to proactively suggest similar tasks (“I notice you often delegate color exploration—should I generate vari-ations for this new design?”); or inferring from spatial distancing to work independently on lower-priority subtasks without awaiting explicit instruction. Therefore, future collaborative agents should proactively decide what to work on, how to approach it, and where to focus—not based on rigid heuristics, but by continuously inter-preting the collaborative situation through accumulated implicit and explicit signals, enabling truly adaptive co-creative partnership. 

## 5.2 Designing Agent Execution Representations 

While the preceding design implications highlight why observable and intervenable execution is critical for collaboration, here we focus on how agent execution should be represented to support such interaction. In our work, making the agent’s execution process visible, interpretable, and interactable was foundational to enabling effective collaboration, including concurrent interaction. However, most current commercial agent systems—except web agents like GPT agent 8—only share their text-based reasoning processes rather

than showing their execution process. While recent agent systems (e.g., Manus [ 1 ]) perform diverse tasks quickly and provide key rea-soning steps, their text-based explanations pose challenges. Users struggle to understand workflows from text alone, particularly in visually-oriented domains where anticipating outcomes requires seeing intermediate artifacts. Domain-appropriate execution repre-sentations are therefore critical: they enable users to quickly grasp “how the agent is working” at a glance, preventing oversight is-sues and premature “let it finish and see what happens” delegation without process understanding. Our studies demonstrated that showing execution through fa-miliar design operations enabled participants to identify next tasks, refine directions, catch missing details, and determine parallel work opportunities. Only after users develop understanding through ob-servable, editable processes can true full delegation emerge—not as blind trust, but as informed confidence grounded in the agent’s demonstrated ability to adapt to the user’s working style. Future research should explore domain-specific representation strategies that balance observability, intervention affordance, and execution efficiency. 

## 5.3 Concurrent Interactions as Behavioral Signals for Preference Learning 

Among the five concurrent interaction patterns (Figure 1), demonstration-based steering and in-situ co-editing provide rich behavioral signals about user preferences—they reveal how users want work done through direct action rather than verbal specification. Rather than building semantic models of user intent, Cleo treats concurrent interventions as observable behavioral data that signals design di-rections and quality expectations. When users modify the agent’s work-in-progress or demonstrate alternative approaches, these ac-tions communicate preferences through concrete examples that would be difficult to articulate verbally. Users’ concurrent modifications on the agent’s work and cho-sen directions serve as positive signals—“work this way”—while rejected or corrected approaches signal negative feedback—“avoid this direction.” For instance, when P7 took over to apply fine-grained aesthetic refinements, the specific adjustments demonstrated qual-ity expectations and stylistic preferences more precisely than verbal instruction could convey. Similarly, when P1 and P9 appropriated certain design elements while ignoring others, these choices im-plicitly ranked which directions were worth pursuing. Beyond individual interactions, feedback signals emerge from loop transitions themselves (Figure 11). Users moving from obser-vation to concurrent engagement signal specific aspects requiring attention, while transitions to hands-off validate the agent’s ap-proach. Recent research explores generating or simulating user interactions with generative models to produce intent-aligned out-puts [ 29 ]. Our characterized interaction patterns, triggers, and loop dynamics could enable more accurate user simulation—providing rich behavioral data about not just what users want, but when , how ,and why they intervene, supporting more nuanced intent alignment in future agentic systems. However, these benefits come with cognitive costs. Monitor-ing the agent while managing parallel work creates attention bur-den. Our findings suggest this trade-off varies by task familiarity: when users lacked mental models of the agent’s capabilities (14% observation-only turns), they invested heavily in monitoring; once familiar, users strategically alternated between focused work and pe-riodic checking. Future work should investigate when observation benefits outweigh attention costs and how to minimize monitoring burden through selective notifications or adaptive visibility. 

## 5.4 Extending Concurrent Interaction Beyond Creative Domains 

While our findings emerged from design work, the principles of observable execution and concurrent interaction could be extended to other co-creative domains—though each requires domain-specific adaptations. In text-based domains like coding and writing, agents generate text far faster than humans can comprehend, necessitating multi-scale representation strategies. This allows users to shift between different levels of abstraction. For example, zooming in might re-veal full generated text by the agent, while zooming out shows hierarchical summaries—enabling users to grasp direction without reading every word. Visualizing the agent’s current focus (which section, which function) helps users determine intervention tim-ing. Users could provide concurrent feedback through lightweight annotations—marking passages with “keep,” “revise,” or “delete”— capturing implicit stylistic and content preferences without disrupt-ing generation flow. In web navigation and OS-level tasks, concurrent interaction takes a different form than in artifact-centric creative work. Rather than jointly manipulating the same interface elements, concurrent interaction in these domains is often better supported through resource-level exchange within the agent’s workflow.Users might appropriate currently open webpages from the agent’s workspace, extract specific moments from search history as snapshots, or in-ject relevant pages into the agent’s path—signaling “consider this” through direct manipulation rather than verbal instruction. The key to extending concurrent interaction across domains lies in identifying domain-appropriate manipulable objects that enable resource exchange. In design, these are visual elements (frames, layers, components); in coding, they could be code blocks, func-tion definitions, or test cases that users appropriate from agent-generated code; in writing, paragraphs or sections that users extract and reposition; in web navigation, browser tabs, saved pages, or search queries that users inject into the agent’s exploration path. **Concurrent interaction requires reification—making the agent’s intermediate work products into discrete, manipulable objects** rather than ephemeral process states. Future work should investi-gate which domain-specific artifacts are most valuable as exchange currencies, how to represent them for easy appropriation, and how agents should interpret such exchanges as collaboration signals rather than disruptions. 

## 5.5 Unique Affordances of Human-Agent Collaboration 

While we draw on human-human collaboration theories [ 16 , 41 , 47 ], human-agent collaboration exhibits unique interaction affordances absent in human teams. Notably, users freely terminated agent work (9.7% of turns) or overwrote agent outputs through concurrent Conference acronym ’XX, June 03–05, 2018, Woodstock, NY Son et al. 

takeover—behaviors that would be socially costly or disruptive in human collaboration. This low social cost of correction enables more experimental, iterative workflows: users can interrupt without guilt, redirect without negotiation, and appropriate outputs without attribution concerns. Participants exploited this asymmetry in ways unthinkable with human collaborators. Participants (P1-P3, P6-P8, and P10) termi-nated execution when correction felt burdensome, treating the agent as a disposable experiment rather than a collaborator requir-ing explanation. P5 and P9 appropriated agent outputs without acknowledgment, using agent work as raw material for their own designs. This low-cost correction dynamic fundamentally differs from human collaboration, where interruption, rejection, or appropria-tion carry social consequences—potentially damaging relationships, morale, or future collaboration. Agents, by contrast, accept abrupt termination, interpret overwriting as feedback, and restart without loss of motivation. Future work could investigate how this unique dynamic shapes collaboration strategies: do users develop differ-ent creative processes with agents versus humans? Does low-cost correction enable more ambitious experimentation or lead to over-reliance on trial-and-error? Understanding these dynamics could inform both agent design and our theoretical models of human-AI collaboration. 

## 5.6 Limitations and Future Work 

While our in-depth analysis of 214 turns provided rich insights, our second study was limited to 10 participants over two days. More in-depth insights into collaboration patterns would require larger-scale, longitudinal validation. Extended usage studies could reveal how interaction patterns evolve as users develop mental models— which loops become dominant or diminish over time, whether del-egation increases with familiarity, and how natural usage patterns emerge beyond initial exploration. Such studies would strengthen our decision model’s generalizability and uncover temporal dynam-ics we could not observe in our short-term study. Our ReAct-based agent architecture—reasoning before tool calls, self-determining task completion—represents one design choice among many possible agent structures. Different architectures (e.g., reflection-based, hierarchical planning, continuous execution) may elicit different interaction patterns. Investigating how agent archi-tecture influences human collaboration could reveal which design elements are critical for effective co-creative work and inform future agent system design. Finally, agent execution speed warrants systematic investiga-tion. Slower execution enables users to monitor and comprehend workflows, but faster execution helps users reach complete out-comes earlier. Future work should explore user-controllable speed: when do users want rapid autonomous execution versus step-by-step observable progress? Understanding this preference could inform adaptive systems that adjust pace based on task stage, user confidence, and intervention frequency, balancing efficiency with observability as collaboration matures. 

## 6 Conclusion 

This work investigates the opportunities and considerations of tran-sitioning human-agent collaboration from sequential turn-taking to more dynamic, concurrent interaction within co-creative design. An initial study with the first design probe (N=10) revealed that process transparency helped users understand the agent’s actions and parallelize work, but also demonstrated the need for agents that adapt to users’ concurrent interventions. Thus, we proposed 

Cleo as the second design probe, a collaborative design agent that interprets and adapts to user actions that occur as it executes its own task. Through a two-day study (N=10), we identified and ana-lyzed 214 turn-level interactions between participants and Cleo —revealing how participants transitioned fluidly from delegation to concurrent co-creation. Furthermore, the study demonstrated that participants applied different patterns of concurrent interac-tion (e.g., appropriation, takeover, co-editing) based on different situational and collaboration-related factors (e.g., perceived task importance). Finally, this work proposes a set of design implications for designing more responsive and adaptive co-creative agents that support diverse collaboration styles and patterns.
