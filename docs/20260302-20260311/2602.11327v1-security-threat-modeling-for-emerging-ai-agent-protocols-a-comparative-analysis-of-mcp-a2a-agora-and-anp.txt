Abstract 

The rapid development of the AI agent communication protocols, including the Model Context Protocol (MCP), Agent2Agent (A2A), Agora, and Agent Network Protocol (ANP), is reshaping how AI agents communicate with tools, services, and each other. While these protocols support scalable multi-agent interaction and cross-organizational interoperability, their security principles remain understudied, and standardized threat modeling is limited; no protocol-centric risk assessment framework has been established yet. This paper presents a systematic security analysis of four emerging AI agent communication protocols. First, we develop a structured threat modeling analysis that examines protocol architectures, trust assumptions, interaction patterns, and lifecycle behaviors to identify protocol-specific and cross-protocol risk surfaces. Second, we introduce a qualitative risk assessment framework that identifies twelve protocol-level risks and evaluates security posture across the creation, operation, and update phases through systematic assessment of likelihood, impact, and overall protocol risk, with implications for secure deployment and future standardization. Third, we provide a measurement-driven case study on MCP that formalizes the risk of missing mandatory validation /attestation for executable components as a falsifiable security claim by quantifying wrong-provider tool execution under multi-server composition across representative resolver policies. Collectively, our results highlight key design-induced risk surfaces and provide actionable guidance for secure deployment and future standardization of agent communication ecosystems. 

Keywords: Model Context Protocol (MCP), Agent2Agent Protocol (A2A), Agent Network Protocol (ANP), Agora protocol, Risk Assessment, AI agent Security, Threat modeling, Secure AI Integration.

2.1. Security Studies on MCP Communication Protocols 

In the last two years, researchers have approached security for the MCP from several angles, though none have yet delivered a fully formal threat model. Hou and colleagues [ 21 ] o ff er the first deep dive into MCP’s architecture and operation. They break the server life cycle into three stages, including creation, operation, and update, then walk through the kinds of problems that can arise at each step. The study assumes a semi-trusted environment in which MCP components behave as specified but considers the presence of malicious servers, tools, and installers in the broader ecosystem. The security model is an informal, risk-based one that analyzes threats in the server lifecycle without defining a formal adversary model, and the primary contribution is in identifying threats at the ecosystem level. Narajala et al. [ 22 ] adopt a di ff erent approach by treating all MCP components (client, server, and host) as untrusted and constructing a defense-in-depth security framework under a Zero-Trust assumption. By systematically cataloging attack vectors, they derive concrete controls for authentication, authorization, and continuous monitoring. While the proposed layered miti-gations and Zero-Trust design patterns provide useful guidance, the authors acknowledge that the recommendations have not been empirically evaluated nor enforced at the protocol level. Ehtesham et al. [ 8 ] present a taxonomy, comparative analysis, and roadmap for AI agent communication protocols, rather than 3proposing new security mechanisms or formally assessing attack surfaces. A key limitation is that security is treated descrip-tively and per-protocol, and not systematically threat-modeled, in terms of cross-protocol attack analysis, or through empirical validation in conditions of malicious multi-agent interaction. Another work examines MCP outside of a security context. Yang et al. [ 23 ] integrate MCP servers in IoT environments using edge devices and evaluate protocol performance in such settings, but provide little discussion of security. Conversely, few design-oriented studies focus on protecting integrity dur-ing installation by advocating cryptographic integrity checks, code-hash verification, manifest signing, and checksums to de-tect tampering with tools and servers [ 23 ], [ 24 ]. While these measures strengthen the installation phase, they do not address runtime attacks nor provide a comprehensive threat model for the MCP ecosystem. Taken together, these studies indicate that researchers have begun to identify risks and propose safeguards for MCP, ranging from lifecycle-based risk analyses to enterprise-oriented harden-ing frameworks. However, the field still lacks formal adversary models, empirical attack-based evaluations, and cross-protocol analysis, leaving substantial scope for more rigorous and sys-tematic security research on MCP. 

2.2. Security Studies on A2A Communication Protocols 

Google’s A2A protocol has become a prominent standard for coordinating multiple AI agents, but its security implica-tions remain an active research area. A case study by Duan and Lu [ 25 ] assesses the A2A protocol for edge-computing en-vironments, noting that edge-based multi-agent systems face heterogeneity, scalability, dynamicity, and resource constraints; their analysis shows that the A2A Agent Card mechanism helps address heterogeneity but lacks a standard schema to capture host-level resource information. Habler et al. [ 26 ] analyze the se-curity of A2A using the MAESTRO threat-modeling framework, with emphasis on vulnerabilities in Agent Card management, task-execution integrity, and authentication, and propose secure development practices. Nevertheless, the analysis is limited to application-level threats and does not address protocol-level threats inherent to A2A. Louck et al. [ 27 ] provide recommendations to enhance the A2A protocol for handling sensitive data in multi-agent work-flows. The authors propose protocol-level refinements; however, these remain largely conceptual and example-driven, without attack-based evaluation, formal security guarantees, or assess-ment of scalability and performance overhead. In addition, they identify critical weaknesses in A2A, including insu ffi cient token-lifetime controls, lack of strong customer authentication, and over-broad access scopes. Empirical prompt-injection tests in-dicate that the proposed refinements can reduce sensitive-data leakage while maintaining low latency. Beyond protocol analysis, a broader multi-agent security study by He et al. [ 28 ] shows that vulnerabilities in individual components can cascade through inter - agent communications, illustrating that rigorous threat modeling and benchmarking are necessary for trustworthy multi-agent systems. A2A has also been extended to specific domains: Duan et al. [ 29 ] propose an AI -Agent Communication Network (ACN) for 6G environments where security is treated as an inherent property of the underly-ing network, rather than being addressed at the protocol level; other works examine A2A’s use in edge-computing scenarios, focusing on scalability and heterogeneity rather than explicit threat analysis. In summary, these studies underscore that while A2A pro-vides a foundational communication framework, robust security requires formal threat models, cryptographic enhancements, and empirical validation. 

2.3. Other Protocols and Related Studies 

In addition to MCP and A2A, two other protocols, ANP [ 37 ]and Agora [ 38 ], are frequently discussed in the emerging litera-ture on agent communication. These protocols are considered in this survey because they adopt di ff erent architectural assump-tions and interaction models, which introduce distinct security challenges. However, compared to MCP and A2A, security and privacy analyses for ANP and Agora remain relatively limited and largely design-oriented. Existing research on Agora primarily emphasizes commu-nication e ffi ciency, scalability, and flexible coordination among large numbers of LLM-driven agents [ 38 ]. The design assumes a cooperative and non-adversarial environment and does not define an explicit threat model. Consequently, critical security aspects such as agent authentication, authorization, resistance to malicious agents, protocol abuse, and data exfiltration are not systematically examined. Similarly, ANP is primarily speci-fied through architectural designs and technical specifications that emphasize decentralized identity, encrypted communica-tion, and interoperability [ 37 ]. While these features suggest built-in security considerations, existing work does not provide formal adversary models, protocol-level attack analyses, or em-pirical validation under malicious multi-agent conditions. Over-all, studies on ANP and Agora currently lack rigorous security assessments, formal threat modeling, and experimental evalua-tion under adversarial conditions. This gap further motivates the need for protocol-centric and ecosystem-level security analysis across heterogeneous agent communication standards. Beyond MCP, A2A, ANP, and Agora, existing studies are fragmented and tend to focus on individual components or iso-lated interaction patterns, rather than o ff ering a holistic security and privacy perspective on the agent communication protocol ecosystem. Kong et al. [ 30 ] provide a survey for LLM - driven agent communication; they introduce a three - class taxonomy, analyse security risks at each communication layer, and per-form experiments using MCP and A2A to illustrate potential vulnerabilities, and o ff er defense countermeasures. Zhang et al. [31 ] review LLM-based multi-agent systems across capabilities, collaboration, architectures, communication, and applications. However, security is treated only at a high level, without formal threat models or protocol-level validation. 3.1. Historical Context and Evolution 

The evolution of agent communication protocols requires collaboration and scalability of AI systems. Early AI assistants (2020-2022) were limited to proprietary platforms and, while they o ff ered many advantages, had significant weaknesses, such as the absence of inter-system integration and redundant logins and authentication transactions. By 2023, open-source frameworks such as LangChain and AutoGPT had emerged, demonstrating the importance of stan-dard, secure, and consistent communication layers to support tool invocation, context sharing, and multi-agent collaboration. Since 2024, a new phase in the evolution of AI agent protocols has begun. Anthropic introduced MCP as the first open protocol to enable the connection of AI agents and external resources. At the same time, research groups announced the idea of Agora, which addresses the triad of agent communication (adaptability, effi ciency, and portability). In 2025, Google launched the A2A protocol, and ANP emerged to predict the internet of agents at a large scale. The combination of these protocols suggests a migration towards secure and interactive systems of agent collaboration. 

3.2. Model Context Protocol (MCP) 

In 2024, Anthropic introduced MCP as a new open standard protocol for connecting AI agents [ 40 ]. It is open-source and platform-agnostic, enabling agents to have a two-way connection with external tools and facilitating complex workflows. Figure 3 shows the benefit of using MCP in decreasing the complexity and increasing the scalability of AI agent ecosystems. MCP helps developers avoid fragmented integration and maintain a standard integrated connection to data sources. The MCP host, MCP client, and MCP server are the three main components of the MCP architecture that provide a smooth connection between data sources and AI applications. In a normal workflow, as depicted in Figure 4, the user sends a request prompt to the MCP client to evaluate the context, then the MCP server chooses the relevant tools and calls external APIs to obtain and process the data. The MCP host is an AI application that hosts the MCP client and o ff ers the environment for executing AI-based operations. It combines data and interactive features to facilitate seamless contact with external services and enhances connectivity with external MCP servers. The MCP client serves as a mediator that controls commu-nication between the MCP host and the MCP servers. It sends requests to MCP servers and asks about the functionalities that are available, then gets answers about the server’s capabilities that guarantee smooth communication with the external tools. Moreover, the MCP client handles alerts from MCP servers and manages requests and responses. The MCP server, which is responsible for managing and pro-viding AI models with the resources, has three phases: creation, operation, and update. Each phase defines activities for secure and e ffi cient functions [ 21 ]. The creation step is the first phase that registers and configures the server. Then, in the operation phase, the MCP server processes requests, invokes appropriate tools, handles multiple slash commands while preventing con-flicts, and enforces a sandbox mechanism to ensure an isolated, secure execution environment. In the last phase, the MCP server maintains security and adaptability by verifying post-update access permissions. The MCP server defines three core components that en-hance AI model operations, including Tools, Resources, and Prompts. Tools allow MCP servers to use a common supply-and-consumption model to automatically call external services and APIs. Resources facilitate data-driven decision-making and give AI models access to structured or unstructured datasets from local storage, databases, or cloud sources. Prompts are predefined templates or workflows for maximizing answers and guaranteeing consistency.

It is built on existing common standards, including Server-Sent Events (SSE), HTTP(S), and JSON-RPC, meaning it is easier to integrate with existing IT stacks and is designed to support enterprise-level authentication and authoriza-tion. A2A uses OAuth 2.0 [ 42 ] for mutual agent authentication and access to resources without sharing credentials; it also uses JSON Web Tokens (JWTs) [ 43 ] for compacting and signing to-kens, smoothing secure transmission of authentication between agents, and ensuring integrity and verification of access [27]. The A2A protocol provides structured and bidirectional com-munication between a client agent and a remote agent to dis-tribute task execution. Within this framework, the client agent formulates tasks and transmits them to the remote agent, then the remote agent works on the tasks to collect the correct infor-mation. Agents advertise their functional capabilities through a stan-dardized Agent Card in JSON format to enable the client agent to find the most relevant remote agent for a given task, thereby optimizing task allocation and execution. In addition, tasks are encapsulated within a well-defined protocol object that has a life cycle. The protocol supports both synchronous completion and asynchronous execution for long-running processes. In the latter case, periodic status updates maintain synchronization between agents. The task’s final output is formalized as an arti-fact, ensuring structured result handling. Agents can exchange contextual messages, intermediate responses, artifacts, and user instructions to keep alignment during task execution [ 44 ]. By this mechanism, A2A provides a flexible communication plat-form that supports interoperability, maintains execution context, and optimizes distributed agent collaboration. 3.5. Agent Network Protocol (ANP) 

ANP [ 37 ] [ 45 ] is an open standard for providing network interoperability between autonomous agents in heterogeneous environments. Stimulated by the development of the human communication networks towards the Internet, ANP imagines the IoA as a global, secure, and e ffi cient environment to col-laborate with billions of machine entities. The ANP design aims at eliminating data silos, facilitating connectivity between agents, and providing high e ffi ciency in machine-to-machine communication (M2M). ANP is based on three foundational principles; the first one is interconnectivity, which means the agents should be able to completely share the contextual information in an interchange method in an obstruction-free manner. The second principle is native interfaces, which means the agents may interact more readily by using APIs and uniform protocols rather than human-centric interfaces such as graphical interfaces and manually entered information. Last but not least is e ffi cient collaboration that agents can create and take part in ad hoc networks and role negotiation autonomously by the use of automatic organization and automatic negotiation approaches. The architecture of the protocol has three layers. The Iden-tity and Encrypted Communication layer integrates the W3C Decentralized Identifiers (DIDs) framework in order to support decentralized authentication, allowing trustless, end-to-end en-crypted communication between di ff erent agents across di ff erent platforms. The Meta-Protocol layer is meant to act as a protocol of protocols where agents may negotiate which communication standard (examples include Agora [ 38 ]) to apply to a certain interaction accordingly. Lastly, the Application Protocol Layer defines agent discovery mechanisms, descriptions of capabili-ties, and execution of tasks in domain-related situations [ 46 ]. Figure 7. demonstrates the layered architecture of ANP with its identity, meta-protocol, and application layers, along with their core components. The functional cycle starts with a local agent submitting a standardized search application to a standard discovery process to find a list of available agents. The agent would then access de-scription files about the capabilities of the discovered agents and interfaces and what is needed in order to authenticate itself. On this basis, the initiating agent builds and delivers authenticated requests and processes the responses to accomplish collaborative tasks. 4.1. Authentication and Access Control 4.1.1. Lack of authentication 

: The early version of MCP v1.0 did not have authentication mechanisms; hence, it was prone to impersonation and spoofing until a later update (MCP v1.2) added token-based authentication that guaranteed the integrity of data transmission and identity verification trustworthiness. 

4.1.2. Weak or limited access control 

: Role or Access Control List (ACL) granularity is desired since MCP lacks fine-grained permissions. Coarse permissions fail to add restrictions at the field, endpoint, or task level and expose systems to unpermitted access and privilege escalation. 10 4.1.3. Naming Collision & Impersonation 

In the creation stage of MCP, once a malicious entity regis-ters an MCP server with a name close to a known one (i.e., mcp-github vs github-mcp), it can impersonate clients and agents. MCP clients discover servers by simply reading their description and name, rather than cryptographic evidence. In decentralized, community-driven environments, there is no central registry to enforce naming rules. A malicious server misleads AI agents into invoking the fake server and gets access to sensitive data, executes unauthorized commands, and disrupts workflows. At this time, MCP operates locally, but in the future, in multitenant environments, the risk of name attack will increase. These challenges highlight the need for centralized management for name registration. Indeed, the lack of a namespace construct in MCP is a root cause that increases both collision likelihood and command-level ambiguity. 

4.1.4. Absence of limitations on token lifetime 

A2A uses OAuth 2.0 for authentication, but does not impose strict expiration durations of tokens for sensitive operations. Consequently, the leaked or intercepted tokens can be stored for hours or even days, allowing the attackers to reuse them to replay or to gain unauthorized access. The issue is even more critical in asynchronous long-lived workflows when the agents of an organization interact with each other. In the absence of cryptographically shortened lifetimes or single-use tokens, the protocol provides a wide attack window. This has been noted in other distributed systems involving similar distributed systems, in which revoked or expired tokens were accepted, and the need to closely associate token lifetimes with particular actions and time boundaries. 

4.1.5. Insu ffi ciently Granular Token Scopes 

Tokens in the A2A are typically coarse-grained, so they give agents more privileges than they need in their respective work. As an example, a token that was supposed to be used for a single payment might allow access to other unrelated resources acci-dentally. This coarse scoping leaves a vulnerability to privilege escalation, with all tokens being compromised, allowing attack-ers to expand their access to areas not originally referenced. Security-wise, this compromises the principle of least privilege and subjects sensitive workflows to exploitation. 

4.2. Supply Chain and Ecosystem Integrity 4.2.1. Installer Spoofing 

During the creation phase, attackers are able to publish al-tered installers or one-click setup programs (e.g., mcp-get, mcp-installer) that install malware or backdoors. Many users, to do so conveniently, have a tendency to use non-o ffi cial community installers, which do not verify packages or signatures. Attackers can have long-term access, exfiltrate credentials, or reconfigure servers by means of malicious installers. MCP is a much more community-driven system; that means that the attack surface is massive and largely uncontrolled. 

4.2.2. Code Injection and Backdoors 

MCP servers are often open-source and rely on community-maintained libraries. In addition, malicious or compromised dependencies introduce backdoors into MCP servers. In this regard, without rigorous dependency checks or reproducible builds, attackers can slip in code-level exploits. After an MCP server is compromised, it leaves behind a backdoor, which sur-vives updates and can silently steal data, increase privileges, or interfere with operations. Unlike Installer spoofing, which is related to the initial delivery /bootstrapping stage of the supply chain, this vector concerns the payload /persistence stage after installation. 

4.2.3. Tool Poisoning 

Tools within the MCP ecosystem, especially in the opera-tion phase, can have the same or misleadingly similar names. Besides, MCP enables agents to independently choose the tools according to the names and descriptions. In the case that a mali-cious tool is given a name or description in order to seem more pertinent, the client may prioritize it. The result is a toolflow hijacking in which attackers trick the MCP client into invoking their tool instead of the intended one, thereby possibly stealing sensitive data or executing malicious code. 

4.2.4. Rug Pulls 

Rug-pull attacks are a kind of risk in which, at first, adversar-ial tools or agents act appropriately to gain trust and be integrated into the workflows of critical operations. When the dependency has been established, the malicious party changes their behavior, either removing the desired functionality or adding some bad be-havior. MCP and A2A promote dynamic discovery and healthy relationships; therefore, rug pulls are a serious threat to integrity and dependability. Their malicious character lies in the fact that they are activated later, and when they are used, they can bypass the initial checks and use the developed trust relations. 

4.3. Operational Integrity and Reliability 4.3.1. Slash Command Overlap 

The flexible multi-tool environment promoted at MCP pro-vides significant extensibility, yet does not have powerful dis-ambiguation. Several tools define the same or similar slash commands (e.g., /delete), which threatened the system integrity during the operation phase. In this state, an attacker can insert an incompatible command and cause the MCP client to take unwanted actions (e.g., deleting logs rather than temporary files). This compromises operational reliability and may lead to loss of data. 

4.3.2. Sandbox Escape 

MCP is based on the local isolation or containerization oper-ation at the operation stage. In case the sandbox implementation contains unpatched vulnerabilities, e.g., system calls or runtime libraries, malicious tools may breach isolation. Indeed, attackers would be able to run arbitrary code on the host, get sensitive system data, or escalate privileges. This is a systemic risk in the enterprise deployment since MCP hosts generally have wide access to tools and resources. 11 4.3.3. Shadowing Attacks 

A less severe form of tool poisoning is the shadowing at-tack, in which malicious actors pretend to be legitimate tools or agents but shadow these agents during their execution. Using this method, the shadowing entity redirects tra ffi c and intercepts workflows, and finally replaces malicious results or modifies outputs. This attack exploits decentralized discovery and can silently corrupt a multi-agent workflow in MCP and A2A proto-cols. 

4.3.4. Post-Update Privilege Persistence 

Post-update privilege persistence is a vulnerability that is critical and occurs when outdated privileges or revoked priv-ileges are still valid after an MCP server update. This issue arises as the updates to privilege are not always replicated across all server instances or invalidated as part of the updating pro-cess (e.g., API key revocations, permission changes, or role updates). This may provide adversaries with an opportunity to abuse residual privileges to conduct malicious activities, steal sensitive resources, or cause a system malfunction. 

4.3.5. Re-deployment of Vulnerable Versions 

The probability of redeploying susceptible MCP versions is due to the community-based and decentralized character of the ecosystem. There is no o ffi cial package management infrastruc-ture or auditing authority to impose the use of secure versions. This leaves a dangerous window between the disclosure of vul-nerability and its adoption by a patch system, where attackers can still take advantage of vulnerabilities that are already known. In this condition, a malicious actor could deliberately attack or-ganizations that have older MCP servers to gain higher privileges or interfere with the server operations. 

4.3.6. Configuration Drift 

It is defined as the gradual build-up of unwanted configura-tion drift that goes against a secure configuration. This issue is particularly acute in MCP environments, where end-users local-ize a server or it is deployed in ecosystems that are community-driven. It is dangerous to individual local deployments, but it is even more severe with remote or cloud MCP services, like those provided by Cloudflare, where configuration errors can a ff ect more than one user or organization at a time. In a multi-tenant environment, one drift event may accidentally reveal sensitive re-sources, escalate privileges, or extend access to attackers across tenants. This section discusses the threats that are reported in the other literature as the weaknesses of AI agent protocols, and most of them refer to the MCP and A2A communication. Based on our searches, no reported security analysis has been pub-lished on ANP or Agora either. This is not a surprise: all four protocols are young and still at an initial stage of development and implementation, and the academic community has only just started to discuss their security aspects. As a result, the attacks reported so far are not comprehensive and do not represent the full range of threats posed by the architecture, trust assumptions, and interoperability features of these new ecosystems. Since the AI-agent protocols are new and are changing fast, it is vital to go beyond what is already being reported and ac-tively investigate structurally possible attack vectors before they become real in deployment. To address this problem, the next section provides further analysis and presents a collection of deduced, architecture-based, and anticipatory threats that are not yet o ffi cially reported in other literature. These attack vectors are reported in order to assist researchers in preempting vul-nerabilities and finding ways to harden protocol specifications and directing systematic security reviews as these ecosystems evolve. 

5. Potential Threats derived from Protocol Design and Ar-chitecture 

While the Threat Model section discussed security threats specifically derived from the literature, this section covers the risks based on the structural and operational characteristics of the protocols themselves. These threats do not appear explicitly in previous literature but are logical deductions in the design deci-sions, communication patterns, and trust assumptions shaped in MCP, A2A, Agora, and ANP. By separating them from literature-derived threats, we emphasize the novelty of this analysis and the speculative quality of it: they are the possible threats that should be taken into account in future work, although they are not yet o ffi cially recognized or even confirmed and validated in the literature. These threats are thus considered as analytical pro-jections and not as established vulnerabilities and are presented in order to inform the future protocol design, assessment, and empirical validation. This section groups threats by their functional nature and point of impact, similar to the previous section. In functional do-main alignment, each group represents a security domain, such as Authentication & Access Control, Supply Chain Integrity, and Operational Reliability. From the threat scope and target granu-larity point of view, each subcategory captures what aspect of the system is directly attacked. Indeed, authentication-based threats target identity and trust establishment, and supply chain–related threats target ecosystem provenance and update integrity. In this way, operational integrity threats target runtime consistency and availability. The taxonomy is shown in Figure 9.

This risk is particularly significant in multi-tenant or enterprise systems, where repeating privileged commands can compromise data integrity. In the case of Agora, messages consist of protocolHash, protocolSources, and body, and the lack of a standard freshness mechanism encourages attackers to monitor or eavesdrop on tra ffi c and reuse a request that was previously accepted in another session. A2A has been designed to work with asynchronous and long-running workflows. 5.1.2. Token Scope Escalation 

In A2A, the agents typically transmit credentials as tokens that they use to access resources or workflows. To be e ff ective, these tokens can encompass several tasks or grant privileges. On the protocol layer, the scoping of A2A is not strictly enforced; indeed, it is left to the identity and authorization infrastructure underneath to implement fine-grained scoping. It poses a struc-tural risk: an attacker who compromises a token that is supposed to grant access to a limited workflow also has access to ex-panded privileges, so they can increase the scope of their control after obtaining it. As an example, a token that will be used in document retrieval can also allow document modification and thus allow artifact tampering. In the absence of a protocol-level mechanism to standardize the design of least-privilege tokens, scope escalation is a latent threat amongst implementations. 

5.1.3. Privilege Escalation 

Privilege escalation is the expansion of permissions that are not meant to be allowed, but an adversary can get them through the exploitation of weak access control or configuration [ 32 ]. This is possible in the case of MCP when tools or servers receive coarse-grained permissions, but without a strict implementation of ACLs, malicious tools can execute operations outside of their approved scope. Moreover, the problem of post-update privilege persistence makes MCP more vulnerable since it allows revoked keys to be in use. In A2A, the escalation of privileges stems not from the design of the token scope but from weaknesses in authorization at the workflow level, such as granting control over artifacts or downstream tasks to agents without proper mutual validation. The issue of escalation appears in the context of ANP, where the agents discuss meta-protocols without enforced policies. Rivals can use poor negotiation results to take up positions of greater responsibility. In Agora, fallback to natural-language routines makes protocol semantics open to adversarial manipulation. As a result, it can persuade peers to accept more permissive capabilities. 

5.1.4. Identity Forgery and Impersonation 

Identity forgery is a type of attack in which attackers reg-ister or advertise a fake identity to impersonate a legitimate server, tool, or agent [ 32 ]. In MCP, the tool and server discov-ery relies on free-text names and descriptions without a strong cryptographic binding. Thereby, a malicious actor can mimic a legitimate tool name and mislead clients into invoking the endpoints that are under the control of attackers. In A2A, this threat is related to the federated model of agent cards and JWT-based authentication. So, if an agent card is forged, attackers can impersonate legitimate agents in the entire organizational domain. ANP uses DIDs, but the lack of a centralized revocation and trust anchors provides a risk window for adversaries to mint pseudonymous agents at scale and expand the impersonation across domains. In Agora, agents negotiate PDs in natural lan-guage. So, adversarial agents can represent fake capabilities or inject forged PDs to impersonate valid roles. Generally, in all the protocols, identity forgery decreases the trust in discovery and leads to hijacked workflows and unauthorized access. 

5.1.5. Sybil Attacks 

In the Sybil attack, adversaries create a massive number of fake identities to get disproportionate influence [ 32 ]. Sybil at-tacks destroy trust, distort discovery, and can deny legitimate agents service. In MCP, if attackers register many malicious tools or servers, they can deceive clients and provide di ffi culty in identifying valid entities. In A2A, the attacker can introduce a large number of pseudo-agents using improperly validated or compromised agent cards; therefore, they can destabilize task assignment or even saturate the coordination mechanisms. In ANP, DIDs can not provide any protection against Sybil genera-tion. It is necessary to have a reputation or staking mechanism; otherwise, adversaries may participate with many pseudonyms and disrupt consensus-driven collaboration. 

5.1.6. Cross-Vendor Trust Boundary Exploitation 

A special feature of A2A is that it is capable of facilitating the collaboration of agents at the inter-organizational and inter-vendor level. Although this allows interoperability, it increases the boundary of trust. Assuming that one of the participating organizations has a weaker validation of JWTs or poor manage-ment of its key distribution, attackers can use that weak point to compromise agents or escalate privileges within other organiza-tions. Since A2A provides a two-way communication scheme where agents promote capabilities through standardized Agent Cards, a compromised trust anchor in one area can spread out and damage the integrity of the whole workflow. A2A, at the protocol level, is based on federated trust but does not spec-ify governance of inter-organizational identity assurance and exposes the boundary between domains to exploitation.

The impact of these kinds of compromises is that, because MCP servers dynamically load tools and resources at runtime, such compromises may spread across ecosystems with minimal consideration. MCP does not require any central auditing or code-signing requirements, and this enables attacks to exploit the trust of community-provided components. 5.2.2. PD Spoofing and Repository Poisoning 

Since PDs in Agora are fetched by URIs provided by peers and only integrity is verified through a hash, an adversary with the ability to manipulate where a PD gets fetched may either attempt substitution of the PD (e.g., by providing a di ff erent PD at the expected URI) or rollback (point to a weaker previously fetched PD whose hash remains known to the adversary). The absence of authenticity, including signatures, trust anchors, or governance on the provenance of PD, can result in agents that may end up executing routines that embody an attacker’s seman-tics while appearing syntactically valid. The threat is increased by decentralized storage and lack of a central registry; hash helps to detect bit-level manipulation of the fetched file, but does not help to prevent an attacker from directing you to which PD /hash to trust in the first place. 

5.2.3. Protocol Fragmentation Risk 

One of the important risks that arises due to the rapid ex-pansion of AI agent protocols is protocol fragmentation. In the case of the proliferation of overlapping protocols, it can result in the formation of redundant ecosystems with uncertain maturity and control. Due to the lack of convergence, an adversary is able to result in a downgrade of less secure or poorly maintained implementations. Riding on such conditions, an attacker will be able to reduce the agent system to a weak or less secure implementation. For example, an attacker can redirect tra ffi cto an old MCP extension that is not properly patched in an en-terprise environment running both MCP and A2A. By doing this, the attacker is able to force a downgrade to the system that compromising system integrity and exposing the system to vulnerabilities that have already been fixed. To address this risk, stronger management policies and version alignment are necessary. 

5.2.4. Version Rollback 

In Agora, the integrity of that particular document is guar-anteed by the identification of PDs by hash; however, it does not determine which version is authoritative in the system. The adversaries have the ability to propagate older PD hashes whose schemas allow broader interpretations or weaker constraints. The absence of a policy on the minimum acceptable versions of the PD can cause subdivision of agents between variants of the protocol, e ff ectively spoiling protocol hardening and permitting a scale of downgrade attacks. 

5.2.5. Onboarding Exploitation 

The first step in the protocol ecosystem is agent onboarding, where tools, agents, or servers are registered, and this needs strong verification, attestation, and governance. In the absence of strong verification and validation mechanisms, this stage be-comes a serious and vulnerable weak point in the environment, and adversaries can introduce malicious entities before any secu-rity controls apply. This threat is di ff erent from impersonation or tool poisoning because it targets the entry point of the ecosystem: as soon as a malicious entity enters the system, subsequent trust assumptions across all protocols are broken. Generally, the onboarding process in MCP is community-driven, and thousands of tools are presented in open catalogs without cryptographic provenance. This opens an entry for unverified code. In A2A, agent cards are used in the onboarding process, and adversaries embed malicious agents into workflows. In ANP, the IDs are decentralized, which worsens the risk in the onboarding stage. Any entity is able to mint a DID and promote itself as an agent, which is why malicious agents can saturate the ecosystem with low-quality or spoofed agents. In Agora, 15 onboarding exploitation is expressed in PD repositories, and PDs are published without an authenticity guarantee. Malicious actors are able to post polluted or vague PDs, which lead agents to unsafe negotiations. In all the protocols, weak onboarding means weak foundations: when a malicious entity is already within the system, the following trust assumptions are a ff ected. 

5.3. Operational Integrity & Reliability 5.3.1. Cross-Protocol Interaction Risks 

The MCP is designed to be used alongside other standards and ecosystems, e.g., OAuth when authenticating, REST APIs to invoke tools, or A2A protocols to communicate between agents. Although this interoperability is more flexible, there is a risk when the security assumptions of di ff erent protocols are not consistent. As an example, when OAuth tokens are not scoped or expired on a regular basis, both in MCP and external services, attackers can use those di ff erences to gain privileges. Likewise, REST APIs, which are incorporated in MCP workflows, can have less strict rules of validation than MCP itself and provide room to create injection-based exploits or misconfigured exploits. Cross-protocol attacks are also highly dangerous since they take advantage of the interfaces between systems instead of vulnerabilities of one protocol. Because MCP is explicitly in support of modularity and integration, its attack surface is not limited to its own design but to the weakest point between standards interconnected by MCP. 

5.3.2. Cross-protocol confusion attacks 

This risk arises from poor interoperability between MCP, A2A, Agora, and ANP. Because these protocols have di ff erent methods for trust assumptions (tokens in MCP, OAuth2 in A2A, DIDs in ANP, and PDs in Agora), cross-protocol interactions have potential for mismatched security guarantees. Attackers can misuse these di ff erences and relay messages between protocols, bypassing the tighter checks in one ecosystem because of weaker checks in another. For better clarification, consider that a long-lived A2A token can be reused in an ANP setting where no DID-based revocation is implemented, and allows privilege escalation. In the absence of standardization of cross-protocol validation, interoperability is not only a feature but also a potential attack area. 

5.3.3. Context Explosion and Resource Exhaustion 

The availability and reliability of agentic workflows in A2A and MCP can be compromised through unrestricted output of tools or complex parameter spaces. The output of an adversarial tool in MCP or A2A-mediated chains can be excessively large and overwhelm downstream agents. Since the protocol has a small payload size, the nest level and rate are not applied equally; context explosion is a denial-of-service attack that leads to di ffi culty in di ff erentiating from normal long-term computing. 

5.3.4. Intent Deception 

Intent deception is the misrepresentation of the intentions or the ability of an adversary with the aim of achieving unintended influence [32]. In MCP, the tools used by malicious actors may promote deceptive descriptions, e.g., they may pretend to be log analyzers when stealing data. In A2A, the adversarial agents can release agent cards that depict seemingly innocent capabilities and behave maliciously during operation. Intent deception oc-curs in the context of automatic negotiation in which agents in ANP falsely represent their constraints or service descriptions to attempt to influence task distribution. And eventually, in Agora, intent deception occurs when adversaries present PDs that have concealed adversarial semantics in natural-language statements. 

5.3.5. Collusion and Free-Riding 

Collusion is a situation where a number of malicious agents join to manipulate results, and free-riding is a scenario where the opponents take advantage of the e ff orts of others without contributing [ 32 ]. In MCP, colluding occurs when various tools support each other in their misleading description in order to overcome discovery rankings. In A2A, malicious agents may create sub-networks of collaborating agents to make deviations in distributed workflows or increase malicious impacts, and free-riding agents receive results without contributing data. Collusion in ANP is one of the most menacing since the meta-protocol provides ad hoc group negotiation, which can be compromised by colluding opponents. Agora is a ff ected by collusion, where there is more than one adversarial agent, and they insert reinforc-ing PDs to crowd out legal documents. These threats undermine equity and e ff ectiveness in the cooperation of multiple agents. 

5.3.6. Semantic Drift Exploitation 

Semantic drift is defined as the progressive drift towards di ff erences in the interpretation of terms, embeddings, or on-tologies by agents. In a distributed ecosystem, adversaries are able to choose this di ff erence by injecting malicious concepts that are seen in a di ff erent way and that corrupt the coordination. In MCP, a malicious tool can produce outputs with ambiguous schema fields or overloaded names (e.g., “ /delete”) and deceive some clients into performing malicious actions while others treat them as harmless actions. In A2A, the adversaries may take advantage of the disparities in the definition of capability description in the Agent Cards by federated organizations. A "verifyTransaction" skill can imply compliance checking in one place and financial authorization in another, allowing attack-ers to use it to escalate privileges. Semantic drift is especially severe in ANP since it negotiates with meta-protocols. Weak schema definitions can be pushed by malicious actors during negotiation that force some agents to accept permissive interpre-tations that lead to critical data leakage. In the case of Agora, semantic drift may be experienced through the application of natural-language fallback to mediate PDs. The attackers are able to seed ambiguous or malicious fragments of language, which will be interpreted di ff erently by various agents, leading to a cascading failure or security breach. Risk management in AI agent communication protocols is an important and ongoing process that examines various aspects of security, including identifying, assessing, and mitigating risks. The main goal of this assessment is to reduce security risks to a manageable level, which is possible by identifying the vul-nerabilities that are most likely and impactful. This assessment should examine the entire life cycle of AI agent communication protocols to provide su ffi cient insight into the risks at each stage of the life cycle. 

6.1. Assessment Methodology 

To e ff ectively assess risks, we need to understand the likeli-hood of a vulnerability being exploited, as well as the impact of the exploitation on the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of agent interactions. In the context of multi-agent systems and protocol interoperability, "likelihood" refers to the probability of a protocol flaw, weak authentication scheme, or misconfigured token being exploited. "Impact" refers to the potential severity of the resulting damage, for example, risks such as unauthorized access to data, disruption of service, privilege escalation, or agent impersonation. Following the NIST SP 800-30 [ 49 ] risk assessment method-ology and ISO /IEC 27005:2022 [ 50 ], this study adopts the stan-dard risk formula: 

R = L × I (1) where R is the overall risk, L is the probability of a threatening event, and I is defined as the magnitude of impact. The formula is popular in cybersecurity evaluation systems (e.g., [ 51 ]), which has been applied here to evaluate risks in the communication protocols of AI agents. In this analysis, we employed a qualitative risk assessment approach as the rationale of NIST SP 800-30. All the variables will be rated on a three-level scale, where Low will be rated for rare frequency or minor impact, Medium will be rated for partial and moderate level, and High will be rated for frequent and severe e ff ects. This method is analytical and enables qual-itative reasoning across protocols. In order to achieve consis-tency between various architectures of AI agent communication protocols, qualitative risk levels (high, medium, low) are deter-mined based on protocol-independent reference metrics and not protocol-dependent baselines. In particular, likelihood ratings are conditional on observable factors, like level of exposure, ease of exploitation, and propagation potential, whereas impact ratings are conditional on the measure of loss of control, blast radius across agents, and recovery complexity. These standard metrics are mapped onto the architectural mechanisms of each protocol (e.g., agent-tool invocation, agent-agent negotiation, or policy-based delegation), and therefore, a given qualitative level would have similar security implications across protocols and lifecycle phases. Consequently, a medium rating in one proto-col, like A2A, would have the identical severity of a common interpretation of an MCP of the same severity, even where the underlying attack vectors di ff er. Since MCP, A2A, Agora, and ANP are still newly proposed protocols with limited public deployments, our evaluation is not based on empirical incident data and reported vulnerabilities. Instead, the ratings of impact and likelihood are based on a sys-tematic, architecturally based analysis derived from protocol specifications, documented design decisions, and observable security control placement. For each vulnerability, we evalu-ate the intrinsic exploitability, which is done by assessing the requirement of a security control as mandatory, optional, or non-existent, and the scope and propagation capability allowed by the communication and trust model of the protocol. This method is aligned with design-time risk assessment guidelines in NIST SP 800-30 and ISO /IEC 27005, where risk is estimated in situa-tions of uncertainty based on threat model, expert judgment, and scenario analysis, rather than attack frequency in the past. Based on NIST SP 800-30, risk assessment involves five tasks, including (1) identifying threat sources, (2) identifying vulnerabilities that may result from those threat sources and events, (3) determining the likelihood of their occurrence, (4) determining the magnitude of impact for each vulnerability, and finally (5) assessing risk values for the identified threats. In the following, each task will be described. 

6.1.1. Task 1- Identifying threat sources 

The goal is to identify all potential threat sources and events that could compromise the security of AI agent protocol ecosys-tems. A threat source can be an actor, process, or environmental condition with the potential to exploit a vulnerability that could result in loss of confidentiality, integrity, or availability. We have described the sources of threats on an abstract basis, but not on a basis of particular adversary personas (e.g., script kids or state-sponsored actors). This decision is explained by the fact that we intend to analyze the protocol-level exposure and exploitable nature regardless of the sophistication of the attacker. Due to this reason, we make assumptions of a capable adversary who has access to the network in accordance with the planned deployment of the protocol. We then evaluate the protocol de-sign decisions that can be exploited on the assumption. This abstraction is in line with the design-time risk assessment prac-tices described in NIST SP 800-30, where threat capability may be fixed to perform in structural vulnerability analysis. The origin of threats is classified in four groups that are adopted from NIST SP 800-30 but extended for the AI agent ecosystem: 1. Malicious Human Actors: persons or groups who try for unauthorized access or manipulation. 2. Compromised or Malicious Agents: Legitimate agents or tools that have been hacked and used to perform unautho-rized actions. 3. Supply-Chain and Infrastructure Attackers: Adversaries misusing external components that the protocol depends on. 4. Accidental and Operational Sources (Non-Malicious): 

• Configuration Errors: human mistakes during de-ployment, update, or policy definition. 17 • Software Defects: logical errors or implementation bugs that weaken enforcement of protocol guaran-tees. 

• Operational Failures: unintended disruptions such as version skew, dependency drift, or partial system outages. 

6.1.2. Task 2- Identifying vulnerabilities 

The goal of this task is to determine the specific technical vulnerabilities that are made by the threat sources determined in the previous task. In this study, vulnerability is a description of a non-existent, weak, or unenforced security control, on the protocol design or deployment level, that facilitates a threat event when it is exploited. These vulnerabilities exist at the design-level or operational-level of protocols and should be explicitly mapped with lifecycle stages to capture and assess risks in each stage of the protocol ecosystem. The vulnerabilities addressed here were chosen based on them being (i) directly due to protocol specifications or docu-mented design decisions and (ii) controlling risk exposure in at least one stage of the life cycle of the di ff erent AI agent commu-nication protocols. To simplify and make the list consistent, the vulnerabilities are categorized based on the stage of the lifecycle they are exposed to: 

• Creation and Configuration Stage: 

– Weak or absent identity verification mechanisms, including shared or static credentials. 

– Lack of integrity protection for registration artifacts (e.g., unsigned or unverifiable registration files). 

– Insu ffi cient namespace isolation, enabling imperson-ation or naming collisions among agents or tools. 

– Absence of baseline security policy or governance constraints during onboarding. 

• Operation Stage: 

– Lack of mandatory validation or attestation for ex-ecutable components, enabling the introduction of unauthorized or untrusted executable components. 

– Insu ffi cient control over data exchange, leading to context leakage or unauthorized information flow. 

– Inadequate enforcement of least-privilege principles, allowing privilege escalation via persistent or reused tokens. 

– Missing rate-limiting, quota enforcement, or back-pressure mechanisms, enabling resource exhaustion or denial-of-service conditions. 

• Update and Maintenance Stage: 

– Failure to revoke or reissue credentials after updates, resulting in residual privileges. 

– Absence of rollback protection or version pinning, enabling downgrade to vulnerable protocol states. 

– Lack of authentication or integrity verification for maintenance packages or updates. 

– Uncontrolled transitive dependency evolution, lead-ing to configuration drift or inconsistent security guarantees. 

6.1.3. Task 3- Determining the likelihood of occurrence 

This task aims at determining the probability that the vul-nerabilities will be used by the threat sources. The intrinsic exploitability and environmental exposure of each protocol in the lifecycle stages are investigated for likelihood estimation. Intrinsic exploitability reflects the design flaws in the protocol, e.g., not expiring tokens and failure to sandbox. On the other hand, environmental exposure shows operational context, e.g., APIs’ openness and monitoring procedures. Likelihood ratings represent inherent exploitability at the protocol level within the standard conditions of deployment. This method is compatible with the NIST SP 800-30 qualitative risks framework that the likelihood is the combination of both design and deployment context. 6.2.2. Stage 2- Operation 

The operation phase is where agents are communicating with each other, calling routines, and performing actions. In this stage, the vulnerabilities are no longer the configuration weaknesses but the dynamic interaction threats, i.e., unsafe data exchanges and logic manipulation, reuse of tokens, or denial-of-service. As this stage includes continuous communication and real-time decision-making, the vulnerabilities in runtime isolation, en-cryption, or authorization directly impact agent ecosystems. In order to measure the protocol’s robustness at any given point in the operational process, protocols are tested at four points of vulnerability, such as malicious or poisoned tools in opera-tion, uncontrolled data exchange, privilege escalation through token persistence, and resource exhaustion /DoS. This discussion demonstrates that the architectural decisions influence runtime security exposure in agent networks. In the operation stage, agents actively run tasks, share con-text, negotiate workflow, and, on top of them, rely on long-lived credentials. Because of the nature of this stage, the four pro-tocols have a high level of security exposure. In the previous stage, some roots of trust are established, but in this stage, oper-ational risk is introduced by runtime behaviors, such as dynamic message processing, privilege propagation, or coordination be-tween di ff erent agents. The assessment results of this phase, summarized in Tables 12, 13, and 14, show that among the vul-nerabilities assessed, MCP and Agora have a high overall risk, and this high risk is due to the lack of runtime code integrity enforcement in MCP and dynamic negotiation based on PD in Agora. Both of these vulnerabilities allow the systemic dissem-ination of poisoned logic or leaked content. A2A presents a moderate risk across all vectors because OAuth2 /JWT reduces exploitability and message-level mediation limits impact, but the downside of this protocol at this stage is that it does not provide any guarantees for semantic validation or strict token lifetime management. ANP exhibits mixed behavior; on the one hand, it has strong DID-based authentication, but on the other hand, the multi-layered dependencies cause the impact to increase dra-matically once an attack happens, especially in cases such as privilege escalation and poisoned dependency chains. Overall, the results obtained for the operation phase show that although runtime flexibility is essential for agent collaboration, it is the main security risk factor in all protocols.

MCP does not standardize a protocol-level mechanism that uniquely and cryptographically binds a tool’s identity to its provider when multiple MCP servers are concurrently avail-able. 8. Conclusion 

This paper presents the first systematic and focused review of the security of emerging AI agent communication protocols at a time when the pace of industry adoption is much faster than the security maturity of the ecosystem. By bringing together scattered initial results and developing a standard taxonomy of security threats, we show that these protocols share common structural weaknesses in authentication, supply chain integrity, operational reliability, and so on. Specific vulnerabilities for MCP and A2A have already been documented in the literature, ranging from weak authentication and command ambiguity to inadequate token management and insecure login. However, for Agora and ANP, no security analysis has been published to date. However, through architectural reasoning, we identify a diverse set of previously unreported attack vectors, such as semantic drift exploitation and protocol fragmentation, that are based on the nature of their decentralized trust models and natural language-based interoperability. In order to provide a more practical foundation for secure deployment, we proposed a risk assessment model of a lifecycle based on NIST and assessed threats in the di ff erent stages of protocols. Our findings indicate that these protocols are not only subjected to individual attacks but also have some systemic weaknesses due to undeveloped governance, inconsistent iden-tity assumptions, and cross-protocol collaboration. Importantly, we complement the qualitative analysis with a measurement-driven case study on MCP that instantiates an operation stage weakness: when tool identity is not cryptographically bound to provider identity, cross-server tool collisions can yield wrong-provider tool execution under realistic resolver policies. By quantifying this misbinding as a violation rate across controlled trials, we demonstrate how a design-level ambiguity can trans-late into a concrete, reproducible security failure. These risks are becoming more consequential as AI agents expand to enter-prise workflows, multi-vendor environments, and even financial operations. Our analysis tries to inform future researchers by 27 predicting both established and emerging threats in order to develop strong, security-sensitive agentic ecologies. 

9. Future Research Directions 

Even though this research o ff ers one of the first in-depth anal-yses of security in the emerging protocols of AI agents, a number of key research gaps are still available. The first gap is related to providing a special security layer for MCP. MCP is the only pro-tocol that has been structured for AI agent-tool communication, but it su ff ers from the lack of a comprehensive security layer to protect it from many attacks. These are especially challenging for financial or safety-critical processes, where the invocation of the tool may cause a sensitive or irreversible activity. For our future work, we would like to implement a formally de-fined security extension to MCP that includes cryptographic identity anchoring, ephemeral access credentials, and verifiable permission scoping, making MCP suitable enough to be used in enterprise-grade and regulated settings. In addition to the security aspect, the other critical research gap is the lack of a privacy-based risk assessment for the emerg-ing AI agent ecosystem. The privacy threats that have not been explored yet include metadata leakage, exposure to multi-tenant contexts, propagation of sensitive data by cross-agents, and ne-gotiation logs, which are privacy-destroying. In our next work, we will provide a detailed privacy-risk assessment framework based on these protocols. Till now, all the research discussed individual protocol secu-rity threats, but in this work, we have demonstrated that MCP, A2A, ANP, and Agora use completely di ff erent trust assump-tions, authentication strategies, and validation strategies, and this creates a chance of confusion, downgrade, and relay-abuse attacks when they are combined. So, cross-protocol security standards and interoperability hardening are urgently required. The next round of research should involve the development of security profiles to be used in interoperability and uniformity of validation rules across protocols.
