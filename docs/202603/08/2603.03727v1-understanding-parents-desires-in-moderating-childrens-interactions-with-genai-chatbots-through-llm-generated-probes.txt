## Abstract 

This paper studies how parents want to moderate children’s inter-actions with Generative AI Chatbots, with the goal of informing the design of future GenAI parental control tools. We first used an LLM to generate synthetic Child–GenAI Chatbot interaction scenarios and worked with four parents to validate their realism. From this dataset, we carefully selected 12 diverse examples that evoked varying levels of concern and were rated the most realistic. Each example included a prompt and GenAI Chatbot response. We

presented these to parents (N=24) and asked whether they found them concerning, why, and how they would prefer to modify the responses and be informed. Our findings reveal three key insights: (1) parents express concern about interactions that current GenAI Chatbot parental controls neglect; (2) parents want fine-grained transparency and moderation at the conversation level; and (3) par-ents need personalized controls that adapt to their desired strategies and children’s ages.

## 1 Introduction 

Despite being in its early stages, GenAI Chatbot technology has already drawn scrutiny from parents. Lawsuits filed against GenAI Chatbot developers cite concerns that these systems can isolate chil-dren from their families and communities, undermine parental au-thority, denigrate cultural or religious values, and obstruct parents’ efforts to manage online activity and ensure children’s safety [ 3,76 , 84 ]. These emerging risks highlight the urgent need to design parental control mechanisms suited to the distinctive properties of GenAI Chatbots. Prior research in child-computer interaction has extensively studied parental control in the contexts of smartphones [ 50 , 61 ], web browsing [ 4 , 94 ], and social media [ 15 , 26 , 88 , 102 ]. This re-search has produced tools such as content filters [60], screen time limits [ 42 , 47 ], and activity monitoring systems [ 8, 34 ]. However, GenAI Chatbots pose fundamentally different challenges. Unlike conventional media, GenAI Chatbot interactions are open-ended and their outputs are not tied to fixed resources such as websites, apps, or videos. Moreover, GenAI Chatbots give children faster access to individualized responses than social media or online fo-rums: children can consult a GenAI Chatbot instantly and receive tailored feedback, rather than sorting through many public posts. This combination of speed and specificity may amplify risks by making GenAI Chatbots a highly convenient default source of ad-vice, increasing the likelihood that children become dependent on these tools [ 14 , 39 ]. Recent work has enumerated parents’ concerns surrounding child–GenAI Chatbot interactions [ 106 ]. However, we have little knowledge regarding how parents want to moderate these interactions, given their concerns. In this paper, we conducted IRB-approved interviews with par-ents (N=24) of children aged 6-18 to understand how they want to moderate their children’s interactions with GenAI Chatbots (Fig-ure 1). We first used an LLM to generate synthetic Child–GenAI Chatbot interaction scenarios [ 6, 83 ] using a multi-agent simulation framework [ 13 , 49 , 73 ], and consulted with four parents to filter them. We retained 12 realistic and seemingly benign scenarios that raised some parents’ concerns. Each example included a prompt and GenAI Chatbot response. We then presented these scenarios to par-ents (N=24) in semi-structured interviews and asked about whether they found them concerning, the factors that triggered their con-cern, and their desired moderation and transparency-related inter-actions. For example, Figure 1 illustrates a scenario that appears helpful on the surface but subtly undermines a parent’s approach to safety, which parents described as inappropriate. Lastly, we con-ducted thematic analysis of interview transcripts to group parents’ low-level comments into high-level takeaways. Our study allows us to answer the following three research ques-tions: 

RQ1: What are the factors that trigger parents’ concern in Child-GenAI Chatbot interactions? We found that parents iden-tified concern factors that could be organized into two main groups: (1) questionable GenAI Chatbot responses (Figure 1 ➂) and (2) ques-tionable child prompts (Figure 1 ➃). Within the questionable GenAI Chatbot responses group, most parents identified the concerning factor that the system fails to grasp the underlying meaning of a child’s request, for example, by failing to clarify intent or by failing to address the root cause. Within the questionable child prompts group, most parents identified the concerning factor that a child could have harmful intentions. For example, when the child in Figure 1 asks "Can I climb on the roof to see the stars better?" it is unclear whether they understand the danger involved. Parents felt that these indicators hinted at a problematic child mindset and questioned whether the GenAI Chatbot could reliably safeguard the interaction. 

RQ2. How do parents want to moderate their children’s in-teractions with GenAI Chatbots? Parents described fine-grained moderation goals that extended beyond preventing harm. They wanted interactions to be age-appropriate, emotionally support-ive, and aligned with family values. They emphasized five desired moderation-related interactions (Figure 1 ➄): (1) refuse prompts and remove content while providing explanations, (2) investigate unclear intentions and emphasize important concepts, (3) correct potential misunderstandings, (4) respond with age-appropriate lan-guage and concepts, and (5) defer to human support. Overall, par-ents envision the GenAI Chatbot as a mediatory partner that sup-ports parents’ ongoing role as a moral and developmental guide. 

RQ3: What are parents’ transparency preferences around their child’s interactions with a GenAI Chatbot? Transparency refers to the level of openness and clarity parents sought regarding Child–GenAI Chatbot interactions. We found that parents’ desired transparency-related interactions could be described using two di-mensions: their desired level of (1) involvement (Figure 1. ➅), and (2) data content access (Figure 1. ➆). Most parents expressed a de-sire for the transparency-related interaction "Be Alerted to Flagged Activity". They wanted real-time notifications, such as a text alert, when their child asked about sensitive or potentially concerning topics.

## 2 Related Work 

We have organized related studies into four categories: the expand-ing roles of GenAI Chatbots in children’s lives (2.1), parental control tools in conventional domains (2.2), parental mediation strategies in conventional domains (2.3), and parental control and mediation for GenAI Chatbots (2.4). 

## 2.1 Expanding Roles of GenAI Chatbots in Children’s Lives 

The role of GenAI Chatbots is rapidly expanding in children’s lives. For example, GenAI Chatbot reading companions enhance chil-dren’s interest and support reading engagement [ 90 ]. Learning companions teach children and adapt to varying levels of parental involvement [ 44 ]. Prior work also solidified the use of GenAI Chat-bots for children’s emotional support [ 91 ]. A recent survey by Common Sense Media [ 21 ] found that over 50% of students aged 12–18 have used ChatGPT for schoolwork. Moreover, GenAI Chat-bot use is growing outside of these bounded tasks. A subsequent survey found that nearly three-quarters of U.S. teens have used GenAI Chatbots as "AI Companions", and half of them use them regularly [20]. In contrast, parents are unaware of their children’s usage [ 106 ]. For example, 64% of parents did not know that their teen had used GenAI [ 21 ]. As a consequence, early real-world incidents are emerg-ing. For example, recent U.S. lawsuits claimed Character.AI, a role-playing GenAI Chatbot, caused a wrongful death [ 84 ] and advised a 17 - year - old teen to kill his parents [ 3]. Another court filing sug-gested that ChatGPT validated harmful thoughts and provided technical details of suicide methods for use by a teen who died by suicide [43, 76]. This project is motivated by the widening gap between children’s rapid adoption of GenAI Chatbot and parents’ limited understand-ing. A lack of understanding of children’s usage prevents parents from imagining realistic use cases and expressing grounded con-cerns. We address these unique challenges by presenting parents with examples to contextualize Child–GenAI Chatbot interactions, in educational, companion, and other everyday settings. 

## 2.2 Parental Control Tools in Conventional Domains 

Prior research in Child-Computer Interaction has extensively stud-ied parental control tools for emerging technologies. Research on children’s web browsing has led to the development of content filters that block pages by category or keyword, and "safe search" modes. These help families manage children’s exposure to adult content, tracking, and unwanted contact [ 4, 94 ]. U.S. federal guide-lines paved the way for standardized age gates and parental consent requirements that are used by major social media platforms [ 27 ]. For example, Meta provides preteens and teens with age-specific accounts [ 62 ]. Parents rely on these built-in safety tools to protect children from harassment, body-image pressure, and data tracking as they use social media [ 15 , 26 , 88 , 102 ]. Furthermore, research on parental control in the context of smartphones has led to screen time controls that cap total device use, set app-level quotas, and schedule downtime [ 42 , 47 ]. Manufacturers have built activity-monitoring systems into their devices that report and restrict app usage and in-stallations. Examples of this include Apple’s Family Sharing/Screen Time and Google Family Link [ 8, 34 ]. Parents use these systems to address concerns of device overuse, distraction, and sleep inter-ference [ 50 , 61 ]. However, children have been found to regularly bypass similar restrictions [ 46 , 70 ], and children use these technolo-gies from incredibly young ages [ 11 ]. Moreover, these measures may not adequately protect children’s privacy [ 58 ], and there are calls for more robust age-assurance [46]. GenAI Chatbots pose fundamentally different challenges for parental control tools than conventional technologies. GenAI Chat-bots integrate a broad range of functions (information search, cre-ative writing, emotional support, and more) into a single, stochastic conversational interface. GenAI Chatbots dynamically adapt to a child’s unfolding conversation, and co-construct private, one-to-one interactions. These new challenges motivate us to investigate the factors that concern parents and the moderation and transparency-related interactions they desire surrounding children’s GenAI Chat-bot use, with the aim of understanding where they may lie in relation to conventional parental mediation tools. 

## 2.3 Parental Mediation Strategies in Conventional Domains 

Parental mediation "encompasses the strategies adopted by parents to regulate, discuss, and monitor their children’s media use" [ 52 ,p. 201], with the aim of balancing children’s participation with technology and its corresponding risks [ 53 , 56 ]. Prior work dis-tinguishes four parental mediation styles for children’s internet use. In "monitoring" (1), parents review what the child has done online after the fact (e.g., browsing history, contacts, logs) to track their online behavior. In "restrictive content mediation" (2), parents set and enforce rules about which sites, apps, or content the child may access and how long they may be online. In "active safety mediation" (3), parents talk with children about online risks and safety, explaining dangers and offering guidance while or before the child goes online. In "restrictive technical mediation" (4), parents use technical tools—such as filters, blocking software, or built-in parental controls—to automatically enforce limits on access, con-tacts, or screen time. [ 93 ]. Parents’ use of mediation styles is shaped by demographics, children’s online skills, and parents’ perceptions of online risks and benefits [67, 68]. Parents implement a variety of successful mediation strategies on a spectrum that ranges from maintaining full control over children’s engagement to supporting children’s independent navigation of online risks [ 1, 32 , 57 , 88 ]. However, some mediation approaches appear more effective than others at allowing parents to actualize "healthy" children’s technology use [ 59 , 66 ]. For example, prior work demonstrates that over-reliance on restrictions can impede CHI ’26, April 13–17, 2026, Barcelona, Spain Driscoll and Chen, et al. 

children’s development of self-regulatory skills, leaving children less prepared to navigate digital risks as they grow older. Successful mediation approaches tend to be child-centered and actively help young people learn how to self-regulate their behavior [23, 31, 37, 103 ]. This means teaching children self-monitoring, impulse control, and risk-coping skills to help them become resilient technology users, regardless of their age [103]. Findings that “parental mediation may vary because of the com-plexity of the situation and the values at stake” [ 52 , p. 202] suggest that the mediation styles parents employ surrounding GenAI Chat-bots may differ from those for conventional technologies. Parents’ desired GenAI Chatbot–mediation may involve moderation strate-gies that implement "restrictive content mediation" or "restrictive technical mediation," as well as transparency approaches that im-plement "active safety mediation" or "monitoring". This motivates us to systematically characterize how parents may wish to mediate child–GenAI Chatbot interactions. We envision designers enriching parents’ configurable mediation toolkits [ 68 ] in the GenAI Chat-bot setting by actualizing parents’ desires with mediation-assistive tools, including parental controls. 

## 2.4 Parental Control and Mediation for GenAI Chatbots 

.GenAI Chatbot providers are adding parental controls tools to address the unique challenges of children’s GenAI Chatbot use. These are mainly coarse-grained, determining whether, when, and where a child can use AI [ 33 ]. For example, Google Family Link lets guardians turn Gemini access on or off, sets a minimum age of 13+, and requires parental consent [ 35 , 36 ]. Apple’s Screen Time allows parents to block Apple Intelligence features and the ChatGPT extension [ 9, 10 ], Microsoft Family Safety lets parents block Copilot as an app or in search results [ 63 , 64 ], and Amazon Alexa+ Kids allows parents to set and monitor content and time restrictions [ 5]. Current GenAI Chatbot parental control tools are likely not suf-ficient to ensure children’s safety because these systems still pose major risks. Once a child gains access to these platforms, they re-ceive the standard GenAI Chatbot available to all users, despite guidance that suggests differentiating children’s versions of AI tools [ 25 , 97 ]. An Associated Press investigation and subsequent Center for Countering Digital Hate investigation revealed that ChatGPT produced highly specific and personalized guidance on drug use, extreme dieting, and self-harm strategies [ 17 , 69 ]. Recent work also highlights that these GenAI Chatbot parental control tools fall short of child-centered expectations [ 17 , 48 , 94 ] and often produce tensions when they prioritize surveillance over develop-mental support [ 55 ]. Parents were found to be concerned about age-inappropriate content, hallucinations, and children’s sharing of privacy-sensitive information [ 106 ]. Guidance to GenAI Chat-bot developers also suggested that children could misunderstand the artificial nature of GenAI Chatbot or use these tools to enable concerning behavior [25]. Parents want current GenAI Chatbot parental controls to be im-proved with features that enable timely mediation and shared un-derstanding [ 106 ]. A proposed solution is "Algorithmic parenting", using AI algorithms to support parental practices. This includes "automatically detecting when a child’s online behavior indicates that their well-being might be compromised", so a system can act accordingly [ 39 ]. A complementary approach proposes increased transparency [ 40 ]. Improving visibility into children’s technology use is known to help families engage in co-reflection and build deeper parental understanding [ 80 , 95 ]. OpenAI has announced they will implement default age-appropriate behavior rules for teen users of ChatGPT and alert parents to children’s behavior [ 72 , 78 ]. These parental control solutions may be effective if they are implemented at the conversation level. Because GenAI Chatbots are stochastic, parents’ moderation and transparency preferences might change according to the specifics of the interaction. This motivates us to elicit parents’ desires at the conversation level. 

## 3 Method 

Our study proceeded in three main stages: (1) we generated simu-lated child–GenAI Chatbot scenarios, (2) we conducted an iterative procedure with a small group of four parents to evaluate and select scenarios, and (3) we conducted semi-structured interviews with 24 parents using the selected scenarios. This study was approved by our university’s IRB. Prior HCI and vignette-based research shows that when experimental tasks are realistic and easy to imagine, participants provide valid, candid feedback that better mirrors real-world behavior [ 1, 55 , 84 , 102 ]. We designed realistic single-turn scenarios for use as technology probes to ground parents’ feedback. 

## 3.1 Scenario Design 

A central design question for our study was whether scenarios should be presented as single-turn exchanges or multi-turn dia-logues. To evaluate this, we conducted an IRB-approved study with parents to assess the feasibility of using each dialogue type in our scenarios. Four of the authors independently invited one eligible parent from their personal networks, confirmed eligibility using the pre-screening survey described in Section 3.4, and collected consent. We excluded close relatives, students, and supervisees to avoid conflicts of interest, undue influence, and biased responses. These parents represented all four developmental age groups de-scribed in Section 3.2. Across participants, two consistent issues emerged: (1) validating multi-turn conversations took parents 3 to 5 times longer than validating single-turn scenarios, and (2) parents surfaced largely similar concerns in both conditions, indicating that multi-turn probes added little analytic value at this stage. Because a central design requirement of our study was to let each parent eval-uate as many distinct scenarios as possible within a fixed interview, multi-turn scenarios consumed more time and constrained how many scenarios each parent could review. We instead prioritized concise single-turn interactions for our data collection. This allowed parents to evaluate a larger and more diverse set of probes, helped maintain parents’ attention, and increased the likelihood that some scenarios would align with their own values and experiences. We note that real child–GenAI Chatbot interactions are often multi-turn. Single-turn probes cannot fully capture how risks evolve across conversations, and some harms emerge only after several turns [ 83 ], including hidden backdoor behaviors that single-turn tests may miss [ 18 ]. In this first stage of work, however, we pri-oritized clarity, interpretability, and participant feasibility, while Understanding Parents’ Desires in Moderating Children’s Interactions with GenAI Chatbots through LLM-Generated Probes CHI ’26, April 13–17, 2026, Barcelona, Spain

## 3.2 Scenario Generation Procedure 

We drew inspiration from prior simulated Child–GenAI Chatbot prompt datasets [ 6 , 48 , 83 ] and created a scenario pool spanning safe and harmful prompts. We decomposed the scenario generation task into specialized subtasks and assigned each to an LLM agent, as parental control is a multi-stakeholder problem involving children, parents, and GenAI Chatbot designers. Decomposition allowed each agent to iteratively represent the interests and constraints of these different stakeholders [13, 49, 73]. The goal of scenario generation was to surface parents’ con-cerns regarding child–GenAI Chatbot interactions, rather than to benchmark or compare model capabilities. Prior work and our pilot interviews indicate that LLM-generated scenarios often exhibit lim-ited realism, whether produced by a frontier model or a lightweight one; for our purposes, the primary requirement was fluent text generation rather than sophisticated reasoning. Accordingly, we se-lected OpenAI’s GPT-4.1-nano for its speed and cost-efficiency. Our design intentionally favored breadth over precision: we generated a large pool of candidate scenarios and then filtered for those that best aligned with our criteria. We queried the model with default API parameters, including temperature (1), max_completion_tokens 

(4096), and top_p (1) [ 71 ]. In practice, GPT-4.1-nano reliably pro-duced coherent, varying-topic child–GenAI Chatbot interactions at scale, and we do not claim it is representative of frontier systems or uniquely suited for this task. Before generation, we created synthetic child personas following prior prompt-simulation methods [ 6, 83 ] and role-based prompting approaches [ 19 ]. Personas varied by context, gender identity, and de-velopmental stage, using Piaget’s four age bands (6–8, 9–12, 13–15, 16–18) [ 12 ]. We intentionally excluded personally identifiable in-formation (e.g., locations, health conditions, school names, family structures [ 106 ]), which limited our ability to simulate privacy-sensitive behaviors such as oversharing. Appendix B provides full demographic specifications and examples. Agent 1 generated a synthetic child prompt by ingesting a ran-domly sampled persona, then, Agent 2 produced the verbatim GenAI Chatbot response to that prompt. These generated each new scenario for which, Agent 3 returned a single-word label (“unique” or “duplicate”) by comparing it against all previously generated scenarios. We supplemented this with cosine similarity analysis using SentenceTransformer embeddings (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) and set a similarity threshold of 0.85, substantially higher than a com-mon starting point of 0.5 [ 22 ]. A scenario was rejected if Agent 3 labeled it a duplicate or if its embedding exceeded the threshold. We iterated in batches of 20 until an entire batch produced no unique scenarios, yielding an initial set of 160 scenarios. The embedding-based cosine similarity provided only a coarse semantic-similarity signal and could not distinguish between sce-narios that shared a topic but raised different parental concerns. As a result, the automatic stage may have over-aggressively removed scenarios that were only slightly distinct. For this study, however, we primarily needed a small set of differentiated probes, and there-fore treated the automatic pipeline as a heuristic filter rather than a fully reliable automatic deduplication system. Future iterations of this pipeline could strengthen the automatic step by allowing Agent 3 to reason before deciding (e.g., chain-of-thought prompt-ing [ 101 ]), by using more capable models, and by incorporating topic-level clustering methods such as BERTopic [ 29 , 38 ] prior to manual review. Because of this limitation, we manually reviewed all automat-ically accepted scenarios. We removed (1) scenarios that shared identical child intent and safety concern but differed only in surface topic (e.g., “How do I build a loudspeaker?” vs. “How do I build a solar oven?”); (2) minor lexical variants that preserved the same conversational structure; and (3) repetitive interaction patterns within an age group (e.g., multiple friendship-advice prompts for teens). We did not remove hallucinated or factually incorrect re-sponses, as these reflect real GenAI Chatbot behavior. After manual curation, we reduced the 160 automatically accepted scenarios to 66 conceptually distinct scenarios. 

## 3.3 Scenario Selection 

To further narrow down our scenario pool, we conducted an IRB-approved iterative procedure with parents (N=4). Four of the au-thors independently invited one parent they knew through their CHI ’26, April 13–17, 2026, Barcelona, Spain Driscoll and Chen, et al. 

personal networks, confirmed their eligibility with a pre-screening survey (described in Section 3.4), and collected their consent. We excluded close relatives, students, and supervisees to avoid conflicts of interest, undue influence, and biased responses that could com-promise informed consent and data integrity. We also excluded the parents who participated in the iterative procedure (Section 3.1). We used this convenience sample of parents only for rapid proto-typing. The ages of these parents’ children were distributed across the four developmental age groups, as described in Section 3.2. All of these parents and their children regularly used GenAI Chatbots. We acknowledge UNICEF’s Guidance on AI for Children, which emphasizes that children’s participation is essential when AI sys-tems are intended for or used by them [ 97 ]. Our use of parent per-spectives at this early-stage does not place them above children’s views; rather, it reflects UNICEF’s requirement that AI systems be rigorously tested to ensure they do not expose children to harmful, misinterpreted, or unsafe content. Because our scenario pool in-cludes potentially sensitive prompts, involving children at this stage would violate these safety principles [ 97 ]. Parent validation, there-fore, serves as an ethically appropriate interim step until scenarios can be adapted for safe, child-facing evaluation. We therefore begin with parent validation, consistent with UNICEF’s safety guidance, and leave direct child participation to future work once appropriate safeguards and IRB protocols are in place. We asked each of the four parents to review all 66 scenarios and rate them on two 7-point Likert scales: (1) how realistic they found the scenario, and (2) how concerned they would be if their child encountered it. We retained scenarios with a median realism rating above 4 (i.e., more realistic than unrealistic) and a variance of concern ratings greater than 1.0 (i.e., scenarios that parents found controversial). This filtering produced 15 scenarios (Fig. 3). We then excluded 3 scenarios for which neither parent expressed concern, leaving a final set of 12 scenarios.

Table 1 presents the final 12 scenarios. For each scenario, we provide a short tagline that captures the prompt’s core context and summarizes the generated response. These scenarios spanned top-ics such as safety, academic habits, and emotional disclosure. The scenarios’ prompts span a range of child intents (e.g., curiosity, mis-chief, support-seeking) and their responses span a range of model behaviors (e.g., refusals, redirections, unintended encouragement). Even though we do not cover the full, unbounded space of possible interactions, for our goal of anchoring parents’ reflections, this sample is “representative” in a qualitative sense: the scenarios span a range of plausible interaction patterns that parents recognized as realistic, and elicited distinct types of concern. 

## 3.4 Participant recruitment 

We recruited parents for interviews through a combination of in-person and online methods. None of the parents who participated in the iterative procedure (Section 3.1) or the early-stage scenario screening (Section 3.3) were recruited for interviews to prevent familiarity bias and ensure that no individual contributed to both prototype refinement and evaluation. For in-person recruitment, we publicized the study through fliers placed in high-traffic ar-eas on and around a university campus, including family housing complexes, childcare centers, and school pick-up zones. We also recruited online through the participant sourcing platform Pro-lific [ 77 ]. In Prolific, we restricted the study to participants who are located in the United States, have at least one child between the ages of 6 and 18, and have an approval rate (the ratio of past approved submissions to rejected submissions) of 95% or higher. We recruited participants on Prolific in batches of four across multiple days to solicit a diverse group. We had all participants (in-person and Prolific) complete a pre-screening survey to confirm their eligi-bility and report their children’s ages and details about their and their children’s GenAI Chatbot usage. Appendix F contains the full pre-screening survey questions. We collected participants’ mini-mal contact information (name and email), given the sensitivity of family/child-related research [ 74 ]. We stopped recruitment when we observed saturation in our analysis (Section 3.6). 

## 3.5 In-depth Interviews 

We conducted IRB-approved semi-structured interviews with par-ents (N=24), using the selected child-GenAI Chatbot interaction sce-narios to investigate the factors behind their concerns, their desired moderation-related interactions, and their desired transparency-related interactions. Prior to scenario review, we asked parents a short set of back-ground questions about their children (e.g. age, personality, GenAI Chatbot use) and whether they currently monitored their child’s GenAI Chatbot use. Parents then reviewed and signed a consent form. All participants reviewed the same set of 12 scenarios in random order and completed as many as possible within the allotted 45-minute session, with the option to skip scenarios at any time. To facilitate comprehension, scenarios were presented as static mock-ups styled to resemble ChatGPT conversations, displaying both the child’s prompt and the GenAI Chatbot response. For each scenario, parents were asked about: (1) Concern. Whether they were concerned about the interaction, rated on a 5-point Likert scale (Not concerned at all, Somewhat unconcerned, Neutral, Somewhat concerned, Concerned). ## 3.6 Data Collection and Analysis 

All interview sessions were recorded with participant consent and subsequently transcribed. One researcher led the interview, whereas the other researcher took notes. We iteratively conducted the in-terviews and analyzed the data concurrently. We conducted in-terviews in rounds of four participants to approximate coverage across the four developmental age groups described in Section 3.2. After each round, the four researchers independently coded one of the four transcripts using Saldaña’s grounded theory–informed framework [ 87 ]. We began creating codebooks from scratch, one for each research question, after the first round of interviews and updated them iteratively with each successive round. We briefly summarize the coding stages we employed: Initial Coding involved examining the transcript line by line to break down the qualitative data, compare segments for similarities and differences, and gen-erate descriptive codes. Focused Coding then identified the most frequent or analytically significant initial codes to develop the most salient categories in the data corpus. Axial Coding explored the rela-tionships among these categories, articulating their properties and dimensions and integrating them into higher-level themes. After each round of independent coding, the team met to compare codes, discuss discrepancies, and reach consensus. Across six rounds, this process— independent coding, collaborative reconciliation, and iter-ative refinement— continued until we observed data saturation [ 89 ], meaning no new themes appeared in the fifth and sixth rounds. Each code we developed during Initial Coding is a type of factor that triggered parents’ concern for RQ1, a type of moderation-related interaction for RQ2, or a type of transparency-related in-teraction for RQ3. The themes we developed during Focused Cod-ing are: concern factors organized by origin for RQ1; moderation-related interactions for RQ2; and transparency-related interactions organized by components for RQ3. We present our initial, focused, and axial codebooks for each research question in Appendix I. We explain the details we focused on in Initial Coding and the dimen-sions we explored in Focused and Axial Coding in each correspond-ing results section. ## 4.1 RQ1: What are the factors that trigger parents’ concern in child GenAI Chatbot interaction? 

We asked parents to rate how concerned they were for each sce-nario and, when they felt concern, to explain why (Section 3.5). We coded these explanations into initial codes for specific con-cern triggers, focused codes for recurring themes, and axial codes that captured where concerns originated in the interaction: in the GenAI Chatbot’s responses or in the child’s prompts (Section 3.6). Table 3 summarizes these themes, Figure 5 shows how they distrib-ute across parents and age groups, and Figure 6 visualizes which factors parents identified per scenario. Following qualitative re-porting conventions, we describe the relative frequency of themes (Section 3.6). Across interviews, parents described how concerns regarding how a child would interpret a GenAI Chatbot response, what the exchange might make their child feel, and what the child might do after the interaction. Some parents pointed to specific content in a response, such as violent or sexual material, confirming worries about response content documented in prior work [ 106 ]. However, most parents’ feedback took a step back and discussed how a factor in a prompt or response evoked concern at the con-versation level. These themes are summarized in Table 3, and we visualize the distribution of initial codes per parent in Figure 5. 

4.1.1 Questionable response from the GenAI Chatbot. Many par-ents identified properties of the GenAI Chatbot’s response as a central source of concern. Parents feedback on the responses them-selves focused on (1) how replies would shape children’s feelings and possible future actions, (2) whether the GenAI Chatbot under-stands the child’s underlying intent, and (3) whether children can realistically use the response. 

Responses shape how children feel and what they may do later. Many parents worried that a response’s tone, content, and values could leave a lasting emotional impact, even when children did not fully understand the words. One parent noted that their child "would walk away not feeling good, even if he didn’t fully understand, because there are things in here that are going to sit with you, whether you fully understand them or not" (P19, S11). A few parents also pointed out that responses quietly expand the set of actions a child might consider. For example, a few parents were concerned that a suggestion to "try it at home" (S1), planted an idea that a child would not have otherwise considered. These concerns highlight that, even when the topics in a response aren’t concerning, parents treat idea seeding or an emotionally upsetting tone as issues. These concern factors seem to be examples of an "empathy gap" which previous work hypothesized GenAI Chatbots may face when responding to children [51]. 

Responses may miss what the child really means. A ma-jority of parents were concerned when the GenAI Chatbot treated a prompt too literally and responded without first investigating why the child was asking. They argued that it was inappropriate for the GenAI Chatbot to give guidance or refuse without consider-ing whether the request might have multiple interpretations. For example, in the keyless-entry scenario (S4), one parent explained that there was "more than one way to look at it" and that, when the GenAI Chatbot refused to help without context, the system "seems that it’s broken" (P24, S4). A few parents similarly worried when a response ignored a interpretation that the child’s prompt could signal an unhealthy mindset. In the Error-free-perfectionism scenario (S3), these parents expected the GenAI Chatbot to consider all possibilities and felt that any response to this scenario could be inappropriate as it lacked necessary information to make an informed response. 

Some responses are unusable to children and therefore feel unsafe. Many parents also judged responses to be unsafe when children were unlikely to understand them, even if the information was accurate. They pointed to explanations that were cluttered, indirectly phrased, or explained at an adult level, for example, with advanced vocabulary or abstract concepts that younger children could not grasp. One parent observed that children "probably don’t really have a very contextual understanding of the word love" (P19, S9). Parents felt that if a child cannot extract the main warning or takeaway, then the response was concerning because it failed to answer in a meaningful way. These concerns mirror themes identified in broader work on developmental appropriateness in media and mediation for conventional technologies [54, 67]. ## 4.2 RQ2: How do parents want to moderate their children’s interactions with an GenAI Chatbot? 

We asked parents how they would want a GenAI Chatbot to respond in each scenario and what changes they would make to the model’s replies. We coded these responses into initial moderation opera-tions (e.g., “explain problems with the child’s prompt,” “refuse and explain,” “defer to parent”), grouped them into focused themes, and summarized them in Table 4. In line with our qualitative approach, we report exact counts in the table and use qualitative frequency terms (e.g., “many,” “most”) in the text. Here, we focus on how parents describe moderation as a set of conversational operations that a GenAI Chatbot should perform, and how these operations extend existing notions of parental mediation from a system-level strategy to a conversation-level partner. Parents’ ideal interactions considered both the action they wanted the system to take and which part of the interaction they wanted changed. Some parents echoed established parental mediation strategies, including active safety mediation, restrictive content mediation, restrictive technical mediation, and monitoring [67, 68, 93]. 

GenAI Chatbots should explain moderation choices. Most parents wanted the GenAI Chatbot to treat moderation as an op-portunity to help children understand why a request may be prob-lematic, rather than simply refusing. They described that ideal responses would reframe the child’s question and highlight the un-derlying risk. These correction-oriented operations mirror active mediation in which parents talk with children about online risks rather than only restricting ac-cess [ 93 , 103 ]. Developmental fit determines safety and usability. Many parents emphasized that moderated responses must remain develop-mentally appropriate so that children can actually use the warning. Prior work on child-facing AI and media highlights age-appropriate communication as a core design requirement [ 25 , 67 , 97 ], often framed in terms of effectiveness or engagement. Parents in our study went further and treated developmental mismatch as a safety problem. In the forgotten-stove scenario, one parent suggested re-placing "asphyxiation" with simpler language because "that word is too big" for children (P2, S8). Another parent worried that “the concepts and themes for this might be a little complex for a ten-year-old” (P15, S11). Parents argued that if a child cannot understand the vocabulary, the structure, or the story’s moral implications, they cannot internalize the warning, even if the content is factually correct. 

Moderation should respond to children’s intentions and emotions, and clarify that the GenAI Chatbot is not a person. 

Many parents wanted moderation to engage with the child’s under-lying intentions and emotional state. They wanted a GenAI Chatbot to ask why the child wanted to do something, validate their feelings, and then steer them toward safer options. One parent suggested that the GenAI Chatbot should “probe a little bit further like, ‘Tell me, what in particular are you struggling with?’” (P21, S3). Parents also worried about the emotional tone of a response. One parent ex-plained that the phrase “but I have to be clear” made them angry at the GenAI Chatbot even as an adult (P19, S1), and feared that similar wording could make children feel scolded rather than supported. Existing work on algorithmic parenting and parental control tools notes that many current systems prioritize surveillance and control over developmental or emotional support [ 39 , 55 , 94 ]. Some parents explicitly wanted the GenAI Chatbot to remind children that it is not human, for example, “I want to make sure they understand it is a computer and not a human” (P3, S5). Guidance for child-facing AI warns that children may misinterpret AI systems as social partners if their nature is not made clear [ 25 , 97 ]. Our findings extend these concerns to the level of individual conversations: parents expect each response to acknowledge the child’s intentions and emotions and to explicitly clarify that a GenAI Chatbot is non-human. 

Parents expect strong boundaries that avoid seeding new ideas. Parents described a different set of moderation expectations when the concerning factor came from the child’s prompt rather than from the GenAI Chatbot’s response. When prompts involved hurting others, humiliating peers, or bypassing rules, many parents wanted the system to set clear limits and avoid normalizing the behavior. In the harsh school-roast scenario, one parent suggested CHI ’26, April 13–17, 2026, Barcelona, Spain Driscoll and Chen, et al. 

the system should say, "It is not good to be mean to your friends" and refuse to help (P18, S12). In risky-tool scenarios, another par-ent said they would "remove [mentions of any child use of fire]" altogether, because even supervised fire use felt too hazardous for a model to suggest (P6, S7). Parents also objected when the model offered workarounds that undercut rules, such as suggesting the child "wait until you get home" to bypass a school firewall (S1), or introduced new tactics the child had not requested: "I don’t want to give them any ideas . . . because they can act on it and put them-selves in a bad position" (P20, S7). Classic mediation work describes restrictive content and technical mediation, where parents limit access to certain material or functionalities [ 42 , 50 , 93 ]. Regulatory reports and audits show that such safeguards and age-assurance tools are often incomplete and can be bypassed [ 46 , 70 ], and recent investigations of GenAI Chatbot platforms have documented that chatbots can still generate detailed guidance for harmful behavior despite formal safeguards [ 17 , 69 , 94 ]. Our results add a complemen-tary concern: because a GenAI Chatbot can invent suggestions and examples on the fly, parents see moderation as needing to control idea generation inside the conversation by refusing, pruning, or simplifying responses in ways that do not expand the child’s option set. 

Moderation should hand certain issues back to trusted humans. Many parents wanted the GenAI Chatbot to hand mod-eration responsibility back to human support. Parents felt that any moderation that didn’t involve them was insufficient for scenarios involving violence, self-harm, or intense emotions. Parents asked the system to encourage children to talk to "a parent or trusted older sibling" (P19, S2), and, recognizing that some children may hesitate to approach parents, to offer additional options such as helplines or school counselors (P18, S11). Guidance for child-facing AI already emphasizes that systems should acknowledge their lim-its and direct users to human help in high-stakes situations [ 25 , 97 ], and proposals for algorithmic parenting imagine systems that de-tect when a child’s well-being might be compromised and support parents in responding [ 39 ]. Our findings extend this work to the conversation level and identify that parents expect moderation to withhold unsafe content and, more importantly, recognize when a situation is too serious for an GenAI Chatbot to handle alone and to defer to human support. 

Parents’ moderation strategies vary by children’s age and scenarios. Parents drew on these desired moderation-related inter-actions in different combinations depending on their child and the situation. Figures 7 and 8 illustrate how no single ideal interaction dominated across all parents or scenarios. For younger children, parents described diverse blends of strategies. For example, some emphasized tailoring by age and clarifying intent, while others focused more on risk awareness or deference to family guidance. In contrast, among adolescents, parents converged more often on operations such as “explain problems with the prompt,” “emphasize risk awareness,” and “clarify child’s intent,” reflecting expectations that teenagers can engage in explanation and introspection. One parent noted for their teenager, "Maybe that sounds very like a helicopter mom. But I think that there are reasons why there are helicopter parents" (P4, S5-Age-faking online). Helicopter parent-ing is characterized by overprotection and control [ 39 , 99 ], which may increase anxiety and depression in children [ 98 ]. At the sce-nario level, even when parents shared the same leading concern factor (e.g., “Child could have questionable intentions” in several scenarios), they proposed different moderation combinations, such as explaining the problem, deferring to parents, or redirecting with alternatives. In a few cases, the most frequent concern and preferred moderation operation aligned closely. For example, scenarios where “Doesn’t clarify child’s intent” co-occurred with requests to “clarify child’s intent”. Prior work shows that parents already mix mediation styles for conventional technologies [ 41 , 67 , 93 , 103 ] rather than relying on a single approach. Our analysis finds this pattern in GenAI Chatbot moderation and specifies the conversation-level operations that parents want the system to perform. Rather than mapping each concern to a single prescribed action, our findings motivate a configurable, matrix-style framework: concerns identified in RQ1 (e.g., exposure to unsafe ideas, potentially harmful intentions) map to different combinations of moderation operations (e.g., explain, refuse, tailor by age, defer to support), which parents mentioned they assemble based on who their child is and what the conversation is about. 

## 4.3 RQ3: What are parents’ transparency preferences around their child’s interactions with a GenAI Chatbot? 

We asked parents what kinds of information they wanted to know and how they would want to be made aware in different scenar-ios. Parents emphasized a need for transparency, in part because they lack direct insight into how GenAI Chatbots interact with their children [ 106 ]. We coded these responses into transparency-related interactions and grouped them into focused themes and axial themes and display them in Table 5. Transcripts and summaries complement alerts. A majority of parents also wanted the option to review full transcripts after the fact, especially for younger children. Parents tailor transparency to the child, not the scenario. 

Parents described their desired transparency-related interactions in ways that reflected their child’s age and personality more than any specific scenario content. Parents prioritize safety over privacy, but recognize a trade-off. When describing desired transparency, a few parents explicitly raised children’s privacy as a concern, but many reflected on a tradeoff described in prior work between parental oversight and children’s independence [ 39 , 75 ]. Some parents envisioned scaling back detailed monitoring as children aged, so that older children could use a GenAI Chatbot to seek help in sensitive situations without fearing constant parental review. Others worried that con-tinuous access could feel intrusive but still judged it necessary to prevent harm. Some parents explicitly said that safety should over-ride privacy, as one parent explained: “I wouldn’t want my kids to think that I’m trying to restrict them from learning. But at the same time, I want to protect them from doing something that’s going to harm them” (P20, S8). These parents were cognizant of the possibility that their monitoring could impact their children’s privacy, but felt that a need for ensuring security outweighed that impact. 

## 5 Discussion 5.1 GenAI Chatbot Parental Control Tools as an Alternative to "All or Nothing" 

We believe that adequate parental controls can shift parents ban-oriented mindsets toward trust-based oversight. In our interviews, mistrust led some parents to favor banning children’s access al-together—viewing an “all-or-nothing” ban as the safest way to eliminate perceived risks. However, such bans may disadvantage Understanding Parents’ Desires in Moderating Children’s Interactions with GenAI Chatbots through LLM-Generated Probes CHI ’26, April 13–17, 2026, Barcelona, Spain 

children by limiting their opportunities to learn from GenAI or develop AI literacy. Because parents’ mistrust often stems from limited visibility and a lack of control [ 106 ], parents’ desired moderation and trans-parency strategies could give parents the insight they need to build trust. Prior discussion suggests that algorithmic parenting could safeguard children from harm without “limiting their freedom of speech and access to information” [ 39 , p.2]. Our findings on par-ents’ desired moderation related interactions (Section 4.2) often embody algorithmic parenting approaches. The fine-grained mod-eration tools that parents discuss in our interviews (e.g., calibrated topic/time limits, response reframing, and escalation to a trusted adult or resource) could provide actionable ways for parents to adjust interactions as they see fit and, in turn, build trust. Em-powering parents with the fine-grained transparency tools they discuss (e.g., usage summaries, flagged-turn review, brief explana-tions of how a GenAI Chatbot interpreted a child’s prompt) could make their children’s use visible and demystify it. Incorporating these strategies into tools for parents, developers can shift parental decision-making toward trust-based oversight. This would realize a goal of algorithmic parenting, preserving children’s uninfringed access to vital AI tools. 

## 5.2 GenAI Chatbot Parental Controls Need Personalization 

Personalization of GenAI Chatbot parental controls by adapting parent’s proposed mediation strategies, particularly, for example, incorporating children’s ages, could help address the nuanced, in-dividualized concerns parents hold across children’s ages, contexts, and family values. Personalization for GenAI Chatbots has been advocated by prior work [ 25 , 97 , 106 ] because an adaptable parental control interface may ease the burden on parents. They posit that personalization could redistribute some of the responsibility of mon-itoring children’s interactions to the GenAI Chatbot in ways chosen by the family. This flexibility could enhance parents’ ability to guide their children while also creating opportunities for more nuanced safeguards. OpenAI has applied default age-appropriate behavior rules for teen users of ChatGPT [ 72 ], which emphasizes the im-portance of age-appropriate responses and alignment with family values. However, these lack personalization. We recommend that de-velopers base personalization on (1) children’s ages, which inform parents’ moderation desires, and (2) parents’ desired moderation related interactions per scenario. We note that personalized modera-tion may be more impactful than personalized transparency because moderation related interactions varied per scenario whereas trans-parency desires largely did not. These provide an actionable way to address the need for personalizing parental controls stated in prior work. This could be done by (1) collecting and implementing chil-dren’s ages and parents’ mediation preferences during onboarding for a GenAI Chatbot platform, (2) defining fine-grained choices for parents to decide how a GenAI Chatbot informs them of their chil-dren’s activity, and (3) applying these decisions at the conversation level. 

## 5.3 How GenAI Chatbots Should Be Age Aware 

Many recent conversations surrounding GenAI Chatbots for chil-dren have emphasized that they be tailored to children’s ages and developmental needs [ 25 , 97 ]. This could be done by prepopulating a GenAI Chatbot conversation with contextual details about a child and their family’s needs, including, for example, a child’s develop-mental stage, age, auditable details recorded from interactions, or information from an account profile for parents in all of their con-versations. To accomplish this, designers could build on prior work that has improved GenAI Chatbots’ cultural and social awareness through prompting and fine-tuning [ 79 ]. Moreover, we found dif-ferences among parents of broad developmental groups; therefore, to protect children’s privacy, a GenAI Chatbot could incorporate general developmental descriptors rather than precise birth dates. This information could be carried through the conversation state, accompanied by an audit trail of value updates, and shown in the interface for easy correction by parents. With this context in place, a GenAI Chatbot could tailor responses to the parental desires we identified by adjusting content, tone, reading level, and depth of ex-planation, and by calibrating transparent parental involvement and access. This could create alignment with children’s developmental stages, balance children’s autonomy with parental oversight, and reduce misalignment with household values. 

## 5.4 Balancing Parenting Needs with Children’s Privacy 

We believe that parental control solutions for GenAI Chatbots can balance children’s privacy with parental authorities’ desires for full access and monitoring by implementing collaborative governance strategies. A common theme in parents’ desired moderation strate-gies was that parents disregard children’s privacy in favor of broad access to content. Although increased transparency and tighter moderation can make parents feel less concerned, this may erode children’s trust by "excessively infringing their privacy" [ 39 ]. We think that parental control solutions should provide children with privacy as the default and require parents to declare purpose-bound and time-limited transparency. Parents could be assisted in creating this mutually beneficial solution by tools that scaffold known "ne-gotiation practices of family members as they advocate for device and creative control" [ 105 , p. 1]. Parents and children could input an explicit, revisable understanding of who sees what, when, why, and for how long to a GenAI Chatbot platform. Developers could employ a GenAI Chatbot to engage in parental collaboration or enable collective governance by mediating negotiations between children and parents [ 7, 45 ]. An example mediated solution that could balance this tension would involve a GenAI Chatbot alerting a parent to concerning behavior and prompting a brief check-in with their child. Transparency could be increased if necessary, with any changes communicated to the child, and could return to the minimum when concerns are resolved. Balancing parental needs with children’s privacy using collective governance could preserve children’s privacy and trust, foster children’s independence, and allow parents to ensure children’s safety. ## 6 Limitations 6.1 Absence of Children’s Perspectives 

This study examined parents’ perspectives as the primary users of parental controls. However, it is children who receive the impacts of parental controls, and their voices are critical for understanding the impacts of design choices in practice [ 30 ]. We did not solicit children’s views on model refusals, explanations, or directives to consult a parent, nor did we explore how children might respond to their parents’ transparency preferences. 

## 6.2 Small Representative Samples and Potential Priming 

Our findings draw from in-depth interviews with 24 parents, a sam-ple that suggested saturation during qualitative analysis. However, due to the nature of theoretical saturation, it is possible that there are diverse ideas we did not capture with our samples. The parents we interviewed represented children from early childhood through adolescence and varied in their supervision practices. We became empirically confident that our findings were saturated when similar instances were repeated. The frequencies that we observed may not be representative of the larger population, as we did not sample adequately for statistical inference. Additionally, we asked parents to review simulated prompts and corresponding GenAI Chatbot responses to alleviate a lack of un-derstanding surrounding children’s GenAI Chatbot use. Because parents articulated concerns and proposed modifications while re-viewing model outputs, the example contexts may have oriented their attention toward specific concerns, moderation-related in-teractions, and transparency-related interactions. In everyday use, these issues may feel less salient, and parents may seek different types of interactions or feel differently about their importance. 

## 6.3 Children Could Potentially Bypass Controls 

A longstanding challenge in parental control systems is that chil-dren can often find ways to bypass imposed restrictions. Recent work shows that children as young as six can outperform GenAI Chatbots at generating loopholes [ 65 ], and prior research finds that parental controls can backfire by undermining children’s au-tonomy [ 37 ]. In light of this, the moderation and transparency strategies surfaced in our study should be understood as similarly vulnerable: even if they are better aligned with parents’ expec-tations, children may still learn to circumvent them in practice, and our work does not evaluate the robustness of these strategies against such attempts. 

## 7 Future Work 

Future work should develop interpretable models that can detect moderation-relevant factors in children’s conversations with GenAI Chatbots (such as intent clarity, risk level, developmental cues, and when handover is needed). To support this, researchers should cre-ate validated surveys and scenario-based tools that measure these factors at scale. For example, these tools could ask families which child behaviors they want a GenAI Chatbot to notify them about and how. Future work should also engage children to ensure that in-teraction designs are supportive, developmentally appropriate, and reflective of children’s interests. It may be valuable to examine how children themselves perceive transparency mechanisms, such as ongoing monitoring or alerts about flagged activity, and how differ-ent configurations shape their sense of autonomy, their willingness to disclose concerns, and the broader dynamics of parent–child relationships and technology use. 

## 8 Conclusion 

In this paper, we conducted interviews with N=24 parents to un-derstand their concerns and desires for GenAI Chatbot parental controls. Our study systematically characterized the factors that trigger parental concerns into those relating to the child’s prompt and the GenAI Chatbot’s response. We also identify parents’ de-sired moderation-related and transparency-related interactions to address these concern factors. Many parents emphasize the need for controls at the conversation level rather than broad access or content restrictions. We observed a range of parental control pref-erences, highlighting the need for personalized tools that reflect individual family values.

## A Prompt Engineering Techniques Used and Prompts Used 

We prompt GPT-4.1-nano as follows, drawing on Sahoo et. al. 2024 for appropriate prompt engineering approaches [ 86 ]: Agent 1 is prompted in a few-shot approach [ 16 ] to produce a psychological narrative explaining the child’s motivation, that evokes abstract reasoning inspired by Zheng et. al. 2024 [ 108 ], and a specific query they would pose to an AI assistant. This prompt is scaffolded as a CRAFT (Context, Role, Action, Format, Target Audience) meta-prompt [ 104 ]. Agent 2 responds to an unengineered prompt output from Agent 1. Agent 3 is prompted in a zero-shot approach [ 82 ]to determine if what Agent 1 generated is substantially different from other generated prompts. We supplement Agent 2 with co-sine similarity analysis using SentenceTransformer embeddings (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) with a threshold of 0.85. Initially, we included a fourth agent, a bystander commenting on a child’s actions to emulate a parent’s judgment, but found it added limited value.

## B Child Personas for Scenario Generation B.1 Demographic Details 

We generate a bank of demographic profiles by enumerating com-binations of characteristics that are effective at diversifying LLM output. We condition the first agent on a randomly selected demo-graphic profile. Each profile includes: (1) one group of polarizing adjectives identified through activation engineering by Albert et. al. 2024 [ 2], (2) a gender (male, female, non-binary), which alters tone and content as shown by Wan et. al. 2023 [ 100 ], (3) ethnicity to uti-lize the model’s racial bias demonstrated in Torres et. al. 2024 [ 96 ], (4) economic status encoded by high and low income locations as in Singh et. al. 2024 [ 92 ], and (5) age group. See Table 6 for a list of traits and example demographic profile. We do not provide the child persona with pseudo-sensitive personal information or intricate backgrounds, as that level of detail may hinder this type of stochastic brainstorming. Thus, these scenarios cannot encompass some potential real-life child behaviors, like oversharing personal information. The complete factorial design generates 4 (PCA categories) x 11 (regions) x 3 (genders) x 4 (socioeconomic classes) x 4 (age groups) = 2,112 unique persona combinations

Gas Leak: If you have a gas stove and the flame goes out but the gas is still on, it could fill the area with gas, potentially causing asphyxiation, especially if the room is not well-ventilated. Quite dangerously, if a source of ignition is introduced, it could lead to an explosion. 3. Wasted Energy: Even if a fire or gas leak doesn’t occur, leaving a stove on wastes energy and could result in higher utility bills. 4. Damage to Cookware: Pots or pans left on a hot stove could be damaged, especially if they’re left empty and overheat. 5. Food spoilage: If food is left on the stove, it can get overcooked or burn, creating unpleasant smells and possibly becoming a health hazard if someone tries to eat it. To avoid all these issues, always ensure you’ve turned off your stove after you’ve finished cooking. Some people find it helpful to establish a routine of double-checking their stove is off before they leave the kitchen or their home. Smart devices or stove alarms might be useful for people who often forget to turn off their stove.

## E Rating Session Questions Asked 

To evaluate realism, we asked: “How realistic, if at all, is this sce-nario for a child in your child’s age group?” Participants responded using the following scale: 1 = Very Unrealistic, 2 = Unrealistic, 3 = Somewhat Unrealistic, 4 = Neutral, 5 = Somewhat Realistic, 6 = Realistic, 7 = Very Realistic To evaluate concern, we asked: “How concerning, if at all, is this scenario for a child in your child’s age group? 1 = Very Concerning, 2 = Concerning, 3 = Somewhat Concerning, 4 = Neutral, 5 = Some-what Unconcerning, 6 = Unconcerning, 7 = Very Unconcerning We intentionally structured the concern scale so that increas-ing values reflected decreasing concern. This design emerged from early pilot feedback, where parents expressed that aligning the scale from negative (very concerning) to positive (very unconcerning) made the rating task more intuitive. Aligning both scales to fol-low a low-to-high (negative-to-positive) trajectory reduced friction in responding and supported more intuitive comparisons across dimensions.
